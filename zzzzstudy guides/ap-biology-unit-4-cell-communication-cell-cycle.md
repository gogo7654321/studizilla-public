### LIGAND-RECEPTOR BINDING

**Ligand**: Signaling molecule (hormone, neurotransmitter, etc.)
**Receptor**: Protein that recognizes specific ligand

**Properties:**
- Highly specific (lock and key)
- Reversible binding
- Induces conformational change
- Triggers cellular response

### RECEPTOR LOCATION DEPENDS ON LIGAND

| Ligand Type | Properties | Receptor Location |
|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| **Hydrophilic** | Cannot cross membrane | Plasma membrane |

- Peptide hormones (insulin, glucagon)

- Steroid hormones (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol)

### MEMBRANE RECEPTORS

**Three main types:**

**1. G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs):**
- Largest family of receptors
- 7 transmembrane domains
- Activate G proteins when ligand binds
- Examples: Epinephrine, many neurotransmitters

**2. Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs):**
- Dimerize when ligand binds
- Autophosphorylate (add phosphate to themselves)
- Activate multiple pathways
- Examples: Insulin, growth factors

**3. Ion Channel Receptors (Ligand-gated):**
- Open/close in response to ligand
- Allow ions to flow
- Very fast response
- Examples: Acetylcholine at neuromuscular junction

### INTRACELLULAR RECEPTORS

**For hydrophobic signals:**
- Located in cytoplasm or nucleus
- Ligand diffuses through membrane
- Hormone-receptor complex acts as transcription factor
- Directly affects gene expression

**Example: Steroid hormones**
1. Steroid crosses membrane
2. Binds receptor in cytoplasm
3. Complex enters nucleus
4. Binds DNA, activates genes
5. mRNA â†’ protein

**Response is slow but long-lasting** (changes gene expression)

## ğŸ”¬ MITOSIS

### OVERVIEW

**Mitosis**: Division of nucleus into two identical nuclei

**Purpose:**
- Growth
- Repair/replacement
- Asexual reproduction

**Result:** 2 genetically identical daughter cells

### MITOSIS PHASES

**PMAT mnemonic:** Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase

### PROPHASE

**"First stage"**

**Events:**
- Chromatin condenses into chromosomes
- Nucleolus disappears
- Nuclear envelope breaks down
- Centrosomes move to opposite poles
- Mitotic spindle begins forming

**Key features:**
- Chromosomes visible (each = 2 sister chromatids)
- Kinetochores form at centromeres

### PROMETAPHASE

**"Before metaphase" (sometimes grouped with prophase)**

**Events:**
- Nuclear envelope completely gone
- Spindle microtubules attach to kinetochores
- Chromosomes begin moving toward center

### METAPHASE

**"Middle stage"**

**Events:**
- Chromosomes align at metaphase plate (cell equator)
- Each chromosome attached to spindles from both poles
- Maximum condensation

**Metaphase checkpoint:**
- Are all chromosomes properly attached?
- If not â†’ pause until fixed

### ANAPHASE

**"Away/apart stage"**

**Events:**
- Sister chromatids separate (now called chromosomes)
- Chromosomes pulled to opposite poles
- Motor proteins "walk" along spindle microtubules
- Cell elongates

**Two processes:**
- **Anaphase A**: Kinetochore microtubules shorten, pull chromosomes
- **Anaphase B**: Non-kinetochore microtubules lengthen, push poles apart

### TELOPHASE

**"End stage"**

**Events:**
- Chromosomes arrive at poles
- Nuclear envelopes reform
- Chromosomes decondense
- Nucleoli reappear
- Spindle disappears

**Result:** Two nuclei with identical chromosomes

## ğŸ§¬ MEIOSIS

### OVERVIEW

**Meiosis**: Cell division that produces gametes (sex cells)

**Key differences from mitosis:**

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Divisions** | 1 | 2 |
| **Daughter cells** | 2 | 4 |
| **Chromosome number** | Same (2n â†’ 2n) | Halved (2n â†’ n) |
| **Genetic diversity** | Identical to parent | Unique combinations |
| **Purpose** | Growth, repair | Sexual reproduction |

### MEIOSIS I vs MEIOSIS II

**Meiosis I**: Reduction division (separates homologs)
**Meiosis II**: Similar to mitosis (separates sister chromatids)

### MEIOSIS I STAGES

**Prophase I:**
- Chromosomes condense
- Homologous pairs find each other (synapsis)
- Tetrad/bivalent forms (4 chromatids together)
- **Crossing over occurs!**
- Nuclear envelope breaks down

**Crossing over:**
- Non-sister chromatids exchange segments
- Creates recombinant chromosomes
- **Major source of genetic diversity!**

**Metaphase I:**
- Tetrads align at metaphase plate
- Homologs pair up (not individual chromosomes)
- **Independent assortment:** Random orientation

**Anaphase I:**
- Homologous chromosomes separate
- Sister chromatids stay together
- One of each homolog to each pole

**Telophase I / Cytokinesis I:**
- Two cells form
- Each has half the chromosomes (n)
- Each chromosome still has 2 chromatids

### MEIOSIS II STAGES

**Similar to mitosis!**

**Prophase II:**
- Chromosomes condense (if decondensed)
- Spindle forms

**Metaphase II:**
- Chromosomes align at metaphase plate
- Individual chromosomes (not pairs)

**Anaphase II:**
- Sister chromatids separate
- Move to opposite poles

**Telophase II / Cytokinesis II:**
- Four haploid cells form
- Each cell has n chromosomes
- Each chromosome has 1 chromatid

### GENETIC DIVERSITY IN MEIOSIS

**Three sources:**

**1. Crossing over (Prophase I):**
- Creates new combinations of alleles
- Recombinant chromosomes

**2. Independent assortment (Metaphase I):**
- Random orientation of homologous pairs
- $2^n$ possible combinations
- Humans: $2^{23}$ = 8.4 million combinations!

**3. Random fertilization:**
- Any sperm can fertilize any egg

**Properties of cancer cells:**

- Oncogene = accelerator stuck ON

**Examples:**

  - Mutant Ras always active
  - Cell divides constantly
  - Found in ~30% of cancers

- **Myc**: Transcription factor
  - Mutant Myc overexpressed
  - Promotes cell cycle genes

**Tumor suppressor**: Gene that slows cell division or promotes apoptosis
- When mutated â†’ lose brakes on cell cycle

**Examples:**

- Checks for DNA damage
- Pauses cell cycle for repair
- Triggers apoptosis if irreparable

- DNA repair genes
- Mutations increase breast/ovarian cancer risk

### TWO-HIT HYPOTHESIS

**For tumor suppressor genes:**
- Need to lose BOTH copies for cancer
- First hit: One allele mutated (inherited or acquired)
- Second hit: Other allele mutated
- Now no functional tumor suppressor

**For oncogenes:**
- Only need ONE copy mutated
- Dominant effect

### MULTI-STEP MODEL OF CANCER

**Cancer requires multiple mutations:**

1. First mutation: Cell divides more
2. Second mutation: Ignores growth inhibition
3. Third mutation: Avoids apoptosis
4. Fourth mutation: Unlimited divisions
5. Fifth mutation: Angiogenesis
6. Sixth mutation: Metastasis

**Takes years/decades to accumulate mutations**
