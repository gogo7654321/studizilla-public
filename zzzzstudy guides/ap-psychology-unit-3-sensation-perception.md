## üî¨ Thresholds

### Absolute Threshold

The **minimum** stimulation needed to detect a stimulus **50%** of the time.

| Sense | Absolute Threshold Example |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Vision | Candle flame 30 miles away (clear night) |
| Hearing | Ticking watch 20 feet away (quiet) |
| Smell | Drop of perfume in 3-room apartment |
| Taste | Teaspoon of sugar in 2 gallons water |
| Touch | Wing of bee on cheek from 1 cm |

### Difference Threshold (JND)

The **minimum difference** between two stimuli that can be detected **50%** of the time.

Also called **Just Noticeable Difference (JND)**

---

## üìê Weber's Law

### The Formula

$$\frac{\Delta I}{I} = k$$

Where:
- $\Delta I$ = Change in intensity
- $I$ = Original intensity
- $k$ = Constant (varies by sense)

### What It Means

The JND is a **constant proportion** of the original stimulus.

| Sense | Weber's Constant (k) |
|-------|---------------------|
| Weight | ~2% (lift 50 lbs, notice 1 lb change) |
| Light | ~8% |
| Sound | ~5% |

### Example

- Original weight: 100 lbs ‚Üí JND = 2 lbs
- Original weight: 50 lbs ‚Üí JND = 1 lb

**Heavier starting weight = larger JND needed**

## üìâ Sensory Adaptation

### Getting Used to Stimuli

| Definition | Reduced sensitivity after constant stimulation |
|------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Purpose | Focus on changes in environment |
| Example | Not feeling your watch after a while |

### Examples

| Sense | Adaptation Example |
|-------|-------------------|
| Smell | Stop smelling your own perfume |
| Touch | Don't feel clothes on skin |
| Vision | Eyes adjust to dark room |
| Taste | Food tastes less intense over time |

### Why It Matters

- Helps us detect **changes** (what's important!)
- Constant stimuli become background "noise"

## üîä Signal Detection Theory

### Beyond Simple Thresholds

Detection depends on:
- **Signal strength**
- **Background noise**
- **Psychological factors** (expectations, motivation)

### Four Outcomes

|  | Signal Present | Signal Absent |
|--|----------------|---------------|
| **"Yes"** | ‚úÖ Hit | ‚ùå False Alarm |
| **"No"** | ‚ùå Miss | ‚úÖ Correct Rejection |

### Example: Radiologist Reading X-rays

- Hit: Detects cancer that exists
- Miss: Fails to detect cancer
- False Alarm: Says cancer when none exists
- Correct Rejection: Correctly says no cancer

## üåà Color Vision

### Two Major Theories

### 1. Trichromatic Theory (Young-Helmholtz)

| Concept | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Three cone types | Red, Green, Blue |
| Color mixing | Combinations create all colors |
| Location | Works in retina |

### 2. Opponent-Process Theory (Hering)

| Concept | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Three opponent pairs | Red-Green, Blue-Yellow, Black-White |
| Explains | Afterimages |
| Location | Works in neural pathways |

### Which Is Right?

**BOTH!** They work at different stages of processing.

- Trichromatic: At retina (cones)
- Opponent-process: At ganglion cells and beyond

According to Weber's Law, which would be more noticeable?

(A) Adding 1 oz to a 10 oz weight
(B) Adding 1 oz to a 100 oz weight

## ‚úçÔ∏è Sample Free Response

**Question**: John is at a loud party and trying to have a conversation.

(a) Explain how **sensory adaptation** allows John to have a conversation despite the loud music.

(b) Explain how **selective attention** helps John focus on his friend's voice.

(c) Using **signal detection theory**, explain factors that might affect John's ability to hear his friend.

(d) Explain how **the cocktail party effect** demonstrates John's attentional abilities.

**Answer**:

(a) **Sensory adaptation**: Over time, John's auditory system becomes less sensitive to the constant background music. This allows his attention to shift to new or changing stimuli (like his friend's voice) rather than the unchanging loud music.

(b) **Selective attention**: John's brain filters out irrelevant sensory information (background noise, other conversations) to focus on his friend's voice. His attention acts like a spotlight, enhancing processing of selected stimuli while diminishing awareness of unselected stimuli.

(c) **Signal detection theory**: John's ability to detect his friend's voice depends on:
- **Signal strength**: How loud his friend speaks
- **Noise level**: Background music and other conversations
- **Psychological state**: John's motivation to hear (high motivation = more hits, but also more false alarms)
- **Expectations**: If John expects certain words, he may perceive them more easily

(d) **Cocktail party effect**: Even while focused on his conversation, John would likely notice if someone across the room said his name. This demonstrates that we unconsciously monitor unattended stimuli and will shift attention when something personally relevant appears.
