### Types of Claims

```
CLAIM CATEGORIES:

CLAIMS OF FACT:
- Argues something is true/false
- "Climate change is accelerating"
- Requires evidence to prove

CLAIMS OF VALUE:
- Argues something is good/bad
- "Education is more important than wealth"
- Requires value criteria

CLAIMS OF POLICY:
- Argues action should be taken
- "Schools should require media literacy"
- Requires problem + solution

CLAIMS OF DEFINITION:
- Argues what something means
- "True patriotism is..."
- Requires criteria and examples

CLAIMS OF CAUSE:
- Argues what causes effects
- "Social media use causes depression"
- Requires causal evidence
```

### The Thesis Statement

```
STRONG THESIS CHARACTERISTICS:

ARGUABLE:
Makes a claim that can be debated
Not just a fact or observation

SPECIFIC:
Clear about what you're arguing
Not vague or general

COMPLEX:
Acknowledges complexity
Shows sophisticated thinking

SUPPORTABLE:
Can be proven with evidence
Research exists to back it up

EXAMPLE EVOLUTION:
Weak: "Social media has effects on teens."
Better: "Social media negatively affects teens."
Strong: "While social media offers connection 
        opportunities, its algorithmically 
        designed addictive features pose 
        significant mental health risks to 
        adolescents that require parental 
        and regulatory intervention."
```

## ğŸ“ 2.1 Using Evidence Effectively

### Evidence Integration

```
EVIDENCE SANDWICH METHOD:

1. INTRODUCE (Top bread)
   Set up the evidence
   Provide context
   "According to Smith (2023)..."

2. PRESENT (Filling)
   Quote or paraphrase
   Include citation
   "...social media use correlates 
   with increased anxiety rates" (p. 45).

3. ANALYZE (Bottom bread)
   Explain significance
   Connect to your argument
   "This research demonstrates that..."
```

### Types of Evidence Use

```
EVIDENCE PURPOSES:

TO SUPPORT:
Evidence that directly backs your claim
"This proves my point because..."

TO EXTEND:
Evidence that adds new dimensions
"Furthermore, this shows..."

TO COMPLICATE:
Evidence that adds nuance
"However, it's more complex..."

TO COUNTER:
Evidence for opposing views
"Critics argue that..."

TO REFUTE:
Evidence against opposition
"Yet this overlooks..."
```

### Signal Phrases

```
INTRODUCING EVIDENCE:

NEUTRAL:
- According to [Author]...
- [Author] states that...
- [Author] found that...
- The study shows...

AGREEING:
- [Author] correctly argues...
- As [Author] demonstrates...
- [Author] convincingly shows...

QUESTIONING:
- [Author] claims that...
- [Author] asserts...
- While [Author] argues...

STRONG AGREEMENT:
- [Author] aptly observes...
- Importantly, [Author] notes...

TRANSITIONS BETWEEN SOURCES:
- Similarly, [Author] finds...
- In contrast, [Author] argues...
- Building on this, [Author]...
```

## ğŸ§  2.2 Reasoning and Logic

### Logical Structures

```
DEDUCTIVE REASONING:
General â†’ Specific

Major Premise: All X have Y
Minor Premise: This is X
Conclusion: This has Y

Example:
All democracies need informed citizens
The US is a democracy
Therefore, the US needs informed citizens

INDUCTIVE REASONING:
Specific â†’ General

Observation 1: Evidence A
Observation 2: Evidence B
Observation 3: Evidence C
Conclusion: Pattern suggests...

Example:
Study 1 shows correlation
Study 2 shows correlation
Study 3 shows correlation
Therefore, correlation likely exists
```

### Logical Fallacies to Avoid

```
COMMON FALLACIES:

AD HOMINEM:
Attacking the person, not argument
"You can't trust him because..."

STRAW MAN:
Misrepresenting opposing view
"They want to ban all cars..."

FALSE DICHOTOMY:
Only two options presented
"Either we do X or disaster occurs"

SLIPPERY SLOPE:
Unfounded chain of events
"If A, then B, then C, then doom"

APPEAL TO AUTHORITY:
Unqualified authority cited
"This celebrity says..."

HASTY GENERALIZATION:
Too few examples
"I know one case, so all..."

CIRCULAR REASONING:
Conclusion in premise
"It's true because it's true"

RED HERRING:
Irrelevant distraction
"But what about..."

FALSE CAUSE:
Correlation â‰  causation
"After A, B occurred, so A caused B"
```

### Strong Reasoning

```
EFFECTIVE REASONING:

WARRANT (Link evidence to claim):
Evidence: Studies show X
Claim: We should do Y
Warrant: Because X means Y is needed

QUALIFICATION (Acknowledge limits):
"In most cases..."
"Generally speaking..."
"With some exceptions..."

BACKING (Support your reasoning):
Why is your logic sound?
What supports your warrant?

EXAMPLE:
Evidence: 90% of experts agree
Claim: We should act accordingly
Warrant: Expert consensus indicates reliability
Backing: Expert consensus has historically 
         been accurate on similar issues
Qualification: While not infallible...
```

## ğŸ”„ 2.3 Counterarguments

### Importance of Counterarguments

```
WHY ADDRESS OPPOSITION:

CREDIBILITY:
Shows you've researched fully
Demonstrates intellectual honesty

THOROUGHNESS:
Proves you understand complexity
Shows sophisticated thinking

PERSUASION:
Preempts reader objections
Strengthens your position

NUANCE:
Acknowledges complexity
Avoids oversimplification
```

### Counterargument Strategies

```
HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:

CONCEDE AND REBUT:
"While it's true that X, 
nonetheless Y is more significant"

REFRAME:
"Although opponents see X, 
this actually supports my point because..."

QUALIFY:
"In some cases X, but in most cases Y"

MINIMIZE:
"While X is a concern, 
the magnitude of Y outweighs it"

EMBRACE COMPLEXITY:
"Both X and Y are true, 
which is why Z is necessary"
```

### Counterargument Structure

```
COUNTERARGUMENT FORMULA:

1. INTRODUCE THE OPPOSITION
   "Some argue that..."
   "Critics contend..."
   "Opponents believe..."

2. PRESENT FAIRLY
   State their view accurately
   Use their best arguments
   Don't strawman

3. RESPOND
   Acknowledge valid points
   Explain limitations
   Rebut with evidence

4. RETURN TO YOUR ARGUMENT
   Reassert your position
   Show why it's stronger
   Transition to next point

EXAMPLE:
"Critics of social media regulation 
argue that it infringes on free speech.
This concern is validâ€”free expression 
is fundamental. However, regulations 
can target harmful algorithms without 
restricting content, addressing safety 
while preserving expression rights."
```

## âœï¸ 2.4 Synthesis

### What Is Synthesis?

```
SYNTHESIS DEFINED:

NOT SUMMARY:
Don't just repeat sources

NOT LIST:
Don't just present one after another

SYNTHESIS IS:
Combining sources to create 
new understanding
Finding connections, tensions,
patterns, and insights

THINK OF IT AS:
Sources are ingredients
Synthesis is the meal
```

### Synthesis Strategies

```
SYNTHESIS APPROACHES:

FIND AGREEMENT:
"Both Smith and Jones argue..."
"Multiple sources confirm..."

FIND TENSION:
"While Smith claims X, Jones contends Y..."
"Sources disagree about..."

FIND GAPS:
"None of the sources address..."
"What's missing is..."

FIND PATTERNS:
"Across sources, a pattern emerges..."
"Common themes include..."

BUILD PROGRESSIVELY:
"Smith established X, which Jones 
extended by showing Y, leading 
Chen to conclude Z"
```

### Synthesis in Practice

```
SYNTHESIS EXAMPLE:

WEAK (Source-by-source):
"Smith says A. Jones says B. Chen says C."

BETTER (Grouped by theme):
"On the topic of X, Smith and Jones 
agree that..., while Chen argues..."

STRONG (True synthesis):
"While Smith's research on A provides 
foundation for understanding X, Jones 
complicates this by introducing B, 
and Chen's findings about C suggest 
that neither fully captures the 
complexity of the issue. Together, 
these perspectives indicate that..."
```

## ğŸ’¬ 2.6 Academic Writing Style

### Formal Academic Voice

```
ACADEMIC STYLE:

USE:
- Third person (generally)
- Formal vocabulary
- Precise language
- Complete sentences
- Proper citations

AVOID:
- Contractions
- Slang
- First person (usually)
- Vague language
- Unsupported claims

EXAMPLE:
Casual: "Social media is bad for teens."
Academic: "Research indicates that 
social media use correlates with 
negative mental health outcomes 
among adolescent populations."
```

### Hedging Language

```
APPROPRIATE HEDGING:

SHOW UNCERTAINTY:
- may, might, could
- suggests, indicates
- appears to
- tends to

LIMIT SCOPE:
- in some cases
- generally
- often
- according to X

ATTRIBUTE:
- Some researchers argue
- The data suggests
- Evidence indicates
- One interpretation is

PURPOSE:
Acknowledges complexity
Shows intellectual humility
Avoids overclaiming
```
