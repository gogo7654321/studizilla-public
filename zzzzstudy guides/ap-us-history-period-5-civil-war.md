|-----------|----------|

## ‚öñÔ∏è Compromise of 1850

### The Crisis

| Issue | Problem |
|-------|---------|
| California | Wanted statehood as free state |
| Balance | Would give North Senate majority |
| Mexican Cession | Slavery status undecided |
| Texas claims | Disputed New Mexico territory |
| Slavery in D.C. | Abolitionists demanded end |

### The Compromise

| Provision | Satisfied |
|-----------|-----------|
| California free state | North |
| No Wilmot Proviso in territories | South |
| Texas debt paid, boundaries set | Texas |
| Slave trade (not slavery) banned in D.C. | North |
| Stronger Fugitive Slave Act | South |
| Popular sovereignty in Utah/New Mexico | Ambiguous |

### Key Figures

| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| Henry Clay | "Great Compromiser" proposed |
| Daniel Webster | Northern support (angered abolitionists) |
| John C. Calhoun | Southern opposition (too weak) |
| Stephen Douglas | Split into separate bills, passed |

## üèÉ Fugitive Slave Act (1850)

### Provisions

| Requirement | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Federal commissioners | Decide cases |
| No jury trial | For alleged fugitives |
| Testimony banned | Accused couldn't testify |
| Citizen assistance | Required to help capture |
| Penalties | Fines for helping fugitives |

### Northern Reaction

| Response | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Personal liberty laws | States obstructed enforcement |
| Underground Railroad | Increased activity |
| Harriet Beecher Stowe | *Uncle Tom's Cabin* (1852) |
| Public outrage | Forced Northerners to confront slavery |

### *Uncle Tom's Cabin*

| Impact | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Bestseller | 300,000 copies first year |
| Emotional appeal | Humanized enslaved people |
| International | Spread anti-slavery sentiment |
| Lincoln's alleged comment | "The little woman who made this great war" |

## ‚öñÔ∏è Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)

### The Case

| Issue | Question |
|-------|----------|
| Dred Scott | Enslaved man, lived in free territory |
| Claim | Residence in free territory made him free |
| Court | Supreme Court, Chief Justice Taney |

### The Decision

| Ruling | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| Scott not a citizen | Can't sue in federal court |
| Black people have "no rights" | Not citizens ever |
| Missouri Compromise unconstitutional | Congress can't ban slavery |
| Slavery protected everywhere | Fifth Amendment property rights |

### Consequences

| Effect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Republican outrage | Their platform ruled unconstitutional |
| Popular sovereignty undermined | Congress has no power anyway |
| Southerners encouraged | Slavery protected nationally |
| Lincoln's response | "House divided" speech |

|---------|-------------|

| Provision | Impact |
|-----------|--------|

|-----------|--------|

## ‚úçÔ∏è Sample Short Answer Questions

### SAQ Example 1

**Briefly explain ONE cause of the Civil War.**

*Sample Answer:*
"A major cause of the Civil War was the expansion of slavery into western territories. After the Mexican-American War, disputes over whether territories like Kansas would allow slavery intensified sectional conflict. The Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854), which allowed popular sovereignty, led to violence in 'Bleeding Kansas' and destroyed the old party system, ultimately leading to the election of Republican Abraham Lincoln, which prompted Southern secession."

### SAQ Example 2

**Briefly explain ONE way that Reconstruction succeeded AND ONE way it failed.**

*Sample Answer:*
"Reconstruction succeeded in establishing constitutional protections for civil rights through the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, which abolished slavery, guaranteed citizenship and due process, and protected voting rights regardless of race. These amendments provided the legal foundation for later civil rights advances.

Reconstruction failed to achieve lasting economic independence for freedpeople because it did not redistribute land. Most formerly enslaved people became sharecroppers, trapped in cycles of debt and economic dependence on the same landowners who had enslaved them, creating conditions that persisted for generations."
