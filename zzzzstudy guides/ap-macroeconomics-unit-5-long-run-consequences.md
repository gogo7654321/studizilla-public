### Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment |
| **Shape** | Downward sloping |
| **Trade-off** | Lower unemployment â†’ higher inflation |

### Definition

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Shape** | Vertical |
| **Location** | At natural rate of unemployment |
| **Trade-off** | None in long run |

### Natural Rate of Unemployment

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|

| Reason | Explanation |

### The Volcker Disinflation (1979-1983)

| Year | Inflation | Unemployment | Fed Funds Rate |
|------|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **1979** | 11.3% | 5.8% | 11.2% |
| **1980** | 13.5% | 7.1% | 13.4% |
| **1981** | 10.3% | 7.6% | 16.4% |
| **1982** | 6.2% | 9.7% | 12.2% |
| **1983** | 3.2% | 9.6% | 9.1% |

**Phillips Curve Interpretation:**
- Volcker's policy moved economy DOWN and RIGHT along SRPC
- Sacrificed jobs to break inflationary expectations
- Once expectations adjusted, SRPC shifted DOWN
- Eventually returned to natural rate at LOWER inflation

### The Sacrifice Ratio

$$\text{Sacrifice Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cumulative Cyclical Unemployment}}{\text{Reduction in Inflation}}$$

**Volcker Example:**
```
Inflation fell from 13.5% to 3.2% = 10.3 percentage points
Excess unemployment (above natural) â‰ˆ 5% average over 3 years
Cumulative excess = 15 percentage points of unemployment

Sacrifice Ratio â‰ˆ 15/10.3 â‰ˆ 1.5

Meaning: Each 1% reduction in inflation "cost" 1.5% extra unemployment
```

### Question 1: Phillips Curve Analysis

**The economy is initially at full employment with 2% inflation. The central bank implements expansionary monetary policy.**

**(a)** On a correctly labeled Phillips Curve graph, show the initial equilibrium and the short-run effect of the policy.

**(b)** Explain what happens to unemployment and inflation in the short run.

**(c)** Explain the long-run adjustment process. Where will the economy end up?

**(d)** What happens to the SRPC during the adjustment?

### Model Answer 1:

**(a)** Graph shows:
- LRPC vertical at natural rate (UN)
- SRPC downward sloping
- Initial point A where SRPC crosses LRPC at 2% inflation
- Arrow moving UP and LEFT to point B on SRPC

**(b)** Short-run effects:
- **Unemployment FALLS** below natural rate (expansionary policy stimulates economy)
- **Inflation RISES** above 2% (movement up along SRPC)
- There IS a short-run trade-off

**(c)** Long-run adjustment:
1. Workers observe higher inflation
2. Workers demand higher nominal wages
3. This increases costs for firms
4. SRAS shifts left (in AD-AS model)
5. Unemployment returns to natural rate
6. Economy ends at natural rate but with HIGHER inflation than before

**(d)** The SRPC shifts RIGHT (up) during adjustment:
- Expected inflation increases
- At each unemployment rate, inflation is now higher
- New equilibrium is on higher SRPC where it intersects LRPC

### Question 2: Economic Growth

**Country X is seeking to increase its rate of economic growth.**

**(a)** Identify and explain two policies that could increase long-run growth.

**(b)** What is the trade-off involved in pursuing higher growth?

**(c)** Using the Rule of 70, if Country X's growth rate increases from 2% to 3%, how many fewer years will it take for GDP to double?

### Model Answer 2:

**(a)** Two growth policies:

1. **Investment in education/human capital**: Improves worker productivity by increasing skills and knowledge. More productive workers produce more output per hour, shifting LRAS right.

2. **Tax incentives for R&D/capital investment**: Lower taxes on investment income encourage businesses to invest in new technology and capital goods. More capital per worker increases productivity.

**(b)** Trade-off:
- Growth requires **sacrificing current consumption** for future consumption
- Resources spent on investment (capital goods, education) cannot be used for consumer goods now
- Shown on PPC: Producing more capital goods means fewer consumer goods
- Present generation bears costs; future generation gets benefits

**(c)** Rule of 70 calculation:
```
At 2% growth: Years to double = 70/2 = 35 years
At 3% growth: Years to double = 70/3 = 23.3 years
Difference = 35 - 23.3 = 11.7 fewer years
```

### Question 3: Crowding Out and Deficits

**The government runs a large budget deficit to finance infrastructure spending.**

**(a)** Using a correctly labeled loanable funds market graph, show the effect of government borrowing.

**(b)** Explain the crowding out effect on private investment.

**(c)** What is the long-run consequence for economic growth?

**(d)** Explain one condition under which crowding out might be minimal.

### Model Answer 3:

**(a)** Loanable Funds Graph:
- Demand shifts RIGHT (government enters market as borrower)
- Real interest rate INCREASES from râ‚ to râ‚‚
- Quantity of loanable funds increases but mostly goes to government

**(b)** Crowding out:
- Higher interest rates increase the cost of borrowing
- Businesses face higher financing costs for investment projects
- Fewer investment projects are profitable at higher rates
- Private investment DECREASES
- Some private spending is "crowded out" by government spending

**(c)** Long-run growth consequence:
- Less private investment means slower capital accumulation
- Slower growth in capital stock
- Lower productivity growth
- LRAS shifts right more slowly
- Future potential GDP is lower than it would have been

**(d)** Condition for minimal crowding out:
**During a recession with high unemployment**, crowding out is minimal because:
- Banks have excess reserves they're not lending
- Private investment demand is already low
- There's excess saving looking for borrowers
- Government borrowing uses funds that would otherwise sit idle
- Interest rates may not rise significantly
