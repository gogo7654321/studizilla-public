**Question**: A student feels anxious before an important exam. Using your knowledge of psychology, explain how each of the following relates to this situation:

(a) Yerkes-Dodson law
(b) Schachter-Singer two-factor theory
(c) Problem-focused coping
(d) Self-efficacy

**Answer**:

(a) **Yerkes-Dodson law**: This law states that performance is optimal at moderate arousal levels. For a complex task like an exam, too much anxiety (high arousal) may interfere with the student's ability to think clearly and recall information. Moderate arousal might help the student stay alert and focused, but extreme anxiety could lead to poor performance. The student should aim to reduce anxiety to an optimal level.

(b) **Schachter-Singer two-factor theory**: According to this theory, the student's emotional experience of anxiety depends on both physiological arousal and cognitive interpretation. The student notices symptoms like racing heart and sweaty palms (arousal) and attributes these to the upcoming exam (cognitive label). If the student relabeled the arousal as excitement rather than anxiety, they might experience a different emotion despite the same physical sensations.

(c) **Problem-focused coping**: This involves taking direct action to address the stressor. The student could use problem-focused coping by studying effectively, organizing study materials, creating a study schedule, or seeking help from a teacher or tutor. By addressing the actual source of stress (the exam), the student may reduce their anxiety and improve their performance.

(d) **Self-efficacy**: Self-efficacy refers to one's belief in their ability to succeed at a specific task. A student with high self-efficacy would believe they can perform well on the exam, which would reduce anxiety and increase effort and persistence. A student with low self-efficacy might feel helpless and be more likely to give up. Previous successes, encouragement, and watching peers succeed can boost the student's self-efficacy.
