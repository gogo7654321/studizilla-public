## üî≠ CELL THEORY

### THE THREE TENETS

**Cell Theory** (1839 - Schleiden, Schwann, Virchow):

1. **All living things are made of cells**
   - Single-celled: Bacteria, amoeba, paramecium
   - Multicellular: Animals, plants, fungi

2. **The cell is the basic unit of life**
   - Smallest unit that can perform all life functions
   - Cannot divide a cell and have it remain alive

3. **All cells come from pre-existing cells**
   - *"Omnis cellula e cellula"* (Virchow)
   - Cells arise through cell division
   - No spontaneous generation

### EXCEPTIONS AND MODIFICATIONS

**Viruses**: Not cells, not considered living
- Need host cell to reproduce
- No metabolism on their own

**First cell**: Must have arisen from non-living matter
- Origin of life ~3.8 billion years ago
- Chemical evolution ‚Üí first self-replicating molecules

- Single circular DNA molecule
- Located in nucleoid region
- Not associated with histones (in bacteria)

**Plasmids:**
- Small circular DNA
- Replicate independently
- Can transfer between cells (horizontal gene transfer)
- Often carry antibiotic resistance genes

## üß¨ THE NUCLEUS

### STRUCTURE

**Nucleus**: Control center of cell, contains DNA

**Components:**

| Structure | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Nuclear envelope** | Double membrane with pores |
| **Nuclear pores** | Allow passage of RNA, proteins |
| **Chromatin** | DNA + proteins (histones) |
| **Nucleolus** | Produces ribosomal RNA (rRNA) |

**Chromatin condensation:**
- **Euchromatin**: Loosely packed, active genes
- **Heterochromatin**: Densely packed, inactive genes

### NUCLEAR ENVELOPE

**Double membrane:**
- Outer membrane continuous with ER
- Inner membrane attached to nuclear lamina
- Perinuclear space between membranes

**Nuclear pores:**
- ~3000-4000 per nucleus
- Complex protein structure (nuclear pore complex)
- Regulates molecular traffic
- Small molecules: Passive diffusion
- Large molecules: Active transport (requires signal)

### CHROMOSOME STRUCTURE

**During interphase:** DNA as chromatin (loosely packed)
**During division:** DNA condenses into chromosomes

**Histone proteins:**
- DNA wraps around histone octamer
- Forms nucleosome (basic unit)
- Further coiling ‚Üí chromatin fiber ‚Üí chromosome

‚Üí Symbiotic relationship ‚Üí became organelle

**Result:** Increased ATP production, allowed larger cell size!

## üì¶ ENDOMEMBRANE SYSTEM

### OVERVIEW

**Endomembrane system**: Network of membranes working together

**Components:**
- Nuclear envelope
- Endoplasmic reticulum (ER)
- Golgi apparatus
- Lysosomes
- Vacuoles
- Plasma membrane

**Not part of endomembrane system:**
- Mitochondria
- Chloroplasts
- Peroxisomes

### ENDOPLASMIC RETICULUM (ER)

**ER**: Network of membranous tubules and sacs

**Two types:**

**1. Rough ER (RER):**
- Studded with ribosomes
- **Functions:**
  - Protein synthesis (for secretion or membranes)
  - Protein folding
  - Glycoprotein synthesis
  - Quality control

**2. Smooth ER (SER):**
- No ribosomes
- **Functions:**
  - Lipid synthesis (phospholipids, steroids)
  - Carbohydrate metabolism
  - Detoxification (liver cells)
  - Calcium storage (muscle cells)

**Transition from RER to SER** depends on cell needs

### GOLGI APPARATUS

**Golgi**: "Post office of the cell" - modifies, sorts, packages proteins

**Structure:**
- Stack of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae)
- **Cis face**: Receiving side (from ER)
- **Trans face**: Shipping side (to destination)

**Functions:**

| Step | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1. **Receive** | Vesicles from ER fuse with cis face |
| 2. **Modify** | Add carbohydrates, phosphates, lipids |
| 3. **Sort** | Tag proteins for destinations |
| 4. **Package** | Vesicles bud from trans face |
| 5. **Ship** | Vesicles go to plasma membrane, lysosomes, etc. |

**Modifications:**
- Glycosylation (adding sugars)
- Phosphorylation (adding phosphates)
- Proteolytic cleavage (cutting proteins)

### LYSOSOMES (Animal cells)

**Lysosome**: "Garbage disposal" - digestive organelle

**Contents:**
- ~40 hydrolytic enzymes
- Acidic pH (~5) maintained by H‚Å∫ pumps
- Enzymes active only at low pH (safety mechanism!)

**Functions:**

| Process | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Autophagy** | Digest damaged organelles |
| **Phagocytosis** | Digest bacteria, debris |
| **Extracellular digestion** | Release enzymes outside cell |
| **Programmed cell death** | Release enzymes to digest cell |

**Lysosomal storage diseases:** Genetic defects in lysosomal enzymes
- Example: Tay-Sachs disease (cannot break down gangliosides)

### VACUOLES

**Vacuole**: Membrane-bound sac for storage

**Plant central vacuole:**
- Up to 90% of cell volume!
- **Functions:**
  - Storage (water, ions, pigments, toxins)
  - Turgor pressure (keeps plant rigid)
  - Waste disposal
  - Breakdown of macromolecules

**Animal vacuoles:**
- Smaller, multiple
- Food vacuoles (digestion)
- Contractile vacuoles (water removal in protists)

### PEROXISOMES

**Peroxisome**: Oxidative organelle

**Functions:**
- Break down fatty acids
- Detoxify alcohol, other poisons
- Produce hydrogen peroxide (H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ)
- Convert H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí H‚ÇÇO + O‚ÇÇ (using catalase)

**Not part of endomembrane system** - bud from ER but not in vesicle pathway

### OVERVIEW

**Cytoskeleton**: Network of protein fibers

**Functions:**
- üèóÔ∏è Structural support
- üöõ Intracellular transport
- üèÉ Cell movement
- ‚úÇÔ∏è Cell division

### THREE TYPES OF FIBERS

| Fiber | Diameter | Protein | Functions |
|-------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Microfilaments** | 7 nm | Actin | Cell shape, muscle contraction, cytokinesis, amoeboid movement |
| **Intermediate filaments** | 8-12 nm | Various (keratin, etc.) | Tension-bearing, nuclear lamina, cell-cell junctions |
| **Microtubules** | 25 nm | Tubulin (Œ± and Œ≤) | Cell shape, chromosome movement, cilia/flagella, vesicle transport |

### MICROFILAMENTS (Actin Filaments)

**Structure:** Two intertwined actin strands

**Functions:**
- Maintain cell shape
- Muscle contraction (with myosin)
- Cytoplasmic streaming
- Cell division (contractile ring)
- Pseudopod formation (amoeboid movement)

**Dynamic:** Can polymerize and depolymerize rapidly

### INTERMEDIATE FILAMENTS

**Structure:** Rope-like, more permanent

**Functions:**
- Tension-bearing (like ropes)
- Nuclear lamina (inside nuclear envelope)
- Anchor organelles
- Cell-cell junctions (desmosomes)

**Examples:**
- Keratin (in hair, nails)
- Neurofilaments (in neurons)

**Most stable** of the three types

### MICROTUBULES

**Structure:**
- Hollow tube of Œ± and Œ≤ tubulin dimers
- 13 protofilaments arranged in circle
- Plus end (fast-growing) and minus end

**Functions:**

| Function | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Cell shape** | Compression-resistant (like pillars) |
| **Chromosome movement** | Form mitotic spindle |
| **Vesicle transport** | Tracks for motor proteins (kinesin, dynein) |
| **Cilia and flagella** | 9+2 arrangement |

**Centrosome:** Microtubule-organizing center (MTOC)
- Contains two centrioles
- Spindle forms from centrosomes during division

### MOTOR PROTEINS

**Motor proteins**: "Molecular motors" walk along cytoskeleton

| Protein | Track | Direction | Function |
|---------|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Myosin** | Actin | Toward plus end | Muscle contraction, cytokinesis |
| **Kinesin** | Microtubule | Toward plus end | Vesicle transport to periphery |
| **Dynein** | Microtubule | Toward minus end | Vesicle transport to center, cilia/flagella beating |

**Mechanism:**
1. Motor protein binds cargo (vesicle)
2. Uses ATP to "walk" along fiber
3. Delivers cargo to destination

## üö™ CELL MEMBRANE STRUCTURE

### FLUID MOSAIC MODEL

**Plasma membrane**: Selective barrier surrounding cell

**Fluid Mosaic Model** (Singer & Nicolson, 1972):
- **"Fluid"**: Phospholipids move laterally
- **"Mosaic"**: Proteins embedded in various patterns

### PHOSPHOLIPID BILAYER

**Structure:**
```
     ~~~~ Extracellular fluid ~~~~
     üîµüîµüîµüîµüîµüîµ  ‚Üê Hydrophilic heads (phosphate)
     ||||||||||||  ‚Üê Fatty acid tails
     ||||||||||||  ‚Üê Fatty acid tails
     üîµüîµüîµüîµüîµüîµ  ‚Üê Hydrophilic heads
     ~~~~ Cytoplasm ~~~~
```

**Amphipathic:** Both hydrophilic (heads) and hydrophobic (tails)

**Self-assembling:** Forms bilayer spontaneously in water!

**Properties:**
- Hydrophobic core: Barrier to polar molecules
- Fluid: Phospholipids move laterally (~10‚Å∑ times per second!)
- Asymmetric: Inner and outer leaflets differ

### MEMBRANE FLUIDITY

**Factors affecting fluidity:**

| Factor | Effect on Fluidity |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Temperature** | ‚Üë Temp ‚Üí ‚Üë Fluidity |
| **Saturated fatty acids** | ‚Üì Fluidity (pack tightly) |
| **Unsaturated fatty acids** | ‚Üë Fluidity (kinks prevent packing) |
| **Cholesterol** | Moderates (‚Üì fluidity when high, ‚Üë when low) |
| **Chain length** | Longer chains ‚Üí ‚Üì Fluidity |

**Homeoviscous adaptation:** Organisms adjust membrane composition to maintain optimal fluidity
- Cold environment: ‚Üë unsaturated fatty acids
- Hot environment: ‚Üë saturated fatty acids, ‚Üë cholesterol

### MEMBRANE PROTEINS

**Two categories:**

**1. Integral proteins:**
- Embedded in membrane
- Often transmembrane (span bilayer)
- Hydrophobic regions interact with fatty acid tails
- Amphipathic

**2. Peripheral proteins:**
- Loosely attached to surface
- Not embedded
- Often attached to integral proteins

**Functions of membrane proteins:**

| Function | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Transport** | Channels, carriers, pumps |
| **Enzymatic activity** | Metabolic pathways |
| **Signal transduction** | Receptors |
| **Cell recognition** | Glycoproteins (cell-cell recognition) |
| **Intercellular joining** | Gap junctions, tight junctions |
| **Attachment** | Cytoskeleton anchoring, ECM binding |

### MEMBRANE CARBOHYDRATES

**Glycocalyx**: Carbohydrate coat on cell surface

**Types:**
- **Glycoproteins**: Carbohydrate + protein
- **Glycolipids**: Carbohydrate + lipid

**Functions:**
- Cell-cell recognition
- Immune system (self vs non-self)
- Cell adhesion
- Protection

**Example:** ABO blood types determined by glycoproteins

## üö∞ PASSIVE TRANSPORT

### OVERVIEW

**Passive transport**: Movement down concentration gradient, no energy (ATP) required

**Types:**
1. Simple diffusion
2. Facilitated diffusion
3. Osmosis (special case of diffusion)

### DIFFUSION

**Diffusion**: Net movement from high to low concentration

**Factors affecting rate:**

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Concentration gradient** | Steeper gradient ‚Üí Faster |
| **Temperature** | Higher temp ‚Üí Faster |
| **Molecular size** | Smaller molecules ‚Üí Faster |
| **Distance** | Shorter distance ‚Üí Faster |
| **Surface area** | Larger area ‚Üí Faster |

**Fick's Law:**
$$\text{Rate} \propto \frac{\text{Surface Area} \times \text{Concentration Difference}}{\text{Distance}}$$

**Examples:**
- O‚ÇÇ diffuses into cells
- CO‚ÇÇ diffuses out of cells
- Perfume spreading in room

### SIMPLE DIFFUSION ACROSS MEMBRANE

**Can cross membrane:**
- Small nonpolar molecules (O‚ÇÇ, CO‚ÇÇ, N‚ÇÇ)
- Small uncharged polar molecules (H‚ÇÇO, glycerol)
- Lipid-soluble molecules

**Cannot cross membrane (need help):**
- Large polar molecules (glucose, sucrose)
- Ions (Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª)

### FACILITATED DIFFUSION

**Facilitated diffusion**: Diffusion with help of transport proteins

**Still passive:** Down gradient, no ATP

**Two types of transport proteins:**

**1. Channel proteins:**
- Form hydrophilic tunnel
- Often specific for one ion
- Can be gated (open/close in response to signal)
- Very fast (~10‚Å∏ ions/second!)

**Examples:**
- Ion channels (Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Cl‚Åª)
- Aquaporins (water channels)

**2. Carrier proteins:**
- Bind specific molecule
- Change shape to move molecule across
- Slower than channels (~1000 molecules/second)

**Examples:**
- Glucose transporter (GLUT)
- Amino acid transporters

### OSMOSIS

**Osmosis**: Diffusion of water across selectively permeable membrane

**Direction:** From low solute concentration ‚Üí high solute concentration
(Same as: From high water concentration ‚Üí low water concentration)

**Water potential (Œ®):** Measure of free energy of water

$$\Psi = \Psi_s + \Psi_p$$

Where:
- $\Psi_s$ = solute potential (always negative or zero)
- $\Psi_p$ = pressure potential (can be positive or negative)

**Water moves from high Œ® to low Œ®**

**Pure water:** Œ® = 0 (maximum water potential)
**Adding solute:** Œ® decreases (becomes more negative)

### TONICITY

**Tonicity**: Relative concentration of solutes

**Three scenarios:**

| Solution | [Solute] Compared to Cell | Water Movement | Cell Response |
|----------|---------------------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Isotonic** | Equal | No net movement | No change |
| **Hypotonic** | Lower (more water) | Water enters cell | **Animal**: Swells, may lyse (burst) / **Plant**: Turgid (firm) |
| **Hypertonic** | Higher (less water) | Water leaves cell | **Animal**: Shrivels (crenation) / **Plant**: Plasmolysis (wilts) |

**Turgor pressure:** Pressure of cytoplasm against cell wall
- Essential for plant structure
- Plants wilt when turgid pressure lost

**Plasmolysis:** Plasma membrane pulls away from cell wall (hypertonic solution)

## ‚ö° ACTIVE TRANSPORT

### OVERVIEW

**Active transport**: Movement against concentration gradient, requires energy (ATP)

**Types:**
1. Primary active transport (direct ATP use)
2. Secondary active transport (indirect ATP use)

### PRIMARY ACTIVE TRANSPORT

**Mechanism:** Transport protein (pump) hydrolyzes ATP directly

**Example: Sodium-Potassium Pump (Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase)**

**Function:** Maintains concentration gradients

**Mechanism:**
1. 3 Na‚Å∫ bind inside cell
2. ATP phosphorylates pump
3. Pump changes shape, releases 3 Na‚Å∫ outside
4. 2 K‚Å∫ bind outside
5. Phosphate released
6. Pump changes shape, releases 2 K‚Å∫ inside
7. Cycle repeats

**Result:**
- [Na‚Å∫] higher outside cell
- [K‚Å∫] higher inside cell
- Creates electrochemical gradient
- Uses ~30% of cell's ATP!

**Importance:**
- Maintains cell volume (osmotic balance)
- Nerve impulse transmission
- Muscle contraction
- Secondary active transport

### SECONDARY ACTIVE TRANSPORT (Cotransport)

**Mechanism:** Uses gradient created by primary active transport

**No direct ATP use,** but depends on Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ pump!

**Two types:**

**1. Symport (same direction):**
- Both molecules move same direction
- Example: Na‚Å∫/glucose cotransporter (intestine, kidney)
  - Na‚Å∫ down gradient (in) + glucose against gradient (in)

**2. Antiport (opposite directions):**
- Molecules move opposite directions
- Example: Na‚Å∫/H‚Å∫ exchanger
  - Na‚Å∫ in, H‚Å∫ out

## üì¶ BULK TRANSPORT

### OVERVIEW

**Bulk transport**: Movement of large molecules or particles

**Types:**
- **Exocytosis**: Out of cell
- **Endocytosis**: Into cell

**Requires:** Energy (ATP) and vesicles

### EXOCYTOSIS

**Exocytosis**: Secretion via vesicle fusion with plasma membrane

**Process:**
1. Vesicle from Golgi moves to plasma membrane
2. Vesicle fuses with membrane
3. Contents released outside cell
4. Vesicle membrane becomes part of plasma membrane

**Examples:**
- Neurotransmitter release (neurons)
- Hormone secretion (endocrine cells)
- Enzyme secretion (pancreas)
- Waste removal

### ENDOCYTOSIS

**Endocytosis**: Taking in material via vesicle formation

**Three types:**

**1. Phagocytosis** ("cell eating"):
- Large particles (bacteria, debris)
- Forms phagosome
- Fuses with lysosome ‚Üí digestion
- Example: White blood cells engulf bacteria

**2. Pinocytosis** ("cell drinking"):
- Liquids and small particles
- Small vesicles
- Nonspecific
- Example: Nutrient uptake

**3. Receptor-mediated endocytosis:**
- Specific molecules bind receptors
- Receptors cluster in coated pit (clathrin protein)
- Vesicle forms (coated vesicle)
- Very efficient and selective
- Example: Cholesterol uptake (LDL receptors)

**Comparison:**

| Type | Size | Specificity | Example |
|------|------|-------------|---------|
| Phagocytosis | Large | Can be specific | Bacteria |
| Pinocytosis | Small | Nonspecific | Nutrients |
| Receptor-mediated | Variable | Highly specific | Cholesterol |

## üì° CELL COMMUNICATION

### OVERVIEW

**Cell signaling**: Cells communicate via chemical signals

**Steps:**
1. **Signal reception**
2. **Signal transduction**
3. **Cellular response**

### SIGNAL RECEPTION

**Receptor**: Protein that binds signaling molecule (ligand)

**Types of receptors:**

**1. Plasma membrane receptors:**
- For hydrophilic (water-soluble) signals
- Cannot cross membrane
- Examples: Peptide hormones, growth factors, neurotransmitters

**2. Intracellular receptors:**
- For hydrophobic (lipid-soluble) signals
- Can cross membrane
- Examples: Steroid hormones, thyroid hormones, NO

**Ligand-receptor binding:**
- Highly specific
- Induces conformational change in receptor
- Triggers transduction cascade

### SIGNAL TRANSDUCTION

**Transduction**: Converting signal to cellular response

**Often involves:**
- Second messengers (cAMP, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, IP‚ÇÉ)
- Phosphorylation cascades
- Amplification

**Second messengers:**

| Messenger | Production | Effects |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **cAMP** | From ATP by adenylyl cyclase | Activates protein kinase A (PKA) |
| **Ca¬≤‚Å∫** | Released from ER | Activates calmodulin, other proteins |
| **IP‚ÇÉ** | From PIP‚ÇÇ by phospholipase C | Triggers Ca¬≤‚Å∫ release |
| **DAG** | From PIP‚ÇÇ by phospholipase C | Activates protein kinase C (PKC) |

**Phosphorylation cascade:**
- Kinase phosphorylates target protein
- Target protein activated
- Activated protein phosphorylates next protein
- Chain reaction continues
- **Amplification:** One signal ‚Üí many responses

### CELLULAR RESPONSE

**Response depends on cell type:**
- Gene expression changes
- Enzyme activation/deactivation
- Cytoskeleton rearrangement
- Cell division
- Apoptosis

**Termination:**
- Ligand dissociates
- Receptor deactivated
- Enzymes break down second messengers
- Phosphatases remove phosphate groups

## üìè SURFACE AREA TO VOLUME RATIO

### THE PROBLEM

**As cell grows:**
- Volume increases faster than surface area
- $\text{Volume} \propto r^3$
- $\text{Surface Area} \propto r^2$

$$\text{SA:V ratio} = \frac{4\pi r^2}{\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3} = \frac{3}{r}$$

**As radius increases, SA:V ratio decreases!**

### WHY IT MATTERS

**Cells need:**
- Large surface area for exchange (nutrients in, wastes out)
- Small volume for efficient distribution

**Large SA:V ratio:**
- ‚úÖ More efficient exchange
- ‚úÖ Shorter diffusion distances
- ‚úÖ Better heat dissipation

**Small SA:V ratio:**
- ‚ùå Insufficient exchange
- ‚ùå Slower diffusion
- ‚ùå Cell stress

**This limits cell size!**

### SOLUTIONS TO SIZE LIMITS

**Small cells** (optimal strategy)

**Flattened/elongated shapes:**
- Increases SA without much volume increase
- Examples: Nerve cells, muscle cells

**Folded membranes:**
- Microvilli (intestine)
- Cristae (mitochondria)

**Multicellularity:**
- Many small cells instead of one giant cell
- Allows large organism size

**Example calculations:**

| Cell Size | Surface Area | Volume | SA:V Ratio |
|-----------|--------------|--------|------------|
| 1 Œºm cube | 6 Œºm¬≤ | 1 Œºm¬≥ | 6:1 |

### FOR CELL IDENTIFICATION

### FOR TRANSPORT PROBLEMS

1. **Identify gradient direction** (with or against?)
2. **Determine if energy needed** (against = active)
3. **Consider molecule properties** (size, charge, polarity)

**Decision tree:**
- Nonpolar/small ‚Üí Simple diffusion
- Polar/charged + with gradient ‚Üí Facilitated diffusion
- Against gradient ‚Üí Active transport
- Very large ‚Üí Bulk transport

### FOR OSMOSIS PROBLEMS

1. **Compare solute concentrations** (hypo/iso/hypertonic)
2. **Predict water movement** (toward higher solute)
3. **Predict cell response** (animal vs plant differs!)

### FOR SA:V CALCULATIONS

$$\text{SA:V ratio} = \frac{\text{Surface Area}}{\text{Volume}}$$
