## üìú Part 1: Foundational Documents (All 9 Required)

### 1. Declaration of Independence (1776)
**Author:** Thomas Jefferson
**Key Ideas:**
- Natural rights: life, liberty, pursuit of happiness
- Social contract theory (influenced by John Locke)
- Popular sovereignty: government derives power from consent of governed
- Right of revolution when government becomes tyrannical

**Exam Focus:** Foundation for democratic ideals, limited government, individual rights

---

### 2. Articles of Confederation (1781)
**Purpose:** First governing document of the United States

**Key Features:**
- Unicameral legislature (one house)
- No executive or judicial branch
- States retained sovereignty
- Congress could not tax or regulate commerce
- Required unanimous consent for amendments
- Required 9/13 states to pass laws

**Weaknesses:**
| Problem | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| No taxing power | Couldn't pay war debts |
| No commerce regulation | Trade wars between states |
| No executive | No enforcement of laws |
| Unanimous amendments | Nearly impossible to change |
| No national currency | Economic chaos |

**Exam Focus:** Why a stronger federal government was needed ‚Üí leads to Constitution

---

### 3. Constitution of the United States (1787)

**Preamble Goals (6 Purposes):**
1. Form a more perfect union
2. Establish justice
3. Ensure domestic tranquility
4. Provide for common defense
5. Promote general welfare
6. Secure blessings of liberty

**Structure:**
- **Article I:** Legislative Branch (Congress)
- **Article II:** Executive Branch (President)
- **Article III:** Judicial Branch (Courts)
- **Article IV:** States' relations
- **Article V:** Amendment process
- **Article VI:** Supremacy Clause
- **Article VII:** Ratification

**Key Principles:**
| Principle | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| Popular Sovereignty | Power from the people |
| Limited Government | Government has only delegated powers |
| Separation of Powers | Three distinct branches |
| Checks and Balances | Each branch limits others |
| Federalism | Power divided between national and state |
| Judicial Review | Courts interpret constitutionality |

**Amendment Process:**
```
Proposal (2/3 of both houses OR 2/3 of state legislatures call convention)
                           ‚Üì
Ratification (3/4 of state legislatures OR 3/4 of state conventions)
```

---

### 4. Federalist No. 10 (1787)
**Author:** James Madison

**Central Argument:** Large republic is the best defense against factions

**Key Concepts:**
- **Faction:** Group united by common interest adverse to rights of others or community
- **Two ways to remove factions:** Destroy liberty (worse than disease) OR give everyone same opinions (impractical)
- **Solution:** Control the EFFECTS of factions through:
  - Large republic with many factions (prevents majority tyranny)
  - Representative democracy (filters public views through elected delegates)
  - Extended sphere (variety of interests)

**Quote to Know:** "The latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man."

**Exam Focus:** Pluralist democracy, republican government vs. direct democracy

---

### 5. Federalist No. 51 (1787)
**Author:** James Madison

**Central Argument:** Separation of powers and checks and balances protect liberty

**Key Concepts:**
- "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition"
- Each branch must have constitutional means to resist encroachments
- "If men were angels, no government would be necessary"
- Double security: Federal system divides power between national and state; then each level has separation of powers

**Compound Republic Structure:**
```
        Federal Government
       /         |         \
  Legislative  Executive  Judicial
       ‚Üë         ‚Üë         ‚Üë
    Checks and Balances
       ‚Üì         ‚Üì         ‚Üì
        State Governments
       /         |         \
  Legislative  Executive  Judicial
```

**Exam Focus:** Checks and balances, separation of powers, federalism

---

### 6. Federalist No. 70 (1788)
**Author:** Alexander Hamilton

**Central Argument:** A single, energetic executive is essential for good government

**Key Concepts:**
- **Energy in the executive requires:**
  - Unity (single president, not plural)
  - Duration (sufficient term length)
  - Adequate compensation
  - Competent powers
- Single executive ensures accountability
- Plural executive leads to:
  - Disagreement and delay
  - Divided responsibility
  - Difficulty assigning blame

**Quote to Know:** "Energy in the Executive is a leading character in the definition of good government."

**Exam Focus:** Unitary executive, presidential power, accountability

---

### 7. Federalist No. 78 (1788)
**Author:** Alexander Hamilton

**Central Argument:** Judiciary is the "least dangerous branch" and judicial review is necessary

**Key Concepts:**
- Judiciary has "neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment"
- Judges should serve during "good behavior" (life tenure)
- **Judicial Review:** Courts can declare laws unconstitutional
- Constitution is fundamental law; judges must prefer Constitution over ordinary laws
- Lifetime appointment protects judicial independence

**Quote to Know:** "The interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts."

**Exam Focus:** Judicial review (before Marbury v. Madison!), judicial independence

---

### 8. Brutus No. 1 (1787)
**Author:** "Brutus" (likely Robert Yates)

**Position:** Anti-Federalist opposing Constitution ratification

**Central Arguments:**
- Republic cannot govern large territory effectively
- Necessary and Proper Clause gives too much power
- Supremacy Clause threatens state sovereignty
- Standing army threatens liberty
- Federal courts will expand national power

**Key Concerns:**
| Issue | Brutus's Fear |
|-------|---------------|
| Size of Republic | Too large for representatives to know constituents |
| Taxing Power | Federal government will abuse it |
| Necessary & Proper | "Elastic clause" will stretch federal power |
| No Bill of Rights | Individual liberties unprotected |

**Exam Focus:** Anti-Federalist arguments, concerns about tyranny

---

### 9. Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963)
**Author:** Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

**Context:** Written while jailed for nonviolent protest in Birmingham, Alabama

**Central Arguments:**
- Moral obligation to disobey unjust laws
- **Just vs. Unjust Laws:**
  - Just law: Aligns with moral law, uplifts human personality
  - Unjust law: Degrades human personality, inflicted on minority by majority
- Critique of "white moderate" who prefers order over justice
- Direct action creates tension to force negotiation
- Civil disobedience has long moral tradition (Socrates, early Christians)

**Key Quote:** "Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere."

**Exam Focus:** Civil rights movement, civil disobedience, First Amendment freedoms

## ‚öñÔ∏è Part 2: Required Supreme Court Cases (All 15)

### First Amendment Cases

#### 1. Engel v. Vitale (1962)
**Issue:** School-sponsored prayer in public schools
**Ruling:** 6-1 against school prayer
**Holding:** School-sponsored prayer violates Establishment Clause
**Significance:** Strict separation of church and state in public schools
**Key Principle:** Government cannot compose or require official prayers

---

#### 2. Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)
**Issue:** Amish families refusing to send children to school past 8th grade
**Ruling:** Unanimous for Yoder
**Holding:** Free Exercise Clause protects Amish religious practices
**Significance:** Religious freedom can override compulsory education laws
**Key Principle:** Compelling interest test for religious liberty cases

---

#### 3. Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)
**Issue:** Students wearing black armbands to protest Vietnam War
**Ruling:** 7-2 for Tinker
**Holding:** Students don't "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate"
**Significance:** Students have First Amendment rights in schools
**Test Established:** Substantial disruption test‚Äîspeech can be limited only if it materially disrupts education

---

#### 4. Schenck v. United States (1919)
**Issue:** Distribution of anti-draft pamphlets during WWI
**Ruling:** Unanimous against Schenck
**Holding:** Speech creating "clear and present danger" is not protected
**Significance:** Limits on free speech during wartime
**Key Quote:** "Fire in a crowded theater" analogy (Holmes)

---

#### 5. New York Times v. United States (1971)
**Issue:** Prior restraint on publishing Pentagon Papers
**Ruling:** 6-3 for NY Times
**Holding:** Government cannot use prior restraint except in extreme circumstances
**Significance:** Strong protection for freedom of the press
**Key Principle:** Heavy presumption against prior restraint

---

### Due Process & Rights of the Accused

#### 6. Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)
**Issue:** Right to attorney in state criminal cases
**Ruling:** Unanimous for Gideon
**Holding:** 6th Amendment right to counsel applies to states via 14th Amendment
**Significance:** Established right to public defender for indigent defendants
**Key Principle:** Selective incorporation of Bill of Rights

---

#### 7. Roe v. Wade (1973) *[Note: Overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson, 2022]*
**Issue:** State law banning most abortions
**Ruling:** 7-2 for Roe
**Original Holding:** Right to privacy includes abortion (trimester framework)
**Current Status:** Overturned; states may regulate/ban abortion
**Key Principle:** Substantive due process, evolving constitutional interpretation

---

#### 8. McDonald v. Chicago (2010)
**Issue:** Chicago handgun ban
**Ruling:** 5-4 for McDonald
**Holding:** 2nd Amendment right to bear arms applies to states
**Significance:** Incorporated 2nd Amendment via 14th Amendment Due Process
**Key Principle:** Selective incorporation continues to present day

---

### Equal Protection Cases

#### 9. Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
**Issue:** Racial segregation in public schools
**Ruling:** Unanimous for Brown
**Holding:** "Separate but equal" is inherently unequal; violates Equal Protection
**Significance:** Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson, launched desegregation
**Key Quote:** "Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal."

---

### Federalism & Government Power

#### 10. Marbury v. Madison (1803)
**Issue:** William Marbury's commission as justice of peace
**Ruling:** Court ruled for Madison (no jurisdiction)
**Holding:** Established JUDICIAL REVIEW‚Äîcourts can declare laws unconstitutional
**Significance:** Most important case in establishing judicial power
**Key Quote:** "It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."

---

#### 11. McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)
**Issue:** Maryland taxing the national bank
**Ruling:** Unanimous for McCulloch/federal government
**Holdings:**
1. Necessary and Proper Clause allows implied powers (creating bank)
2. States cannot tax federal institutions (supremacy)
**Key Quote:** "The power to tax is the power to destroy."
**Significance:** Expanded federal power through implied powers

---

#### 12. United States v. Lopez (1995)
**Issue:** Gun-Free School Zones Act
**Ruling:** 5-4 for Lopez
**Holding:** Congress exceeded Commerce Clause power
**Significance:** First limit on Commerce Clause in 60 years
**Key Principle:** Federalism limits; some areas reserved to states

---

### Voting & Elections

#### 13. Baker v. Carr (1962)
**Issue:** Tennessee legislative districts of vastly unequal population
**Ruling:** 6-2 for Baker
**Holding:** Redistricting is justiciable (courts can decide)
**Significance:** Led to "one person, one vote" standard
**Key Principle:** Political questions doctrine has limits

---

#### 14. Shaw v. Reno (1993)
**Issue:** Bizarrely shaped North Carolina congressional district (race-based)
**Ruling:** 5-4 for Shaw
**Holding:** Racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection
**Significance:** Race cannot be predominant factor in redistricting
**Test:** Strict scrutiny applies to race-based districts

---

### Citizenship & Civil Rights

#### 15. Citizens United v. FEC (2010)
**Issue:** Corporate-funded campaign ads (electioneering communications)
**Ruling:** 5-4 for Citizens United
**Holding:** Corporations have free speech rights; campaign spending is speech
**Significance:** Overturned limits on corporate/union election spending
**Key Principle:** Money = speech; led to Super PACs

## üèõÔ∏è Part 4: Federalism

### Types of Powers

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| Enumerated | Explicitly granted to federal government | Coin money, declare war, regulate commerce |
| Implied | Derived from Necessary and Proper Clause | Create national bank, draft soldiers |
| Reserved | Kept by states (10th Amendment) | Education, marriage, voting requirements |
| Concurrent | Shared by federal and state | Tax, borrow, establish courts |
| Denied | Prohibited to government | Bill of attainder, ex post facto laws |

### Evolution of Federalism

| Era | Type | Characteristics |
|-----|------|-----------------|
| 1789-1932 | Dual Federalism | "Layer cake"; clear separation; states dominant |
| 1932-1968 | Cooperative Federalism | "Marble cake"; shared responsibilities; New Deal |
| 1968-present | New Federalism | Return power to states; block grants; devolution |

### Types of Grants

| Grant Type | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Categorical | Specific purpose; strict requirements |
| Block | Broad purpose; state flexibility |
| Formula | Based on population/need criteria |
| Project | Competitive application process |
| Unfunded Mandate | Federal requirement without funding |

### Key Federalism Cases

- **McCulloch v. Maryland:** Implied powers; federal supremacy
- **United States v. Lopez:** Limits on Commerce Clause
- **Printz v. United States:** Cannot commandeer state officials

## üóΩ Part 5: Civil Liberties vs. Civil Rights

### Key Distinction

| Civil Liberties | Civil Rights |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Freedoms FROM government | Protections BY government |
| Bill of Rights | Equal Protection, voting rights |
| Individual rights | Group-based protections |
| Example: Free speech | Example: Anti-discrimination laws |

### Selective Incorporation

**Process:** Applying Bill of Rights to states through 14th Amendment Due Process Clause

**Key Incorporated Rights:**
| Amendment | Right | Case |
|-----------|-------|------|
| 1st | Free speech | Gitlow v. New York (1925) |
| 1st | Free press | Near v. Minnesota (1931) |
| 1st | Free exercise | Cantwell v. Connecticut (1940) |
| 1st | Establishment | Everson v. Board (1947) |

| 5th | Self-incrimination | Malloy v. Hogan (1964) |
| 6th | Right to counsel | Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) |
| 8th | Cruel & unusual | Robinson v. California (1962) |

### First Amendment Freedoms

**Religion:**
- **Establishment Clause:** No government endorsement of religion (Lemon test)
- **Free Exercise Clause:** Cannot burden religious practice (strict scrutiny)

**Speech:**
- **Protected:** Political speech, symbolic speech, commercial speech (limited)
- **Unprotected:** Obscenity, fighting words, true threats, incitement

**Press:**
- Prior restraint presumptively unconstitutional
- Libel: Public figures must prove actual malice (NY Times v. Sullivan)

**Assembly & Petition:**
- Time, place, manner restrictions allowed if content-neutral

### Equal Protection Analysis

**Three Levels of Scrutiny:**

| Level | Classification | Test | Example |
|-------|---------------|------|---------|
| Strict | Race, national origin, religion | Compelling interest + narrowly tailored | Affirmative action |
| Intermediate | Gender, legitimacy | Important objective + substantially related | VMI case |
| Rational Basis | Most classifications | Legitimate purpose + rationally related | Age, disability |

### Civil Rights Legislation

| Law | Year | Purpose |
|-----|------|---------|
| Civil Rights Act | 1964 | Banned discrimination in public accommodations, employment |
| Voting Rights Act | 1965 | Banned discriminatory voting practices; preclearance |
| Fair Housing Act | 1968 | Banned housing discrimination |

## ‚úçÔ∏è Part 9: FRQ Strategies

### Question Type 1: Concept Application (3 points)

**Format:** Given a scenario, apply political concepts

**Strategy:**
1. Read scenario carefully
2. Identify the concept being tested
3. Define the concept
4. Apply directly to the scenario
5. Explain the connection clearly

**Example Prompt:** A state passes a law requiring all voters to show photo ID...
- Identify the constitutional issue
- Explain how it affects voting rights
- Apply relevant precedent or principle

---

### Question Type 2: Quantitative Analysis (4 points)

**Format:** Analyze data (chart, graph, table)

**Strategy:**
1. Describe what you SEE in the data
2. Draw a conclusion supported by data
3. Explain political significance
4. Connect to broader concept

**Tips:**
- Use specific numbers from the data
- Compare trends or groups
- Explain cause/effect relationships

---

### Question Type 3: SCOTUS Comparison (4 points)

**Format:** Compare a non-required case to a required case

**Strategy:**
1. Describe the holding of the required case
2. Identify the constitutional principle
3. Explain similarities to the new case
4. Explain differences in facts or ruling
5. Discuss how precedent applies

**Template:**
```
In [Required Case], the Court held that [holding]. The constitutional 
principle established was [principle]. In [Given Case], a similar 
issue arose because [connection]. However, [difference/similarity] 
led to [outcome].
```

**Required Cases to Know Cold:**
- Marbury v. Madison (judicial review)
- McCulloch v. Maryland (federalism/implied powers)
- Brown v. Board (equal protection)
- Tinker v. Des Moines (student speech)
- Gideon v. Wainwright (right to counsel)

---

### Question Type 4: Argument Essay (6 points)

**Format:** Take a position on a political question

**Structure:**
1. **Thesis** (clearly states your position)
2. **Evidence 1** (from provided documents)
3. **Evidence 2** (from your knowledge)
4. **Opposing viewpoint** (acknowledge and refute)
5. **Conclusion** (reinforce thesis)

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Clear, defensible thesis (1 point)
- Use of foundational document evidence (1 point)
- Support thesis with relevant evidence (2 points)
- Respond to opposing perspective (1 point)
- Logical reasoning throughout (1 point)

**Tips:**
- Choose a clear side‚Äîdon't waffle
- Quote or reference specific documents
- Explain WHY evidence supports your point
- Acknowledge the other side, then counter it
- Use political science vocabulary

| Necessary and Proper | Congress can make laws needed to carry out powers |
