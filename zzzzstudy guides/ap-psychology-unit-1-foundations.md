| Method | Description | Strength | Weakness |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------|

**Goal**: Find relationships between variables

| Correlation | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Positive (+)** | Both variables move same direction | More study â†’ Higher grades |

## ğŸ¯ Confounding Variables

### What Can Go Wrong

| Threat | Definition | Solution |

## âœï¸ Sample Free Response

**Question**: A researcher wants to study whether caffeine improves reaction time.

(a) Identify the independent and dependent variables.

(b) Describe how the researcher could use random assignment.

(c) Explain why a double-blind procedure would be important.

(d) If the researcher found a correlation between caffeine consumption and reaction time, could causation be concluded? Explain.

**Answer**:

(a) **IV**: Caffeine (presence/absence or amount)
**DV**: Reaction time (measured in milliseconds)

(b) **Random assignment**: Participants would be randomly placed into either the caffeine group or control group. This could be done using a random number generator or drawing names from a hat. This ensures groups are equivalent before the study begins.

(c) **Double-blind**: Neither the participants nor researchers administering the test know who received caffeine. This prevents:
- Participants' expectations from affecting performance (placebo effect)
- Researchers' expectations from influencing how they record data (experimenter bias)

(d) **No causation from correlation**: Correlation only shows a relationship, not cause-and-effect. A third variable could explain both (e.g., motivation). Only a controlled experiment with random assignment can establish causation.
