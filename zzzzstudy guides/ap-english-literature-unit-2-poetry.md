## ğŸ“ Poetic Form

### Fixed Forms

| Form | Description | Structure |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Sonnet** | 14-line poem | Italian or English |
| **Villanelle** | 19-line poem with refrains | 5 tercets + quatrain; ABA rhyme |
| **Sestina** | 39-line poem | 6 stanzas of 6 lines; word repetition |
| **Haiku** | 17-syllable Japanese form | 5-7-5 syllables |
| **Ballad** | Narrative song form | Quatrains, ABAB or ABCB |
| **Ode** | Formal address to subject | Various structures |
| **Elegy** | Meditation on death | Various structures |
| **Epic** | Long narrative poem | Grand themes, heroic action |

### Sonnet Forms

| Type | Structure | Rhyme Scheme |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Italian (Petrarchan)** | Octave (8) + Sestet (6) | ABBAABBA + CDECDE (varies) |
| **English (Shakespearean)** | 3 Quatrains + Couplet | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG |
| **Spenserian** | 3 Quatrains + Couplet | ABAB BCBC CDCD EE |

### Sonnet Analysis

| Part | Italian | English | Function |
|------|---------|---------|----------|
| **Octave/Quatrains** | Problem, question | Develops argument | Sets up |
| **Volta (turn)** | After line 8 | Before couplet | Shift in thought |
| **Sestet/Couplet** | Response, resolution | Conclusion, twist | Resolves |

### Free Verse

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **No fixed meter** | Varies throughout |
| **No required rhyme** | May use occasional rhyme |
| **Organic form** | Shape follows content |
| **Line breaks** | Deliberate, meaningful |
| **White space** | Significant |

### Stanza Types

| Type | Lines | Common Use |
|------|-------|------------|
| **Couplet** | 2 | Epigrammatic, concluding |
| **Tercet** | 3 | Terza rima (Dante) |
| **Quatrain** | 4 | Most common; ballad, hymn |
| **Cinquain** | 5 | Various |
| **Sestet** | 6 | Part of sonnet |
| **Octave** | 8 | Part of sonnet |

## ğŸµ Sound and Rhythm

### Meter Basics

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Meter** | Pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables |
| **Foot** | Basic unit of meter |
| **Scansion** | Analyzing metrical pattern |

### Types of Feet

| Foot | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Iamb** | unstressed-STRESSED (u/) | be-HOLD, a-RISE |
| **Trochee** | STRESSED-unstressed (/u) | GAR-den, PO-et |
| **Anapest** | unstressed-unstressed-STRESSED (uu/) | in-ter-VENE |
| **Dactyl** | STRESSED-unstressed-unstressed (/uu) | MER-ri-ly |
| **Spondee** | STRESSED-STRESSED (//) | HEART-BREAK |
| **Pyrrhic** | unstressed-unstressed (uu) | (rare, transitional) |

### Line Lengths

| Name | Feet | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| **Monometer** | 1 | Very rare |
| **Dimeter** | 2 | Short, punchy |
| **Trimeter** | 3 | Hymns, songs |
| **Tetrameter** | 4 | Common in songs |
| **Pentameter** | 5 | Most common in English |
| **Hexameter** | 6 | Classical epic |
| **Heptameter** | 7 | Ballad meter (often 4+3) |

### Common Metrical Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Iambic pentameter** | 5 iambs | Shakespeare's sonnets, blank verse |
| **Trochaic tetrameter** | 4 trochees | "The Raven" by Poe |
| **Anapestic tetrameter** | 4 anapests | "'Twas the Night Before Christmas" |

### Rhyme Types

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Perfect/True rhyme** | Exact sound match | cat/hat, love/dove |
| **Slant/Near rhyme** | Close but not exact | prove/love, soul/all |
| **Eye rhyme** | Looks alike | love/move, cough/bough |
| **Internal rhyme** | Within a line | "We beat on, neat on" |
| **End rhyme** | At line ends | Most common |

### Rhyme Schemes

| Scheme | Pattern | Example Form |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| **ABAB** | Alternating | Shakespearean sonnet quatrain |
| **AABB** | Couplets | Heroic couplets |
| **ABBA** | Enclosed | Italian sonnet |
| **ABCABC** | Interlocking | Terza rima pattern |

### Sound Devices

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Alliteration** | Repeated consonant sounds | "Peter Piper picked" |
| **Assonance** | Repeated vowel sounds | "The rain in Spain" |
| **Consonance** | Repeated consonants (middle/end) | "stroke of luck" |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words imitating sounds | "buzz, hiss, splash" |
| **Euphony** | Pleasant, harmonious sounds | "Soft, silken, silver" |
| **Cacophony** | Harsh, discordant sounds | "Crackle, grating, jarring" |

## ğŸ“ Poetry Analysis Framework

### TPCASTT Method

| Letter | Step | Questions |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **T** | Title | What does it suggest before reading? |
| **P** | Paraphrase | What happens literally? |
| **C** | Connotation | What figurative language, imagery, sound devices? |
| **A** | Attitude | What is the tone? Speaker's attitude? |
| **S** | Shifts | Where does tone, focus, or speaker change? |
| **T** | Title (again) | What does it mean now? |
| **T** | Theme | What is the poem's central insight? |

### Close Reading Steps

| Step | Action | Focus |
|------|--------|-------|
| **1** | Read aloud | Hear the sounds |
| **2** | Paraphrase | Understand literally |
| **3** | Identify form | Structure, meter, rhyme |
| **4** | Analyze language | Imagery, figurative language |
| **5** | Consider speaker | Voice, audience, situation |
| **6** | Trace development | How poem moves |
| **7** | Interpret theme | Central meaning |

### Analysis Questions by Element

| Element | Questions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Form** | Why this structure? How does form relate to content? |
| **Sound** | What effects do rhythm and sound create? |
| **Imagery** | What senses are evoked? What patterns emerge? |
| **Figurative language** | What comparisons are made? What do they suggest? |
| **Speaker** | Who speaks? What is their situation? Attitude? |
| **Tone** | What is the attitude? Does it shift? |
| **Theme** | What ideas are explored? What insight is offered? |

## ğŸ“– Extended Passage Analysis Examples

### Example 1: Sonnet Analysis

**Poem: Shakespeare's Sonnet 130**
> "My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun;
> Coral is far more red than her lips' red;
> If snow be white, why then her breasts are dun;
> If hairs be wires, black wires grow on her head."

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Form** | English sonnet, iambic pentameter | Traditional form with subversive content |
| **Anti-Petrarchan** | Rejects conventional blazon | Critiques false praise in love poetry |
| **Irony** | Seems insulting, is loving | Authentic love vs. poetic convention |
| **Volta** | "And yet, by heaven, I think my love as rare" | Shift reveals sincerity |

**Sample thesis:** Shakespeare's Sonnet 130 employs the blazon tradition only to subvert it, using ironic negations to argue that genuine love transcends the ornate falsifications of conventional love poetry.

### Example 2: Romantic Ode Analysis

**Poem: Keats's "Ode on a Grecian Urn" (excerpt)**
> "Thou still unravish'd bride of quietness,
> Thou foster-child of silence and slow time,
> Sylvan historian, who canst thus express
> A flowery tale more sweetly than our rhyme"

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Apostrophe** | Addresses the urn directly | Elevates object to conversational partner |
| **Paradox** | "Unravish'd bride," "foster-child" | Urn's eternal, frozen state |
| **Personification** | Urn as historian, bride, child | Multiple identities explore art's nature |
| **Sound** | "Silence and slow time" | Sibilance creates hushed tone |

**Sample thesis:** Keats's ode explores the paradox of artistic permanence through personification of the urn as simultaneously bride, child, and historian, suggesting that art achieves immortality by sacrificing the dynamic reality it represents.

### Example 3: Modern Poetry Analysis

**Poem: Langston Hughes's "Harlem"**
> "What happens to a dream deferred?
> Does it dry up
> like a raisin in the sun?
> Or fester like a soreâ€”
> And then run?"

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Central question** | Opens with question | Engages reader, establishes theme |
| **Similes** | Raisin, sore, meat, sweet | Sensory, visceral comparisons |
| **Progression** | Increasingly violent images | Building tension toward explosion |
| **Form** | Short lines, free verse | Jazz-influenced rhythm |

**Sample thesis:** Hughes's "Harlem" uses a sequence of increasingly disturbing similes to transform abstract "deferred dreams" into visceral sensory experiences, culminating in the explosive image that suggests suppressed aspirations become revolutionary force.

## ğŸ“Š FRQ Rubric Breakdown for Poetry

### Row A: Thesis (0-1 point)

| Earning the Point | NOT Earning the Point |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| Interprets poem's meaning or theme | Restates prompt without position |
| Connects techniques to meaning | Lists devices without argument |
| Specific and arguable | Vague or obvious |

**Example strong thesis:** "Dickinson's irregular meter and slant rhyme in 'Because I could not stop for Death' create a deliberately unsettled reading experience that mirrors the speaker's ambivalent relationship to mortality."

**Example weak thesis:** "Dickinson uses literary devices like personification and imagery to describe death."

### Row B: Evidence and Commentary (0-4 points)

| Score | Characteristics |

### Poetry Analysis Essay Structure

**Introduction (8-10%):**
- Hook engaging with poem's theme or central tension
- Brief context (poet, title, relevant background)
- Thesis connecting techniques to meaning

**Body Paragraph Template:**
1. Topic sentence (specific claim about technique)
2. Brief context for quoted lines
3. Quoted evidence (properly formatted)
4. Analysis of HOW technique creates meaning
5. Connection to thesis
6. Transition

**Conclusion (8-10%):**
- Restate thesis with new insight
- Synthesize analysis
- Broader significance of the poem's achievement

### Comparative Poetry Essay Structure

**Introduction:**
- Introduce both poems and shared concern
- Thesis identifying similarity AND difference

**Body Option 1: Alternating**
- Para 1: Poem A on element 1
- Para 2: Poem B on element 1
- Para 3: Poem A on element 2
- Para 4: Poem B on element 2

**Body Option 2: Block**
- Para 1-2: Poem A analysis
- Para 3-4: Poem B analysis
- Para 5: Synthesis and comparison
