# üö© Road to Revolution

## üí∞ The British Debt Crisis

After the French and Indian War, Britain needed money! üí∑

### The Problem

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| War debt | ¬£150 million (doubled national debt) |
| New territory | Required defense (10,000 troops) |
| Colonial contribution | Colonists paid ~1/26 of British taxes |
| British view | Colonies should help pay |

### The Colonial View

| Argument | Basis |
|----------|-------|
| "No taxation without representation" | Not represented in Parliament |
| Local assemblies | Should tax themselves |
| Virtual representation | Rejected British argument |
| Rights of Englishmen | Protected from arbitrary taxation |

---

## üìã British Taxation Policies

### Sugar Act (1764)

| Provision | Colonial Response |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Lowered molasses tax | But enforced it! |
| Expanded customs | Vice-admiralty courts |
| Affected merchants | Protests in port cities |
| First revenue tax | Distinguished from trade regulation |

### Currency Act (1764)

| Provision | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Banned colonial paper money | Economic hardship |
| Required hard currency | Scarce in colonies |
| Hurt debtors | Couldn't pay debts |

---

## üìú The Stamp Act (1765)

### What It Required

| Requirement | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| Tax on printed materials | Newspapers, legal documents, playing cards |
| Stamp required | Visible proof of payment |
| Affected everyone | Lawyers, merchants, printers, colonists |
| First direct tax | On colonists, not trade |

### Colonial Response

| Response | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Stamp Act Congress | 9 colonies met in New York |
| Sons of Liberty | Secret organization, protests |
| Boycotts | Non-importation agreements |
| Violence | Attacked stamp distributors |

### Key Arguments

| Colonists Said | British Said |
|----------------|--------------|
| No taxation without representation | Virtual representation exists |
| Only assemblies can tax | Parliament is supreme |
| Rights of Englishmen | Colonists owe obedience |

### Outcome

| Result | Significance |
|--------|--------------|
| Repealed (1766) | Colonies won this round |
| Declaratory Act | Parliament asserted full authority |
| Precedent | Colonial resistance works |

---

## üçµ Townshend Acts (1767)

### Provisions

| Provision | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| Import duties | Tea, glass, paint, paper, lead |
| Revenue used | Pay colonial governors (remove local control) |
| New enforcement | Writs of assistance (search warrants) |
| Board of Customs | In Boston |

### Colonial Response

| Response | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Boycotts renewed | Non-importation |
| Letters from a Farmer | John Dickinson's arguments |
| Massachusetts Circular Letter | Sam Adams coordinated resistance |
| Partial repeal | All duties except tea (1770) |

## ‚úçÔ∏è Sample Short Answer Questions

### SAQ Example 1

**Briefly explain ONE way the French and Indian War contributed to the American Revolution.**

*Sample Answer:*
"The French and Indian War contributed to the American Revolution by leaving Britain with massive war debt that led Parliament to impose new taxes on the colonies, such as the Stamp Act (1765). Colonists resisted these taxes with the argument of 'no taxation without representation,' beginning a cycle of British policies and colonial resistance that escalated to revolution."

### SAQ Example 2

**Briefly explain ONE continuity AND ONE change in the concept of liberty between the colonial period and the early republic (1790s).**

*Sample Answer:*
"A continuity was the commitment to representative government: colonists demanded representation in any body that taxed them, and the Constitution established a republic with elected representatives. A change was the expansion of who could participate: while colonial assemblies often required substantial property ownership to vote, many states reduced property requirements during and after the Revolution, broadening (though not universalizing) political participation."
