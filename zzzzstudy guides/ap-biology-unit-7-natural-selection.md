## ğŸŒ± 7.2 Natural Selection

### Darwin's Observations ğŸ‘€

1. **Variation** exists within populations
2. Organisms produce **more offspring** than can survive
3. Resources are **limited** (struggle for existence)
4. Individuals with advantageous traits are more likely to **survive and reproduce**
5. Traits are **heritable**

### Requirements for Natural Selection ğŸ“‹

| Requirement | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Variation** | Differences in traits among individuals |
| **Heritability** | Traits can be passed to offspring |
| **Differential reproduction** | Some variants leave more offspring |

$$\text{Evolution by natural selection} = \text{Variation} + \text{Heredity} + \text{Selection}$$

### Directional Selection

- Environment changes or new environment colonized
- Shifts mean phenotype in one direction
- Example: Antibiotic resistance, industrial melanism

### Stabilizing Selection

- Maintains current average
- Reduces variation
- Example: Human birth weight (too small or large = problems)

### Disruptive (Diversifying) Selection

- Favors extreme phenotypes
- Disfavors intermediate
- Can lead to speciation
- Example: Beak size in African seed crackers

### Sexual Selection ğŸ’•

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Intersexual** | Mate choice | Peacock tail display |
| **Intrasexual** | Same-sex competition | Deer antler combat |

Can produce traits that **reduce survival** but **increase mating success**

## ğŸ§® 7.3 Population Genetics

### The Gene Pool ğŸŠ

All alleles in a population at a given time:

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Allele frequency** | Proportion of an allele in population |
| **Genotype frequency** | Proportion of a genotype in population |
| **Gene pool** | Total genetic diversity |

### Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium ğŸ“

Population NOT evolving if:

$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$
$$p + q = 1$$

Where:
- $p$ = frequency of dominant allele
- $q$ = frequency of recessive allele
- $p^2$ = frequency of homozygous dominant
- $2pq$ = frequency of heterozygotes
- $q^2$ = frequency of homozygous recessive

### Hardy-Weinberg Conditions

For equilibrium (NO evolution):

| Condition | Violation = |
|-----------|-------------|
| **No mutation** | Introduces new alleles |
| **Random mating** | Sexual selection |
| **No natural selection** | Differential reproduction |
| **Large population** | Genetic drift |
| **No gene flow** | Migration changes allele frequencies |

**Key insight**: These conditions are NEVER fully met in nature, so populations always evolve!

### Using Hardy-Weinberg ğŸ”¢

**Example Problem**:
In a population, 16% show recessive phenotype. Find allele frequencies.

**Solution**:
1. $q^2 = 0.16$ (frequency of homozygous recessive)
2. $q = \sqrt{0.16} = 0.4$
3. $p = 1 - q = 1 - 0.4 = 0.6$
4. Heterozygote frequency: $2pq = 2(0.6)(0.4) = 0.48 = 48\%$

## ğŸ² 7.4 Mechanisms of Evolution

### Five Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Effect on Variation | Effect on Fitness |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Mutation** | Introduces new alleles | Random (usually neutral) |
| **Gene flow** | Increases (between pops) | Equalizes frequencies |
| **Genetic drift** | Reduces | Random |
| **Natural selection** | Depends on type | Increases fitness |
| **Sexual selection** | Variable | Increases mating success |

### Mutation ğŸ§¬

- Ultimate source of genetic variation
- Most are neutral or harmful
- Rare beneficial mutations are raw material for selection

$$\text{Mutation rate} \approx 10^{-9} \text{ per nucleotide per generation}$$
