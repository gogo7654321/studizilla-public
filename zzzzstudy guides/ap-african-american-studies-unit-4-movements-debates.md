## üéØ Sub-Theme 1: Origins of the Modern Civil Rights Movement

### World War II and Double V

```
CONTEXT:

DOUBLE V CAMPAIGN:
- Victory abroad (against fascism)
- Victory at home (against racism)
- Pittsburgh Courier campaign
- Fighting for democracy abroad
  while denied it at home

BLACK MILITARY SERVICE:
- Over 1 million served
- Segregated units
- Tuskegee Airmen
- 761st Tank Battalion
- Returned expecting change

WARTIME DEVELOPMENTS:
- A. Philip Randolph's March threat
- Executive Order 8802 (1941)
- Fair Employment Practice Committee
- Defense industry integration
- Some progress, much resistance
```

### Post-War Momentum

```
KEY DEVELOPMENTS:

JACKIE ROBINSON (1947):
- Broke baseball's color line
- Brooklyn Dodgers
- Faced tremendous abuse
- Succeeded brilliantly
- National symbol

INTEGRATION OF MILITARY (1948):
- Executive Order 9981
- Truman administration
- Desegregated armed forces
- Slow implementation
- Completed in Korean War

NAACP LEGAL STRATEGY:
- Thurgood Marshall
- Charles Hamilton Houston
- "Separate is inherently unequal"
- Building toward Brown
- Careful case selection
```

### Brown v. Board of Education

```
THE CASE (1954):

BACKGROUND:
- Consolidated cases from 5 states
- NAACP Legal Defense Fund
- Thurgood Marshall lead attorney
- Kenneth and Mamie Clark doll studies

DECISION:
- Unanimous 9-0
- Chief Justice Earl Warren
- "Separate educational facilities
  are inherently unequal"
- Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson

BROWN II (1955):
- Implementation decision
- "With all deliberate speed"
- Allowed delay
- Massive resistance followed

IMPACT:
- Legal segregation unconstitutional
- Energized movement
- White backlash intense
- Implementation took decades
```

### Martin Luther King Jr.

```
PHILOSOPHY AND APPROACH:

NONVIOLENT DIRECT ACTION:
- Influenced by Gandhi
- Christian theology
- Love your enemy
- Redemptive suffering
- Appeal to conscience

SCLC (founded 1957):
- Southern Christian Leadership Conference
- Church-based organizing
- King as president
- Regional campaigns

KEY SPEECHES:
- "I Have a Dream" (1963)
- "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (1963)
- "Beyond Vietnam" (1967)
- "I've Been to the Mountaintop" (1968)

EVOLUTION:

- Economic justice emphasis
- Radical by end of life
```

### Sit-ins and Freedom Rides

```
SIT-INS (1960):

GREENSBORO, NC (Feb 1, 1960):
- Four A&T students
- Woolworth's lunch counter
- Sat, refused to leave
- Returned day after day

SPREAD:
- Within weeks: 50+ cities
- Thousands of students
- SNCC formed (April 1960)
- Youth energy unleashed

TACTICS:
- Sit quietly, peacefully
- Accept violence without retaliation
- Fill jails
- Economic pressure
- Media attention

FREEDOM RIDES (1961):

PURPOSE:
- Test integration of interstate travel
- Boynton v. Virginia (1960)
- CORE organized
- Interracial groups

VIOLENCE:
- Buses firebombed (Anniston, AL)
- Riders beaten (Birmingham, Montgomery)
- National attention
- Federal response forced
```

KEY FIGURES:

- "Strong people don't need strong leaders"
- Mentored young activists

JOHN LEWIS (1940-2020):
- SNCC chairman
- Freedom Rider
- March on Washington speaker
- Later congressman
- "Good trouble"

DIANE NASH (b. 1938):
- Nashville movement leader
- Freedom Rides organizer
- Fearless strategist

FANNIE LOU HAMER (1917-1977):
- Mississippi sharecropper
- SNCC field secretary
- "I'm sick and tired of being sick and tired"
- MFDP challenge
```

## üìú Sub-Theme 3: Major Campaigns and Victories

### Birmingham Campaign (1963)

```
PROJECT C (CONFRONTATION):

STRATEGY:
- Target Birmingham, AL
- "Most segregated city in America"
- Bull Connor as opponent
- Economic boycott + demonstrations

CHILDREN'S CRUSADE:
- May 2-5, 1963
- Thousands of children marched
- Police used dogs, fire hoses
- Shocking images worldwide
- Filled the jails

"LETTER FROM BIRMINGHAM JAIL":
- King's response to white moderates
- "Injustice anywhere is a threat
  to justice everywhere"
- Moral urgency
- Classic statement

OUTCOME:
- Some desegregation agreement
- 16th Street Church bombing (4 girls killed)
- Pushed federal action
- Kennedy announces civil rights bill
```

### March on Washington (1963)

```
AUGUST 28, 1963:

ORGANIZATION:
- A. Philip Randolph's vision
- Bayard Rustin organized logistics
- Coalition of civil rights groups
- 250,000+ participants

DEMANDS:
- Passage of civil rights bill
- Desegregation of schools
- Job programs
- Fair employment
- Voting rights

"I HAVE A DREAM":
- King's famous speech
- American ideals vs. reality
- Vision of beloved community
- "Let freedom ring"

SIGNIFICANCE:
- Largest demonstration in US history (then)
- Pressure for legislation
- Iconic moment
- But work continued
```

### Freedom Summer and Selma

```
FREEDOM SUMMER (1964):

MISSISSIPPI PROJECT:
- SNCC and COFO organized
- Voter registration in most dangerous state
- 1,000+ volunteers (many white students)
- Freedom Schools

MURDERS:
- James Chaney, Andrew Goodman,
  Michael Schwerner killed
- Klan with police involvement
- National outrage
- FBI investigation

MFDP CHALLENGE:
- Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party
- Challenged all-white delegation
- Atlantic City convention
- Rejected‚Äî2 seats offered
- Radicalized many activists

SELMA TO MONTGOMERY (1965):

BLOODY SUNDAY (March 7, 1965):
- Marchers attacked on Edmund Pettus Bridge
- State troopers, tear gas, clubs
- John Lewis skull fractured
- Televised nationally

RESPONSE:
- National outrage
- LBJ addresses Congress
- "We shall overcome"
- Voting Rights Act passed
```

### Legislative Victories

```
CIVIL RIGHTS ACT OF 1964:

PROVISIONS:
- Outlawed segregation in public places
- Banned employment discrimination
- Established EEOC
- Strengthened voting rights enforcement
- Title VI: federal funding

VOTING RIGHTS ACT OF 1965:

PROVISIONS:
- Outlawed literacy tests
- Federal registrars in South
- Preclearance requirement (Section 5)
- Black registration soared
- "Most effective civil rights law ever"

FAIR HOUSING ACT OF 1968:

PROVISIONS:
- Outlawed housing discrimination
- Passed after King assassination
- Weak enforcement mechanisms
- Residential segregation continued
```

### Emergence of Black Power

```
CONTEXT:

FRUSTRATIONS:
- Slow pace of change
- Northern urban conditions ignored
- Continued poverty
- Police brutality
- Nonviolence questioned

STOKELY CARMICHAEL (1941-1998):
- SNCC chairman (1966)

MEANING OF BLACK POWER:
- Self-definition
- Self-determination
- Economic power
- Political power
- Cultural pride
- Varied interpretations
```

### Black Panther Party

```
FOUNDED: October 1966
LOCATION: Oakland, California
FOUNDERS: Huey Newton, Bobby Seale

TEN-POINT PROGRAM:
1. Freedom and self-determination
2. Full employment
3. End to exploitation
4. Decent housing
5. Education (true history)
6. Exemption from military service
7. End to police brutality
8. Freedom for prisoners
9. Juries of peers
10. Land, bread, housing, education,
    clothing, justice, peace

PROGRAMS:
- Free Breakfast for Children
- Health clinics
- Liberation schools
- Community patrols
- Newspaper (250,000 circulation)

GOVERNMENT RESPONSE:
- FBI COINTELPRO targeting
- Police raids
- Leaders killed or imprisoned
- Fred Hampton assassinated (1969)
```

### Barack Obama

```
SIGNIFICANCE:

2008 ELECTION:
- First Black president
- Historic moment
- 53% of popular vote
- Coalition of diverse voters

PRESIDENCY:
- ACA (Affordable Care Act)
- Economic recovery from recession
- Symbolic importance immense
- Faced intense opposition

DEBATES:
- "Post-racial" America? No.
- Did not end racism
- Some argued made it worse
- Inspired Black achievement
- Also inspired backlash

LEGACY:
- Representation matters
- Limits of individual achievement
- Structural racism persists
- Energized both hope and hate
