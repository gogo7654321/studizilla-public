## ÔøΩüìã Unit Overview

Civil liberties and civil rights are fundamental to American democracy. Civil liberties protect individuals from government power, while civil rights protect groups from discrimination. This unit explores how the Constitution protects freedoms, how the Supreme Court has interpreted these protections, and how groups have fought for equality! üóΩ

### Essential Questions

| Question | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| What are civil liberties? | Protections from government |
| How does incorporation work? | Applying Bill of Rights to states |
| What does the First Amendment protect? | Religion; speech; press; assembly |
| What are due process rights? | Fair legal procedures |
| How have civil rights expanded? | Equal protection; movements |

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Civil liberties** | Freedoms from government |
| **Civil rights** | Equal treatment |
| **Incorporation** | Applying Bill of Rights to states |
| **Due process** | Fair procedures |
| **Equal protection** | No discrimination |

## üóΩ Bill of Rights

### Purpose and History

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Adoption** | 1791 |
| **Purpose** | Protect individual liberties |
| **Origin** | Anti-Federalist demand |
| **Scope** | Originally limited federal government only |

### The First Ten Amendments

| Amendment | Protection |
|-----------|------------|
| **1st** | Religion, speech, press, assembly, petition |
| **2nd** | Right to bear arms |
| **3rd** | No quartering of soldiers |
| **4th** | No unreasonable searches and seizures |
| **5th** | Grand jury, double jeopardy, self-incrimination, due process, takings |
| **6th** | Speedy trial, jury, counsel, confrontation |
| **7th** | Civil jury trial |
| **8th** | No excessive bail, cruel and unusual punishment |

| **10th** | Powers reserved to states |

## üîó Incorporation Doctrine

### Before Incorporation

| Era | Application |
|-----|-------------|
| **1791-1868** | Bill of Rights limited federal government |
| **Barron v. Baltimore (1833)** | Bill of Rights does NOT apply to states |
| **State violations** | Only state constitutions protected |

### 14th Amendment (1868)

| Clause | Text |
|--------|------|
| **Citizenship** | Born or naturalized |
| **Privileges and Immunities** | State can't abridge |
| **Due Process** | States can't deprive without due process |
| **Equal Protection** | Equal protection of laws |

### Incorporation Process

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Selective incorporation** | Rights applied case by case |
| **Vehicle** | 14th Amendment Due Process |
| **Method** | Supreme Court decisions |
| **Result** | Most Bill of Rights now applies to states |

### Key Incorporation Cases

| Right | Case | Year |
|-------|------|------|
| **Free speech** | Gitlow v. New York | 1925 |
| **Free press** | Near v. Minnesota | 1931 |
| **Assembly** | DeJonge v. Oregon | 1937 |
| **Free exercise** | Cantwell v. Connecticut | 1940 |
| **Establishment** | Everson v. Board | 1947 |
| **Counsel (felony)** | Gideon v. Wainwright | 1963 |
| **Self-incrimination** | Malloy v. Hogan | 1964 |
| **Cruel punishment** | Robinson v. California | 1962 |
| **Privacy** | Griswold v. Connecticut | 1965 |
| **Bear arms** | McDonald v. Chicago | 2010 |

### Not Fully Incorporated

| Right | Status |
|-------|--------|
| **Grand jury (5th)** | Not incorporated |
| **Civil jury (7th)** | Not incorporated |
| **Excessive bail (8th)** | Partially |

## ‚úùÔ∏è First Amendment: Religion

### Two Religion Clauses

| Clause | Protection |
|--------|------------|
| **Establishment Clause** | Government can't establish religion |
| **Free Exercise Clause** | Government can't prohibit practice |

### Establishment Clause

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Wall of separation** | Jefferson's metaphor |
| **Neutrality** | Government neither promotes nor inhibits |
| **No endorsement** | Can't favor or disfavor religion |

### Lemon Test (Lemon v. Kurtzman, 1971)

| Prong | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|
| **Purpose** | Must have secular purpose |
| **Effect** | Neither advance nor inhibit religion |
| **Entanglement** | No excessive government-religion involvement |

### Key Establishment Cases

| Case | Issue | Ruling |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Engel v. Vitale (1962)** | School prayer | Unconstitutional |
| **Abington v. Schempp (1963)** | Bible reading | Unconstitutional |
| **Lemon v. Kurtzman (1971)** | Aid to religious schools | Created test |
| **Lee v. Weisman (1992)** | Graduation prayer | Unconstitutional |
| **Santa Fe v. Doe (2000)** | Football prayer | Unconstitutional |
| **Town of Greece v. Galloway (2014)** | Legislative prayer | Constitutional |

### Engel v. Vitale (1962)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | NY required school prayer |
| **Question** | Violates Establishment Clause? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; government can't compose prayers |
| **Significance** | No official school prayer |

### Free Exercise Clause

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Protection** | Believe and practice |
| **Limits** | Generally applicable laws may restrict practice |
| **Compelling interest** | Government needs strong reason to limit |

### Key Free Exercise Cases

| Case | Issue | Ruling |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Reynolds v. US (1879)** | Polygamy | Practice can be limited |
| **Sherbert v. Verner (1963)** | Unemployment for Sabbath | Must accommodate |
| **Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)** | Amish schooling | Religious exemption |
| **Employment Division v. Smith (1990)** | Peyote use | Neutral laws can restrict |

### Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Amish refused high school |
| **Question** | Religious exemption from compulsory education? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; burden on religion outweighs state interest |
| **Significance** | Religious practice can override some laws |

## üó£Ô∏è First Amendment: Speech

### Types of Protected Speech

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Political** | Criticism of government |
| **Symbolic** | Wearing armbands; flag burning |
| **Commercial** | Advertising (less protected) |
| **Artistic** | Art; music; literature |

### Unprotected Speech

| Type | Reason |
|------|--------|
| **Obscenity** | Miller test |
| **Fighting words** | Provoke violence |
| **True threats** | Specific threats |
| **Defamation** | False statements harming reputation |
| **Incitement** | Imminent lawless action |
| **Child pornography** | Harm to children |

### Limits on Speech

| Standard | Source |
|----------|--------|
| **Clear and present danger** | Schenck v. US (1919) |
| **Imminent lawless action** | Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969) |
| **Time, place, manner** | Regulations allowed |

### Key Speech Cases

| Case | Issue | Ruling |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Schenck v. US (1919)** | Anti-draft pamphlets | "Clear and present danger" |
| **Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969)** | KKK speech | "Imminent lawless action" test |
| **Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)** | Student armbands | Students retain rights |
| **Texas v. Johnson (1989)** | Flag burning | Protected symbolic speech |
| **Morse v. Frederick (2007)** | "Bong Hits 4 Jesus" | School can limit drug speech |

### Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Students wore black armbands protesting Vietnam |
| **Question** | Can schools ban symbolic speech? |
| **Ruling** | No; students don't "shed rights at schoolhouse gate" |
| **Standard** | Must show "substantial disruption" |
| **Significance** | Student speech protected (with limits) |

### Schenck v. United States (1919)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Distributed anti-draft pamphlets |
| **Question** | Protected by First Amendment? |
| **Ruling** | No; "clear and present danger" |
| **Significance** | Speech can be limited in certain circumstances |

### New York Times v. United States (1971)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Pentagon Papers publication |
| **Question** | Could government block publication? |
| **Ruling** | No prior restraint; heavy presumption against |
| **Significance** | Press freedom from prior censorship |

### Fourth Amendment

| Protection | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Search and seizure** | Must be reasonable |

### Fifth Amendment

| Protection | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Grand jury** | Federal felony indictment |
| **Double jeopardy** | Can't try twice for same offense |
| **Self-incrimination** | Right to remain silent |
| **Due process** | Fair procedures |
| **Takings** | Just compensation for property |

### Sixth Amendment

| Protection | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Speedy trial** | No excessive delay |
| **Public trial** | Open proceedings |

### Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Gideon denied lawyer in felony case |

| **Ruling** | Yes; 6th Amendment incorporated |

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Confession without warning of rights |
| **Question** | Must police inform of rights? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; "Miranda warnings" required |
| **Warnings** | Right to silence; right to attorney |

### Eighth Amendment

| Protection | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Excessive bail** | Bail must be reasonable |

## üîí Right to Privacy

### Constitutional Basis

| Source | Connection |
|--------|------------|
| **Not explicit** | Not in Constitution text |
| **Derived from** | 1st, 3rd, 4th, 5th, 9th Amendments |
| **"Penumbras"** | Zones of privacy |

### Griswold v. Connecticut (1965)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Law banned contraception |
| **Question** | Constitutional right to privacy? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; marital privacy protected |
| **Significance** | Established privacy right |

### Roe v. Wade (1973)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Texas abortion ban |
| **Question** | Constitutional right to abortion? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; within privacy right (trimester framework) |
| **Later** | Overruled by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) |

### McDonald v. Chicago (2010)

| Issue | Ruling |
|-------|--------|
| **Facts** | Chicago handgun ban |
| **Question** | Does 2nd Amendment apply to states? |
| **Ruling** | Yes; incorporated through 14th Amendment |
| **Significance** | Second Amendment incorporated |

## üåà Civil Rights

### Definition

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Civil rights** | Equal treatment under law |
| **Based on** | 14th Amendment Equal Protection |
| **Protected classes** | Race, gender, religion, etc. |

### 14th Amendment Equal Protection

| Clause | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| **Text** | "Equal protection of the laws" |
| **Originally** | For freed slaves |
| **Expanded** | To many groups |
| **Standards** | Different levels of scrutiny |

### Levels of Scrutiny

| Level | Applied To | Test |
|-------|------------|------|
| **Strict scrutiny** | Race; religion; national origin | Compelling interest; narrowly tailored |
| **Intermediate** | Gender | Substantially related to important interest |
| **Rational basis** | Other classifications | Rationally related to legitimate interest |

|-------|--------|
