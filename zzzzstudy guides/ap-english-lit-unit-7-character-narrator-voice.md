## üéØ Sub-Theme 1: Character Construction

### Methods of Characterization

```
DIRECT CHARACTERIZATION:
Author explicitly tells us about character

"Tom was a cruel man who cared nothing 
for others' feelings."

Advantages:
- Clear, unambiguous
- Efficient

Limitations:
- Less engaging
- Tells rather than shows


INDIRECT CHARACTERIZATION (STEAL):

S - SPEECH:
    What/how character speaks
    Dialect, vocabulary, topics

T - THOUGHTS:
    Internal monologue
    Desires, fears, beliefs

E - EFFECTS ON OTHERS:
    How others react
    Reputation, relationships

A - ACTIONS:
    What character does
    Choices, behaviors

L - LOOKS:
    Physical appearance
    Clothing, expression
```

### Character Complexity

```
E.M. FORSTER'S CATEGORIES:

FLAT CHARACTERS:
- Built around single idea/quality
- Recognizable type
- Don't surprise us
- "Humorous" characters
- Often minor roles

Example: Mrs. Micawber
(always believes something will "turn up")

ROUND CHARACTERS:
- Complex, multi-faceted
- Capable of surprising us
- Change and develop
- Psychologically realistic
- Usually protagonists

Example: Elizabeth Bennet
(evolves from pride to self-knowledge)


STATIC vs. DYNAMIC:

STATIC:
- Remain essentially unchanged
- Not necessarily flat
- Can be complex but consistent

DYNAMIC:
- Undergo significant change
- Learn or transform
- Usually protagonists
```

### Character Functions

```
PROTAGONIST:
Central character; story focuses on them
- May be hero or antihero
- Pursues goals
- Faces obstacles

ANTAGONIST:
Opposes protagonist
- Not always evil
- Can be person, force, self
- Creates conflict

FOIL:
Contrasts with another character
- Highlights qualities through difference
- Same situation, different response
- Illuminates protagonist

CONFIDANT(E):
Character who hears others' thoughts
- Watson to Holmes
- Reader gains access to protagonist
- Vehicle for exposition

STOCK/ARCHETYPE:
Conventional character type
- The wise mentor
- The innocent
- The trickster
- Cultural familiarity
```

## üó£Ô∏è Sub-Theme 2: Understanding Narrators

### Narrator vs. Author

```
CRITICAL DISTINCTION:

AUTHOR:
- Real person who wrote the text
- Has biographical existence
- Makes artistic choices
- Creates narrator

NARRATOR:
- Voice telling the story
- May be character or not
- Created by author
- Never simply "is" the author

Even in autobiography/memoir,
the "I" on the page is constructed.

IMPLIED AUTHOR:
- The "author" we construct from text
- Values and sensibility we infer
- May differ from real author
- "The author this text suggests"
```

### Types of Narrators

```
PARTICIPANT NARRATORS:

PROTAGONIST NARRATOR:
Tells their own story
- Jane Eyre, Great Expectations
- Most intimate access
- Limited to their knowledge

WITNESS NARRATOR:
Tells another's story
- Nick Carraway (Gatsby)
- Dr. Watson (Sherlock Holmes)
- Some distance from action
- Can observe protagonist

PERIPHERAL NARRATOR:
Minor participant
- Nelly Dean (Wuthering Heights)
- Limited direct involvement
- Observational role


NON-PARTICIPANT NARRATORS:

OMNISCIENT:
All-knowing, all-seeing
- Access to all minds
- Can move through time/space
- God-like perspective
- Victorian novels often

LIMITED OMNISCIENT:
Focused on one character's mind
- Third person but close
- Like camera following one person
- Free indirect discourse
- Modern novels often

OBJECTIVE/DRAMATIC:
Reports only external action
- No mind access
- Like camera
- Hemingway's style
- "Hills Like White Elephants"
```

### Focalization

```
WHO SEES vs. WHO SPEAKS:

NARRATOR:
The voice telling the story
WHO SPEAKS

FOCALIZER:
The perspective through which we see
WHO SEES

These can differ!

Example:
"Emma looked at Mr. Knightley. He seemed 
distressed, though she couldn't imagine why."

NARRATOR: Third person (author-like)
FOCALIZER: Emma (we see through her eyes)

TYPES OF FOCALIZATION:

ZERO FOCALIZATION:
Narrator knows more than characters
Omniscient

INTERNAL FOCALIZATION:
Narrator knows what one character knows
Limited

EXTERNAL FOCALIZATION:
Narrator knows less than characters
Objective, dramatic
```

## üîÆ Sub-Theme 3: Unreliable Narration

### What Makes a Narrator Unreliable?

```
UNRELIABLE NARRATOR:
One whose account we cannot fully trust

CAUSES OF UNRELIABILITY:

1. LIMITED UNDERSTANDING:
   - Youth/naivety
   - Lack of information
   - Cultural blindness
   Example: Huck Finn

2. PSYCHOLOGICAL INSTABILITY:
   - Madness
   - Trauma
   - Delusion
   Example: Poe's narrators

3. SELF-DECEPTION:
   - Denial
   - Rationalization
   - Ego protection
   Example: Stevens (Remains of the Day)

4. DELIBERATE DECEPTION:
   - Manipulation
   - Protecting self
   - Controlling narrative
   Example: Gone Girl narrators

5. IMPAIRED PERCEPTION:
   - Intoxication
   - Memory issues
   - Physical limitation
   Example: various
```

### Detecting Unreliability

```
SIGNALS OF UNRELIABILITY:

TEXTUAL CLUES:
- Contradictions in narrative
- Excessive self-justification
- Gaps and omissions
- Overemphasis on certain details
- Protest too much

CONTEXTUAL CLUES:
- Others' reactions differ from narrator's account
- Historical/factual inaccuracies
- Implausible events presented as normal
- Genre expectations

READER RESPONSE:
- Discomfort with narrator's views
- Sense that something is "off"
- Information that doesn't add up
- Sympathy withheld

IMPLICIT AUTHOR'S SIGNALS:
- Irony and distance
- Symbolic elements
- Structure revealing truth
- Alternative perspectives
```

### Functions of Unreliability

```
WHY USE UNRELIABLE NARRATORS?

1. PSYCHOLOGICAL REALISM:
   - All perception is limited
   - Everyone self-deceives
   - More honest about subjectivity

2. READER ENGAGEMENT:
   - Reader becomes detective
   - Active interpretation required
   - More engaging than passive reception

3. IRONIC DISTANCE:
   - Author can critique narrator
   - Reader sees more than narrator
   - Complex moral positioning

4. THEMATIC DEPTH:
   - Truth vs. perception
   - Self-knowledge themes
   - Power of narrative

5. DRAMATIC IRONY:
   - We know what narrator doesn't
   - Tension and suspense
   - Tragic effect possible
```

## üí¨ Sub-Theme 4: Voice in Literature

### What Is Voice?

```
VOICE = The distinctive way a text "sounds"

COMPONENTS OF VOICE:

DICTION:
- Word choice
- Vocabulary level
- Connotation

SYNTAX:
- Sentence structure
- Length and complexity
- Rhythm

TONE:
- Attitude toward subject
- Attitude toward reader
- Emotional coloring

REGISTER:
- Formal to informal
- Appropriate to situation
- Social positioning

IMAGERY:
- Characteristic figures
- Recurring patterns
- Sensory emphasis
```

### Narrator's Voice vs. Character's Voice

```
DISTINGUISHING VOICES:

In third-person narration:

NARRATOR'S VOICE:
The overarching narrative style
- Usually consistent
- Author's presence
- Frame of the story

CHARACTER'S VOICE:
How characters speak and think
- Distinct from narrator
- Individual qualities
- Revealed in dialogue, FID


FREE INDIRECT DISCOURSE:
Where voices blur

"She would not go to the party. It was 
absurd to expect her to go, when everyone 
knew how she had been treated."

Whose voice? Narrator or character?
BOTH - that's the point.

Effect:
- Intimacy with character
- Ironic distance possible
- Reader judges independently
```

### Analyzing Voice

```
QUESTIONS TO ASK:

1. WHOSE VOICE IS THIS?
   - Narrator or character?
   - First or third person?
   - Reliable or unreliable?

2. WHAT CHARACTERIZES IT?
   - Formal or informal?
   - Complex or simple syntax?
   - Specific vocabulary patterns?

3. WHAT'S THE TONE?
   - What attitude is conveyed?
   - Toward the subject?
   - Toward the reader?

4. HOW DOES IT CREATE EFFECT?
   - What do we feel reading it?
   - What perspective do we adopt?
   - How are we positioned?

5. DOES IT CHANGE?
   - Shifts in tone?
   - Different registers?
   - Development over text?
```

## üìä Sub-Theme 5: Dialogue and Speech

### Functions of Dialogue

```
DIALOGUE DOES MANY THINGS:

1. CHARACTERIZATION:
   - Reveals personality
   - Shows intelligence, emotion
   - Distinguishes characters

2. PLOT ADVANCEMENT:
   - Information conveyed
   - Decisions made
   - Conflict enacted

3. RELATIONSHIP DISPLAY:
   - Power dynamics
   - Intimacy levels
   - Tension and harmony

4. THEME EXPLORATION:
   - Ideas debated
   - Values revealed
   - Perspectives clash

5. PACING:
   - Creates immediacy
   - Varies rhythm
   - Dramatizes moments
```

### Analyzing Dialogue

```
WHAT TO EXAMINE:

WHAT is said:
- Content of speech
- Topics raised/avoided
- Information conveyed

HOW it's said:
- Vocabulary level
- Sentence structure
- Speech patterns

WHAT'S NOT said:
- Subtext
- Evasions
- Silences

WHO SPEAKS MORE:
- Power dynamics
- Who dominates?
- Who is silenced?

HOW THEY RESPOND:
- Direct answers?
- Evasions?
- Topic changes?
```

### Dialect and Sociolect

```
DIALECT:
Regional speech variation
- Phonetic spelling
- Grammar differences
- Vocabulary

Example:
"I ain't got no learnin'."
vs.
"I haven't received an education."

SOCIOLECT:
Social group speech patterns
- Class markers
- Professional jargon
- Generational language

FUNCTIONS:
- Characterization
- Social positioning
- Authenticity
- Cultural commentary

CHALLENGES:
- Stereotyping risk
- Readability
- Othering danger
- Authorial politics
```

## üîÑ Sub-Theme 6: Perspective and Focalization

### Multiple Perspectives

```
SHIFTING PERSPECTIVES:

EPISTOLARY NOVELS:
Letters from multiple writers
- Different viewpoints
- Reader synthesizes
- Clarissa, The Color Purple

ROTATING NARRATORS:
Chapters from different POVs
- As I Lay Dying
- The Poisonwood Bible
- Each voice distinct

FRAME NARRATIVES:
Story within story
- Wuthering Heights
- Frankenstein
- Multiple distances

EFFECT:
- Truth is perspectival
- Complexity of events
- Reader must judge
- No single authority
```

### The Implications of Perspective

```
PERSPECTIVE CHOICES MEAN:

FIRST PERSON LIMITS:
- What narrator knows
- What narrator sees
- What narrator understands
- What narrator will admit

THIRD PERSON CHOICES:
- Whose experience matters?
- Whose thoughts do we access?
- Who is objectified?
- Who is humanized?

POLITICAL DIMENSIONS:
- Whose story gets told?
- From whose viewpoint?
- What's marginalized?
- Power in narration

Example:
Wide Sargasso Sea retells Jane Eyre
from Bertha's perspective, challenging
the original's narrative authority.
```

### Interior Monologue and Stream of Consciousness

```
INTERIOR MONOLOGUE:
Character's thoughts rendered

"She wondered if he would come. 
Probably not, she thought. He never 
came when she needed him."

Organized, grammatical, accessible


STREAM OF CONSCIOUSNESS:
Attempting to capture actual thought-flow

"If he would come but he won't not 
ever yes the clock and mother said 
always always the train..."

Characteristics:
- Free association
- Fragmented syntax
- No clear boundaries
- Sensory impressions
- Memory intrusion

Writers: Joyce, Woolf, Faulkner

Effect:
- Psychological intimacy
- Time subjectively rendered
- Consciousness as experience
```

## üé® Sub-Theme 7: Character Types and Archetypes

### Common Character Archetypes

```
THE HERO:
- Pursues quest
- Faces trials
- Transformed by journey
- Often ordinary becoming extraordinary

THE MENTOR:
- Guides the hero
- Provides wisdom/tools
- Often sacrificed
- Gandalf, Dumbledore

THE SHADOW:
- Dark reflection of hero
- Represents what hero could become
- Tests hero's values
- Often antagonist

THE TRICKSTER:
- Disrupts order
- Reveals truth through chaos
- Often comic
- Puck, Loki figures

THE THRESHOLD GUARDIAN:
- Tests hero's commitment
- Guards boundary
- Must be overcome
- Bouncers, gatekeepers
```

### Antihero and Complex Protagonists

```
TRADITIONAL HERO:
- Admirable qualities
- Moral compass
- Audience identification
- Triumph of good

ANTIHERO:
- Flawed, morally ambiguous
- May lack heroic qualities
- Still protagonist
- Complex audience response

Types of Antiheroes:

THE RELUCTANT HERO:
- Doesn't want the role
- Forced by circumstances
- Ordinary person thrust into action

THE FLAWED HERO:
- Heroic qualities with significant weakness
- Internal conflict
- Tragic potential

THE VILLAIN PROTAGONIST:
- Morally corrupt protagonist
- Challenges audience sympathy
- Macbeth, Humbert Humbert

THE BYRONIC HERO:
- Passionate, moody, rebellious
- Haunted by past
- Rochester, Heathcliff
```

### Developing vs. Static Characters

```
CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT:

DYNAMIC CHARACTER:
Undergoes significant internal change

- Elizabeth Bennet learns humility
- Pip loses illusions
- Raskolnikov finds redemption

STATIC CHARACTER:
Remains essentially unchanged

- Sherlock Holmes (consistent genius)
- Atticus Finch (steady moral compass)
- Not necessarily flat

DEVELOPMENT PATTERNS:

GROWTH:
- Innocence ‚Üí Experience
- Ignorance ‚Üí Knowledge
- Weakness ‚Üí Strength

DECLINE:
- Corruption
- Degradation
- Tragic fall

REVELATION:
- True nature exposed
- Hidden depths revealed
- Not change but disclosure
```

### Sample Thesis Statements

```
CHARACTER THESIS:

WEAK:
"Heathcliff is a complex character."
"Elizabeth Bennet changes in the novel."

STRONG:
"Heathcliff's transformation from 
sympathetic victim to cruel tormentor 
exposes how cycles of abuse perpetuate 
themselves, challenging readers to 
maintain sympathy for a protagonist 
who becomes monstrous."

"Elizabeth Bennet's evolution from 
prideful wit to humbled self-knowledge 
parallels Darcy's journey, suggesting 
that true love requires both parties 
to confront their flaws honestly."


VOICE/NARRATOR THESIS:

WEAK:
"The narrator is unreliable."
"The book uses first person."

STRONG:
"Stevens's meticulously formal 
narration in The Remains of the Day 
functions as a psychological defense 
mechanism, revealing through its very 
restraint the emotional devastation 
he cannot directly acknowledge."

"Through Nick Carraway's retrospective 
narration, Fitzgerald creates a 
perspective simultaneously seduced 
by and critical of Gatsby's dream, 
positioning readers to share Nick's 
impossible ambivalence."
```

## üìù Practice Analysis

### Sample Character Analysis

```
CHARACTER: JAY GATSBY

DIRECT CHARACTERIZATION:
- Nick calls him "great"
- "Gorgeous" in personality
- Mysterious background

INDIRECT (STEAL):

SPEECH:
- "Old sport" catchphrase
- Carefully constructed manner
- Reveals Midwest origins

THOUGHTS:
- Not directly accessed
- Only through Nick's interpretation
- Dreams revealed through action

EFFECT ON OTHERS:
- Fascinates Nick
- Daisy attracted then retreats
- Rumors swirl around him

ACTIONS:
- Throws parties but doesn't enjoy them
- Accumulates wealth for Daisy
- Takes blame for Myrtle's death

LOOKS:
- Elaborate dress
- Carefully constructed appearance
- "Platonic conception of himself"

ANALYSIS:
Gatsby embodies self-invention and 
its limits. His characterization 
through others' eyes (we never access 
his thoughts directly) reinforces his 
status as projection and dream rather 
than fully knowable person.
```

### Sample Voice Analysis

```
PASSAGE - LOLITA OPENING:

"Lolita, light of my life, fire of 
my loins. My sin, my soul. Lo-lee-ta: 
the tip of the tongue taking a trip 
of three steps down the palate to 
tap, at three, on the teeth."

ANALYSIS:

DICTION:
- Romantic, even reverent ("light of my life")
- Sensual ("fire of my loins")
- Linguistic ("palate," "tip of the tongue")

SYNTAX:
- Short declarations
- Rhythmic, almost poetic
- Careful, deliberate

SOUND:
- Alliteration ("light of my life")
- Attention to phonetics
- Musical quality

EFFECT:
- Beautiful, seductive prose
- Troubling given subject
- Narrator's sophistication evident
- Reader implicated in pleasure

UNRELIABILITY:
The gorgeous prose is weapon‚Äî
Humbert uses language to seduce reader 
as he seduced Lolita. Recognizing this 
is crucial to ethical reading.
```

## üéì Key Takeaways

```
1. Characters are constructed
   - Not real people
   - Built through technique
   - Serve artistic purposes
   - Open to interpretation

2. Narrators shape everything
   - Always mediated
   - Never neutral
   - Choices meaningful
   - Reliability matters

3. Voice is meaning
   - How it's told matters
   - Sound creates effect
   - Style is substance
   - Listen to the prose

4. Perspective is power
   - Who tells matters
   - What's shown/hidden
   - Political dimensions
   - Reader positioning

5. Complexity is the goal
   - Simple characters can work
   - Complex ones reward analysis
   - Ambiguity is feature not bug
   - Literature explores humanity
```
