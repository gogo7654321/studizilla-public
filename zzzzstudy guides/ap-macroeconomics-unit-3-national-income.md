### Spending Multiplier Derivation

**Starting Point:** Each round of spending becomes income for someone else

| Round | New Spending | Formula |
|-------|--------------|---------|
| 1 | Î”G | Initial injection |

**Total Change in GDP:**
$$\Delta GDP = \Delta G \times (1 + MPC + MPC^2 + MPC^3 + ...)$$

**Using geometric series formula (where 0 < MPC < 1):**
$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} MPC^n = \frac{1}{1-MPC}$$

**Therefore:**
$$\boxed{\text{Spending Multiplier} = \frac{1}{1-MPC} = \frac{1}{MPS}}$$

### Balanced Budget Multiplier Proof

**If Î”G = Î”T (equal increase in both):**

$$\Delta GDP = (\text{Spending Mult} \times \Delta G) + (\text{Tax Mult} \times \Delta T)$$

$$\Delta GDP = \frac{1}{MPS} \times \Delta G + \frac{-MPC}{MPS} \times \Delta T$$

**Since Î”G = Î”T:**
$$\Delta GDP = \frac{\Delta G}{MPS} - \frac{MPC \times \Delta G}{MPS}$$

$$\Delta GDP = \frac{\Delta G (1 - MPC)}{MPS} = \frac{\Delta G \times MPS}{MPS} = \Delta G$$

$$\boxed{\text{Balanced Budget Multiplier} = 1}$$

### Question 1: AD-AS Analysis

**Assume the economy is in long-run equilibrium. A major hurricane destroys significant production capacity.**

**(a)** Draw a correctly labeled AD-AS graph showing the initial equilibrium.

### Model Answer 1:

**(a)-(b)** Graph should show:
- Initial equilibrium where AD, SRAS, and LRAS intersect
- LRAS shifts LEFT (productive capacity destroyed)
- SRAS shifts LEFT (higher costs, less output possible)
- New equilibrium: Higher PL, Lower Y (stagflation)

**(c)** This is a **negative supply shock**. It causes **stagflation** - both higher prices AND higher unemployment simultaneously. Unemployment rises because output falls below the new (lower) potential.

**(d)** Long-run adjustment:
1. With lower output and higher unemployment, wages eventually fall
2. Falling wages reduce production costs
3. SRAS shifts right gradually
4. Economy moves toward new LRAS (at lower potential GDP)
5. However, full recovery requires rebuilding capital (LRAS shift right)

### Question 2: Multiplier Calculation

**The marginal propensity to consume is 0.75. The economy has a recessionary gap of $200 billion.**

**(a)** Calculate the spending multiplier.

**(b)** How much additional government spending is needed to close the gap?

**(c)** Calculate the tax multiplier.

**(d)** What tax cut would close the same gap?

**(e)** Explain why the required tax cut is larger than the spending increase.

### Model Answer 2:

**(a)** Spending Multiplier:
$$\frac{1}{1-MPC} = \frac{1}{1-0.75} = \frac{1}{0.25} = 4$$

**(b)** Required spending:
$$\Delta G = \frac{\text{Gap}}{\text{Multiplier}} = \frac{\$200B}{4} = \$50 \text{ billion}$$

**(c)** Tax Multiplier:
$$\frac{-MPC}{1-MPC} = \frac{-0.75}{0.25} = -3$$

**(d)** Required tax cut:
$$\Delta T = \frac{\text{Gap}}{\text{Tax Mult}} = \frac{\$200B}{3} = \$66.7 \text{ billion tax CUT}$$

**(e)** The tax cut must be larger because some of the tax cut is saved, not spent. Only 75% (MPC) of the tax cut enters the spending stream initially, while 100% of direct government spending does. This "leakage" to savings means taxes are less powerful per dollar than spending.

### Question 3: Policy Trade-offs

**The economy is experiencing an inflationary gap with unemployment below the natural rate.**

**(a)** What fiscal policy action should the government take?

**(b)** Explain the effect on AD and the macroeconomic equilibrium.

**(c)** What are two potential problems with using fiscal policy to address this situation?

### Model Answer 3:

**(a)** The government should use **contractionary fiscal policy**: decrease government spending (G) and/or increase taxes (T).

**(b)** Effect on AD:
- Decreased G directly reduces AD
- Increased T reduces disposable income â†’ reduces C â†’ reduces AD
- AD shifts LEFT
- Price level falls, real GDP decreases toward potential
- Unemployment rises toward natural rate

**(c)** Two problems:

1. **Time lags**: Recognition lag (identifying the problem), action lag (passing legislation), and impact lag (policy taking effect) mean the economy might self-correct before policy works, or policy might be pro-cyclical.

2. **Political difficulty**: Cutting spending or raising taxes is politically unpopular. Politicians may resist contractionary measures even when economically appropriate.
