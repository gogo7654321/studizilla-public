## ‚öõÔ∏è ELEMENTS AND ATOMS

### THE ELEMENTS OF LIFE

**96% of living matter consists of just 4 elements!**

| Element | Symbol | % of Body Mass | Role |
|---------|--------|----------------|------|
| **Oxygen** | O | 65% | Water, organic molecules, cellular respiration |
| **Carbon** | C | 18% | Backbone of all organic molecules |
| **Hydrogen** | H | 10% | Water, organic molecules, pH |
| **Nitrogen** | N | 3% | Proteins, nucleic acids |

**Trace elements** (< 0.01%): Iron (Fe), Iodine (I), Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn), etc.

### ATOMIC STRUCTURE

**Atom**: Smallest unit of matter that retains element properties

**Components:**
- **Protons** (+): Atomic number, defines element
- **Neutrons** (0): Isotopes differ in neutron number
- **Electrons** (-): Occupy orbitals, determine chemical behavior

**Key formulas:**
- Atomic number = # protons
- Mass number = # protons + # neutrons
- Neutral atom: # protons = # electrons

### ISOTOPES

**Isotope**: Atoms of same element with different mass numbers

**Example:** Carbon isotopes
- $^{12}C$: 6 protons, 6 neutrons (98.9% of carbon)
- $^{13}C$: 6 protons, 7 neutrons (1.1%)
- $^{14}C$: 6 protons, 8 neutrons (radioactive, used for dating)

**Radioactive isotopes** used in:
- üß™ Medical imaging (PET scans)
- üìÖ Carbon-14 dating
- üß¨ Tracers in research

### ELECTRON SHELLS AND ORBITALS

**Electron shells:** Energy levels around nucleus
- 1st shell: 2 electrons max
- 2nd shell: 8 electrons max
- 3rd shell: 8 electrons max (for most elements in biology)

**Valence electrons**: Electrons in outermost shell
- Determine chemical reactivity
- Atoms "want" full outer shell (stable = noble gas configuration)

**Example:** Carbon has 4 valence electrons ‚Üí can form 4 bonds ‚úì

## üíß WATER AND ITS PROPERTIES

### WHY WATER IS SPECIAL

**Water = "Universal Solvent" of Life**

**Molecular structure:**
- Bent shape (104.5¬∞ angle)
- O is Œ¥‚àí, H is Œ¥+
- **Polar molecule**

### PROPERTIES OF WATER

| Property | Cause | Biological Importance |
|----------|-------|----------------------|
| **Cohesion** | H-bonds between water molecules | Surface tension, water transport in plants |
| **Adhesion** | H-bonds to other polar surfaces | Capillary action, water climbs xylem |
| **High specific heat** | H-bonds resist temperature change | Moderates Earth's climate, stable body temp |
| **High heat of vaporization** | Breaking many H-bonds to evaporate | Evaporative cooling (sweating) |
| **Ice floats** | H-bonds form crystal lattice | Insulates aquatic life in winter |
| **Universal solvent** | Polar, forms H-bonds | Dissolves ions and polar molecules |

### COHESION AND ADHESION

**Cohesion**: Water molecules stick to each other (H-bonds)
- Creates **surface tension**
- Allows insects to walk on water
- Water forms droplets

**Adhesion**: Water molecules stick to other polar surfaces
- **Capillary action**: Water climbs narrow tubes
- Helps transport water in plants (xylem)

**Transpiration-Cohesion-Tension Theory:**
1. Water evaporates from leaves (transpiration)
2. Creates tension (negative pressure)
3. Cohesion pulls water column up from roots
4. Adhesion keeps water against xylem walls

### TEMPERATURE MODERATION

**Specific heat capacity:** Amount of heat to raise 1 g by 1¬∞C

**Water's specific heat = 4.18 J/g¬∞C** (very high!)

**Why?** H-bonds absorb energy before molecules speed up

**Results:**
- üåä Oceans moderate coastal climates
- ü©∫ Body temperature stays stable
- üå°Ô∏è Takes long time to heat/cool water

**Heat of vaporization**: Energy to convert liquid ‚Üí gas

**Water's heat of vaporization = 2260 J/g** (highest of all liquids!)

**Evaporative cooling:**
- Sweating cools body
- Panting cools dogs
- Transpiration cools plants

### ICE FLOATS (Density Anomaly)

**Most substances:** Solid denser than liquid
**Water:** Solid LESS dense than liquid!

**Why?**
- Liquid water: H-bonds constantly break/reform
- Ice: H-bonds form stable crystal lattice
- Lattice has more space ‚Üí less dense

**Biological importance:**
- ‚ùÑÔ∏è Ice insulates lakes/oceans in winter
- üêü Aquatic life survives beneath ice
- üåç Without this, oceans would freeze solid from bottom up!

### WATER AS A SOLVENT

**Hydrophilic** ("water-loving"): Polar/ionic substances dissolve in water
- Examples: NaCl, glucose, amino acids

**Hydrophobic** ("water-fearing"): Nonpolar substances don't dissolve
- Examples: Oils, fats, waxes

**Hydration shell**: Water molecules surround dissolved ions/molecules

**Example:** Dissolving NaCl
```
Na‚Å∫Cl‚Åª ‚Üí Na‚Å∫(aq) + Cl‚Åª(aq)

Water molecules orient:
  Œ¥‚àí O around Na‚Å∫
  Œ¥+ H around Cl‚Åª
```

## üî¨ ACIDS, BASES, AND pH

### DISSOCIATION OF WATER

Water can dissociate (autoionization):

$$H_2O \rightleftharpoons H^+ + OH^-$$

or more accurately:

$$2H_2O \rightleftharpoons H_3O^+ + OH^-$$

At 25¬∞C: $[H^+] = [OH^-] = 1 \times 10^{-7}$ M

### pH SCALE

**pH**: "Power of Hydrogen" = measure of acidity

$$pH = -\log[H^+]$$

| pH Range | Type | [H‚Å∫] vs [OH‚Åª] | Examples |
|----------|------|----------------|----------|
| **0-6.9** | Acidic | [H‚Å∫] > [OH‚Åª] | Stomach acid (pH 2), lemon juice (pH 2.3) |
| **7** | Neutral | [H‚Å∫] = [OH‚Åª] | Pure water |
| **7.1-14** | Basic/Alkaline | [H‚Å∫] < [OH‚Åª] | Baking soda (pH 9), bleach (pH 13) |

**Key insight:** pH scale is logarithmic!
- pH 4 is **10√ó more acidic** than pH 5
- pH 4 is **100√ó more acidic** than pH 6

**Calculating pH:**
- pH = ‚àílog[H‚Å∫]
- [H‚Å∫] = 10^(‚àípH)
- pH + pOH = 14

**Example:** [H‚Å∫] = 1 √ó 10‚Åª‚Åµ M

### ACIDS AND BASES

**Acid**: Donates H‚Å∫ (proton donor)
- HCl ‚Üí H‚Å∫ + Cl‚Åª
- Examples: HCl, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, CH‚ÇÉCOOH

- Examples: NaOH, NH‚ÇÉ, HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª

**Strong acids/bases**: Completely dissociate

### BUFFERS

**Buffer**: Resists pH changes by accepting or donating H‚Å∫

**How they work:**
- Weak acid (HA) ‚áå H‚Å∫ + A‚Åª
- Add acid (H‚Å∫) ‚Üí A‚Åª absorbs it
- Add base (OH‚Åª) ‚Üí HA releases H‚Å∫ to neutralize

**Example:** Carbonic acid buffer in blood

- If pH rises: H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ releases H‚Å∫

**Acidosis**: Blood pH < 7.35 (too acidic)

### ISOMERS

**Isomer**: Same molecular formula, different structure

**Types:**

**1. Structural isomers**: Different bonding arrangements
- Example: C‚ÇÖH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ

**2. Glycogen** (animals)
- Glucose polymer (Œ±-1,4 and Œ±-1,6 linkages)

**Structural polysaccharides:**

- Linear, no branching
- H-bonds between chains ‚Üí strong fibers
- Most abundant organic compound on Earth!
- Humans can't digest (lack enzyme)
- Provides dietary fiber

**4. Chitin** (fungi, arthropods)
- Modified glucose polymer
- Contains nitrogen
- Forms exoskeletons, fungal cell walls

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Starch/Glycogen | Cellulose |

**Proteins fold into specific 3D shape (conformation)**

**Driven by:**
- Hydrophobic effect (nonpolar R groups cluster inside)
- H-bonds
- Ionic interactions
- Disulfide bonds

**Chaperone proteins**: Help proper folding, prevent aggregation

**Denaturation**: Loss of protein structure/function
- Causes: Heat, pH changes, chemicals
- Often irreversible (e.g., cooking egg)

### PROTEIN FUNCTION DEPENDS ON SHAPE

**"Structure determines function"** - Key concept!

**Example: Enzymes**
- Active site shape complements substrate
- Even small shape changes destroy function

**Example: Antibodies**
- Variable region binds specific antigen

### DNA STRUCTURE

**Double helix** (Watson & Crick, 1953)

**Key features:**
- Two antiparallel strands
- Sugar-phosphate backbone (outside)
- Bases inside (rungs of ladder)
- Complementary base pairing via H-bonds:
  - **A = T** (2 H-bonds)
  - **G ‚â° C** (3 H-bonds)

**Chargaff's rules:**
- Amount of A = amount of T
- Amount of G = amount of C
- A + G = T + C

**Why double helix?**
- Protects bases inside
- Complementary strands allow replication
- Base pairing ensures accuracy

**5' to 3' directionality:**
- 5' end: Phosphate group
- 3' end: Hydroxyl group
- Strands run antiparallel (opposite directions)

### NUCLEOTIDE POLYMERS

**Phosphodiester bond**: Links nucleotides

**Formation:** Dehydration synthesis
- Phosphate of one nucleotide bonds to 3' OH of next
- Creates sugar-phosphate backbone

**Polynucleotide**: Chain of nucleotides

### OTHER NUCLEOTIDES

- Adenine + ribose + 3 phosphates

**NAD‚Å∫, NADH, FAD, FADH‚ÇÇ:**

### WHAT ARE ENZYMES?

**Enzyme**: Biological catalyst (usually protein)

**Catalyst**: Speeds up chemical reaction without being consumed

**Properties:**
- üöÄ Increase reaction rate (10‚Å∂ to 10¬π‚Å∑ times faster!)
- üîÑ Not consumed or altered
- üéØ Highly specific (one enzyme, one reaction typically)
- ‚öôÔ∏è Can be regulated

### HOW ENZYMES WORK

**Activation energy (E‚Çê)**: Energy needed to start reaction

**Enzymes lower E‚Çê** ‚Üí reaction occurs faster

**Active site**: Region where substrate binds
- Complementary shape to substrate
- Contains catalytic amino acids

**Induced fit model:**
1. Substrate approaches active site
2. Enzyme changes shape slightly
3. Substrate binds (enzyme-substrate complex)
4. Reaction occurs
5. Products released
6. Enzyme returns to original shape

$$E + S \rightleftharpoons ES \rightarrow E + P$$

Where:
- E = enzyme
- S = substrate
- ES = enzyme-substrate complex
- P = product

### ENZYME SPECIFICITY

**Lock-and-key model** (old): Active site rigid, substrate fits exactly

**Induced fit model** (modern): Active site flexible, adjusts to substrate

**Why specific?**
- Active site shape
- Chemical environment
- Amino acid R groups position substrates

**Example:** Lactase breaks down lactose (not sucrose or maltose)

### FACTORS AFFECTING ENZYME ACTIVITY

**1. Temperature**
- ‚Üë Temperature ‚Üí ‚Üë kinetic energy ‚Üí ‚Üë reaction rate
- Too high ‚Üí denaturation ‚Üí loss of function
- Optimal temperature: ~37¬∞C for human enzymes

**2. pH**
- Each enzyme has optimal pH
- Extreme pH ‚Üí denaturation
- Examples:
  - Pepsin (stomach): pH 2
  - Trypsin (intestine): pH 8
  - Most enzymes: pH 6-8

**3. Substrate concentration**
- ‚Üë [S] ‚Üí ‚Üë rate (more collisions)
- Plateau when all active sites occupied (saturation)
- **Vmax**: Maximum rate at saturation

**4. Enzyme concentration**
- ‚Üë [E] ‚Üí ‚Üë rate (more active sites)
- Linear relationship (if substrate abundant)

### ENZYME INHIBITION

**Competitive inhibitor:**
- Competes with substrate for active site
- Similar shape to substrate
- Can be overcome by increasing [S]
- Example: Statin drugs (inhibit cholesterol synthesis)

**Noncompetitive inhibitor:**
- Binds to allosteric site (not active site)
- Changes enzyme shape ‚Üí active site altered
- Cannot be overcome by increasing [S]
- Example: Cyanide (inhibits cellular respiration)

**Allosteric regulation:**
- Regulatory molecule binds to allosteric site
- Changes enzyme shape and activity
- Can be activation or inhibition
- Common in metabolic pathways

**Feedback inhibition:**
- End product inhibits earlier enzyme
- Prevents overproduction
- Example: Isoleucine inhibits threonine deaminase

### COFACTORS AND COENZYMES

**Cofactor**: Non-protein helper molecule
- **Inorganic**: Metal ions (Zn¬≤‚Å∫, Fe¬≤‚Å∫, Mg¬≤‚Å∫)
- **Organic**: Coenzymes

**Coenzyme**: Organic cofactor
- Often derived from vitamins
- Examples: NAD‚Å∫ (from niacin), FAD (from riboflavin), Coenzyme A

**Prosthetic group**: Cofactor covalently bonded to enzyme
