# AP European History Final Exam Review

## Exam Overview

The AP European History exam tests your understanding of European history from approximately 1450 to the present. The exam consists of:

| Section | Questions | Time | Weight |
|---------|-----------|------|--------|
| Section I, Part A | 55 Multiple Choice | 55 minutes | 40% |
| Section I, Part B | 3 Short Answer | 40 minutes | 20% |
| Section II, Part A | 1 Document-Based Question (DBQ) | 60 minutes | 25% |
| Section II, Part B | 1 Long Essay Question (LEQ) | 40 minutes | 15% |

### Unit Weightings

| Unit | Topic | Exam Weight |

## Unit 1: Renaissance and Exploration (c. 1450-1648)

### Key Concepts

#### The Italian Renaissance
- **Humanism**: Intellectual movement emphasizing classical learning, human potential, and secular concerns
- **Civic humanism**: Application of humanist ideals to political life and civic duty
- **Individualism**: Celebration of individual achievement and unique talents
- **Secularism**: Growing focus on worldly matters alongside religious concerns

#### Key Renaissance Figures

| Figure | Field | Contributions |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| Petrarch | Literature | "Father of Humanism," rediscovered classical texts |
| Machiavelli | Political Philosophy | *The Prince*, realpolitik, separation of politics from morality |
| Leonardo da Vinci | Art/Science | Mona Lisa, Last Supper, scientific drawings |
| Michelangelo | Art | Sistine Chapel, David, St. Peter's Basilica |
| Raphael | Art | School of Athens, Madonna paintings |
| Botticelli | Art | Birth of Venus, Primavera |

#### Renaissance Art Innovations
1. **Linear perspective**: Mathematical system for creating depth
2. **Chiaroscuro**: Use of light and shadow for three-dimensionality
3. **Sfumato**: Soft, hazy transitions between colors
4. **Naturalism**: Accurate depiction of human anatomy
5. **Oil painting**: Allowed for greater detail and color richness

#### Northern Renaissance
- **Christian humanism**: Combined classical learning with Christian reform
- **Erasmus**: *In Praise of Folly*, criticized Church corruption
- **Thomas More**: *Utopia*, imagined ideal society
- **Printing press** (Gutenberg, c. 1450): Revolutionized information spread

### Age of Exploration

#### Causes of Exploration
1. **Economic**: Search for new trade routes to Asia
2. **Religious**: Spread Christianity, convert "heathens"
3. **Political**: National competition for power and prestige
4. **Technological**: Better ships (caravel), navigational tools
5. **Intellectual**: Renaissance curiosity about the world

## Unit 2: Age of Reformation (c. 1450-1648)

### Protestant Reformation

#### Causes of the Reformation
1. **Church corruption**: Simony, nepotism, pluralism, absenteeism
2. **Sale of indulgences**: Payments to reduce time in purgatory
3. **Clerical ignorance**: Many priests poorly educated
4. **Papal wealth and politics**: Renaissance popes focused on worldly power
5. **Printing press**: Spread of reform ideas rapidly

#### Martin Luther's Key Ideas
1. **Sola fide**: Salvation by faith alone
2. **Sola scriptura**: Bible as sole religious authority
3. **Sola gratia**: Salvation by grace alone
4. **Priesthood of all believers**: No special priestly class needed
5. **Two kingdoms**: Separation of spiritual and temporal authority

#### John Calvin's Key Ideas
1. **Predestination**: God has already chosen the elect
2. **Total depravity**: Humans inherently sinful
3. **Theocracy in Geneva**: Church and state united
4. **Protestant work ethic**: Worldly success as sign of election

#### Comparison of Reform Movements

| Movement | Leader | Key Beliefs | Location |
|----------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Lutheranism | Martin Luther | Justification by faith, Bible authority | Germany, Scandinavia |
| Calvinism | John Calvin | Predestination, theocracy | Switzerland, France, Scotland, Netherlands |
| Anglicanism | Henry VIII | Royal supremacy, Catholic-Protestant blend | England |
| Anabaptism | Various | Adult baptism, separation of church/state | Various |
| Zwinglianism | Ulrich Zwingli | Symbolic communion, iconoclasm | Zurich |

### Catholic/Counter-Reformation

#### Council of Trent (1545-1563)
- Reaffirmed Catholic doctrines (transubstantiation, seven sacraments)
- Condemned Protestant heresies
- Reformed clergy education (seminaries)
- Ended worst abuses (indulgence sale)
- Created the Index of Forbidden Books

#### Jesuits (Society of Jesus)
- Founded by Ignatius Loyola (1540)
- Rigorous education and discipline
- Missionary work worldwide
- Education as tool for reconversion
- *Spiritual Exercises* for meditation

### Religious Wars

#### French Wars of Religion (1562-1598)
- Huguenots (Calvinists) vs. Catholics
- St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572)
- War of the Three Henrys
- Henry IV converts to Catholicism ("Paris is worth a Mass")
- Edict of Nantes (1598): Religious toleration

#### Thirty Years' War (1618-1648)
1. **Bohemian Phase** (1618-1625): Defenestration of Prague
2. **Danish Phase** (1625-1629): Protestant defeat
3. **Swedish Phase** (1630-1635): Gustavus Adolphus
4. **French Phase** (1635-1648): France vs. Habsburgs

**Peace of Westphalia (1648)**:
- End of religious wars in Holy Roman Empire
- Recognition of Calvinism
- Sovereignty of German states
- Decline of Habsburg power
- Rise of modern state system

## Unit 3: Absolutism and Constitutionalism (c. 1648-1815)

### Absolute Monarchy

#### Characteristics of Absolutism
1. **Divine right of kings**: Monarchs chosen by God
2. **Centralized power**: Weakening of nobility and representative bodies
3. **Standing armies**: Professional military loyal to crown
4. **Bureaucracy**: Trained administrators implementing royal will
5. **Mercantilism**: Economic policy serving state interests

#### France Under Louis XIV (1643-1715)

| Aspect | Policy/Achievement |
|--------|-------------------|
| Government | "L'état, c'est moi"; Versailles Palace |
| Religion | Revocation of Edict of Nantes (1685) |
| Economy | Colbert's mercantilism |
| Military | Aggressive wars; largest army in Europe |
| Culture | Patronage of arts; French cultural dominance |
| Nobility | "Domesticated" at Versailles |

#### Other Absolute Monarchs

| Country | Ruler | Key Policies |
|---------|-------|--------------|
| Prussia | Frederick William I | "Soldier King," efficient bureaucracy |
| Prussia | Frederick the Great | Enlightened absolutism, religious toleration |
| Russia | Peter the Great | Westernization, St. Petersburg, Table of Ranks |
| Russia | Catherine the Great | Expanded territory, Enlightenment patron |
| Austria | Maria Theresa | Centralization, educational reform |
| Austria | Joseph II | Most radical Enlightened despot; abolished serfdom |

## Unit 4: Scientific, Philosophical, and Political Developments (c. 1648-1815)

### Scientific Revolution

#### Key Scientific Figures

| Scientist | Field | Contribution |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Copernicus | Astronomy | Heliocentric theory |
| Tycho Brahe | Astronomy | Precise astronomical observations |
| Johannes Kepler | Astronomy | Laws of planetary motion |
| Galileo Galilei | Physics/Astronomy | Telescope observations; law of falling bodies |
| Isaac Newton | Physics/Math | Laws of motion, gravity; calculus |
| Francis Bacon | Philosophy | Empiricism, scientific method |
| René Descartes | Philosophy/Math | Rationalism, "I think, therefore I am" |
| William Harvey | Medicine | Blood circulation |
| Robert Boyle | Chemistry | Boyle's Law; "father of chemistry" |

#### Scientific Method
1. Observation of phenomena
2. Formation of hypothesis
3. Experimentation and testing
4. Analysis of results
5. Formation of theory/law

### The Enlightenment

#### Core Enlightenment Ideas
1. **Reason**: Human reason can solve problems
2. **Progress**: Society can improve over time
3. **Natural law**: Universal principles govern nature and society
4. **Individual rights**: Humans possess inherent rights
5. **Religious toleration**: Freedom from persecution
6. **Separation of powers**: Prevent tyranny through divided government

#### Key Enlightenment Thinkers

| Philosopher | Key Ideas | Major Work |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| John Locke | Natural rights, social contract, tabula rasa | *Two Treatises of Government* |
| Voltaire | Religious toleration, free speech, criticism | *Candide*, *Letters on England* |
| Montesquieu | Separation of powers, checks and balances | *The Spirit of the Laws* |
| Rousseau | General will, popular sovereignty, noble savage | *The Social Contract* |
| Diderot | Encyclopedia, spread of knowledge | *Encyclopédie* |
| Adam Smith | Free market, laissez-faire, "invisible hand" | *Wealth of Nations* |
| Mary Wollstonecraft | Women's rights, education | *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* |
| Cesare Beccaria | Criminal justice reform, end torture | *On Crimes and Punishments* |

#### Enlightened Despotism
- Rulers who used Enlightenment ideas to strengthen their rule
- Frederick the Great (Prussia), Catherine the Great (Russia), Joseph II (Austria)
- Reforms in education, law, religious toleration
- Did not give up absolute power

### Spread of Enlightenment
- **Salons**: Gatherings in homes (often hosted by women)
- **Coffeehouses**: Public discussion spaces
- **Academies**: Scientific societies
- **Encyclopédie**: Comprehensive knowledge compilation
- **Freemasonry**: Secret societies promoting Enlightenment values

### French Revolution

#### Causes of the Revolution
1. **Social inequality**: Three Estates system; bourgeoisie frustration
2. **Financial crisis**: War debts, royal extravagance
3. **Enlightenment ideas**: Liberty, equality, popular sovereignty
4. **Weak leadership**: Louis XVI indecisive
5. **Economic hardship**: Bad harvests, bread prices

- Kingdom of Netherlands (Belgium + Holland)
- Poland divided again
- Holy Alliance (Austria, Prussia, Russia)
- Concert of Europe for maintaining order

## Unit 6: Industrialization and Its Effects (c. 1815-1914)

### Industrial Revolution

#### Why Britain First?
1. **Geography**: Coal, iron, rivers, ports
2. **Agriculture**: Agricultural Revolution increased food/labor
3. **Capital**: Profits from trade available for investment
4. **Technology**: Culture of innovation
5. **Government**: Stable, supportive of business
6. **Empire**: Colonial markets and raw materials

#### Key Inventions

| Invention | Inventor | Date | Significance |
|-----------|----------|------|--------------|
| Spinning Jenny | Hargreaves | 1764 | Increased thread production |
| Water Frame | Arkwright | 1769 | Water-powered spinning |
| Steam Engine | Watt | 1769 | Power source for factories |
| Cotton Gin | Whitney | 1793 | Increased cotton processing |
| Locomotive | Stephenson | 1814 | Rail transportation |
| Telegraph | Morse | 1837 | Instant communication |
| Bessemer Process | Bessemer | 1856 | Cheap steel production |

### Second Industrial Revolution (c. 1870-1914)
- Steel, chemicals, electricity
- Assembly line production
- Petroleum and internal combustion engine
- Rise of Germany and United States

### Social Effects of Industrialization

#### Urbanization
- Massive population growth in cities
- Poor living conditions: overcrowding, sanitation, disease
- Factory system: long hours, low wages, child labor
- Class divisions: bourgeoisie vs. proletariat

#### Reform Movements
1. **Labor unions**: Collective bargaining
2. **Factory Acts**: Limited child labor, working hours
3. **Public health**: Sanitation, clean water
4. **Education**: Compulsory schooling
5. **Suffrage expansion**: Working class voting rights

### Economic Theories

| Theory | Thinker | Key Ideas |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Classical Liberalism | Adam Smith | Free markets, limited government |
| Utilitarianism | Bentham/Mill | Greatest good for greatest number |
| Socialism | Various | Public ownership, redistribution |
| Marxism | Marx/Engels | Class struggle, revolution, communism |
| Revisionist Socialism | Bernstein | Gradual reform, not revolution |
| Social Darwinism | Spencer | Survival of fittest in society |

## Unit 7: 19th-Century Perspectives and Political Developments (c. 1815-1914)

### Political Ideologies

#### Conservatism
- Tradition, order, hierarchy
- Monarchy, Church, aristocracy
- Gradual change, not revolution
- Key figures: Edmund Burke, Metternich

#### Liberalism
- Individual rights and freedoms
- Constitutional government
- Free markets (laissez-faire)
- Rule of law, equality before law
- Key figures: John Stuart Mill

#### Nationalism
- Loyalty to nation over dynasty
- Self-determination
- Unifying force OR divisive force
- Cultural nationalism: language, history, traditions

#### Other Ideologies
- **Romanticism**: Emotion, nature, individualism vs. Enlightenment reason
- **Realism**: Accurate depiction of contemporary life
- **Feminism**: Women's rights, suffrage
- **Anarchism**: Abolition of all government

### Nationalism and Unification

#### Italian Unification (Risorgimento)

- Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (June 28, 1914)

## DBQ Writing Guide

### Document Analysis (HIPP)
- **H**istorical Context: What was happening at the time?
- **I**ntended Audience: Who was it written for?
- **P**oint of View: What is the author's perspective?
- **P**urpose: Why was it created?

### DBQ Structure
1. **Contextualization** (1 paragraph): Set the historical scene
2. **Thesis** (end of intro): Clear argument with categories
3. **Body paragraphs** (2-3): Each with multiple documents + outside evidence
4. **Sourcing**: HIPP analysis for at least 3 documents
5. **Outside evidence**: At least 1 piece beyond the documents
6. **Complexity** (optional): Nuanced analysis, alternative perspectives

### DBQ Scoring Rubric

| Points | Skill |
|--------|-------|
| 1 | Thesis/Claim |
| 1 | Contextualization |
| 3 | Evidence (documents + outside) |
| 1 | Sourcing (HIPP for 3+ docs) |
| 1 | Complexity |
| **7** | **Total** |

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing dates and periods** - Know your chronology
2. **Superficial causation** - Explain HOW and WHY, not just WHAT
3. **Ignoring document context** - Always consider HIPP
4. **Weak thesis statements** - Make an argument, not a list
5. **Not using all documents** - Use at least 6 of 7 in DBQ
6. **Ignoring outside evidence** - Go beyond the documents
7. **Generalizing** - Use specific names, dates, places
8. **Presentism** - Don't judge the past by modern standards
