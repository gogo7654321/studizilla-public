## ‚ùì Phase 1: Developing Your Inquiry

### Finding a Topic

```
TOPIC SELECTION:

PERSONAL INTEREST:
What genuinely fascinates you?
What would you explore voluntarily?
What keeps you curious?

EXISTING KNOWLEDGE:
What do you already know?
What courses have inspired you?
What experiences inform this?

GAP IN KNOWLEDGE:
What don't researchers know yet?
What needs more investigation?
Where can you contribute?

Can you access needed sources?
Can you conduct needed research?
Is timeline realistic?
Do you have mentor support?
```

### From Topic to Question

```
QUESTION DEVELOPMENT:

START BROAD:
"Mental health in teenagers"

ADD FOCUS:
"Effects of exercise on teen mental health"

ADD SPECIFICITY:
"Relationship between regular physical 
activity and anxiety levels in high 
school students"

MAKE RESEARCHABLE:
"To what extent does participating in 
structured physical activity programs 
reduce self-reported anxiety levels 
among high school students compared 
to those who do not participate?"

TEST YOUR QUESTION:
‚ñ° Is it original (not already answered)?
‚ñ° Is it researchable (can gather data)?
‚ñ° Is it significant (matters)?
‚ñ° Is it focused (manageable scope)?
‚ñ° Is it complex (not yes/no)?
```

### Research Question Types

```
QUESTION FRAMEWORKS:

CAUSAL:
"What is the effect of X on Y?"
"To what extent does X cause Y?"

COMPARATIVE:
"How does X compare to Y in terms of Z?"
"What are the differences between...?"

CORRELATIONAL:
"What is the relationship between X and Y?"
"How does X relate to Y?"

EVALUATIVE:
"How effective is X at achieving Y?"
"What are the strengths and limitations of...?"

EXPLORATORY:
"What factors contribute to X?"
"How can X be understood through Y framework?"
```

## üìñ Phase 2: Literature Review

### Purpose of Literature Review

```
WHY LITERATURE REVIEW:

ESTABLISH CONTEXT:
- What has been studied?
- What do we know?
- What are key debates?

IDENTIFY GAP:
- What hasn't been studied?
- Where can you contribute?
- What's missing?

BUILD FOUNDATION:
- Theory to use
- Methods to consider
- Variables to examine

DEMONSTRATE EXPERTISE:
- Show you know the field
- Credibility for your work
- Academic rigor
```

### Finding Sources

```
SOURCE STRATEGIES:

ACADEMIC DATABASES:
- JSTOR
- PubMed
- Google Scholar
- ERIC
- PsycINFO
- Specialized databases

CITATION CHAINING:
- Check source bibliographies
- Find who cites key papers
- Follow the conversation
- Identify key scholars

TYPES TO INCLUDE:
- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- Academic books/chapters
- Dissertations
- Conference papers
- Primary sources
- Relevant data sets
```

### Organizing Literature

```
SYNTHESIS MATRIX:

Create table with:
Rows: Themes/concepts
Columns: Sources

| Theme    | Source 1 | Source 2 | Source 3 |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| Theme A  | Finding  | Finding  | Finding  |
| Theme B  | Finding  | Gap      | Finding  |
| Theme C  | Disagree | Disagree | Gap      |

IDENTIFY:
- Agreement across sources
- Disagreement/debate
- Gaps in research
- Your contribution
```

### Writing Literature Review

```
LITERATURE REVIEW STRUCTURE:

INTRODUCTION:
- Scope of review
- Key themes/sections
- Gap preview

THEMATIC SECTIONS:
- Organize by theme, not source
- Synthesize, don't summarize
- Show connections
- Build toward your gap

GAP AND RATIONALE:
- What's missing?
- Why does it matter?
- How will you address it?

TRANSITION TO YOUR STUDY:
- Your question emerges from gap
- Your approach addresses gap
- Your contribution is clear
```

### Academic Writing Style

```
SCHOLARLY CONVENTIONS:

USE:
- Formal register
- Third person (typically)
- Precise language
- Technical vocabulary
- Proper citations

AVOID:
- First person (usually)
- Contractions
- Colloquialisms
- Vague language
- Unsupported claims

HEDGING:
Use appropriately to show:
- Limitations of claims
- Tentative conclusions
- Scholarly caution

"The data suggest..."
"This may indicate..."
"One interpretation is..."
```
