# üóΩ AP African American Studies Unit 3: The Practice of Freedom

## üìö Unit Overview

This unit explores how African Americans defined and practiced freedom from Reconstruction through the Harlem Renaissance, including the building of institutions, resistance to Jim Crow, and cultural flourishing despite oppression.

## üéØ Sub-Theme 1: Reconstruction (1865-1877)

### The Promise of Freedom

```
RECONSTRUCTION AMENDMENTS:

13TH AMENDMENT (1865):
- Abolished slavery
- Except as punishment for crime
- Foundation for freedom

14TH AMENDMENT (1868):
- Citizenship for all born in US
- Equal protection of laws
- Due process
- Overturned Dred Scott

15TH AMENDMENT (1870):
- Voting rights cannot be denied
  based on race
- Did NOT guarantee the vote
- States found loopholes

CONGRESSIONAL RECONSTRUCTION:
- Military occupation of South
- Required states to ratify amendments
- Protected Black voting temporarily
```

### Black Political Power

```
ACHIEVEMENTS:

VOTING:
- Black men voted in large numbers
- Voter registration drives
- Community organizing
- Republican Party loyalty

OFFICEHOLDING:
- 2,000+ Black officeholders
- 16 US Congressmen
- 2 US Senators (Revels, Bruce)
- State legislators, local officials

HIRAM REVELS (1827-1901):
- First Black US Senator (1870)
- Mississippi
- AME minister
- Advocated moderation

BLANCHE K. BRUCE (1841-1898):
- Second Black Senator (1875-1881)
- Full six-year term
- Mississippi
- Former enslaved person
```

### Institution Building

```
FREEDPEOPLE'S PRIORITIES:

EDUCATION:
- Hunger for learning
- Freedmen's Bureau schools
- Churches sponsored schools
- HBCUs founded
- By 1870: 150,000+ students

LAND:
- "40 acres and a mule" promise
- Sherman's Field Order 15
- Mostly NOT fulfilled
- Sharecropping instead

FAMILY:
- Legalized marriages
- Searched for separated family
- Established households
- Reclaimed children

CHURCHES:
- Left white-controlled churches
- AME, Baptist growth
- Community centers
- Political organizing spaces

LABOR:
- Rejected gang labor
- Sought fair wages
- Negotiated contracts
- Collective action
```

### Violence and Resistance to Reconstruction

```
WHITE SUPREMACIST TERROR:

KU KLUX KLAN (founded 1866):
- Night riders
- Violence against Black voters
- Intimidation
- Murders, whippings, arson

OTHER GROUPS:
- Knights of the White Camelia
- Red Shirts
- White League
- Rifle clubs

MASSACRES:
- Memphis (1866): 46 Black killed
- New Orleans (1866): 34+ killed
- Colfax (1873): 150+ killed
- Hamburg (1876): murdered militia

PURPOSE:
- Destroy Black political power
- Restore white supremacy
- Terror as political weapon
- "Redeem" the South

FEDERAL RESPONSE:
- Enforcement Acts (1870-1871)
- KKK Act
- Some prosecutions
- Eventually abandoned
```

### End of Reconstruction

```
COMPROMISE OF 1877:

CONTEXT:
- Disputed 1876 election
- Hayes vs. Tilden
- Electoral votes contested

AGREEMENT:
- Hayes becomes President
- Federal troops withdrawn
- South left to "home rule"
- Reconstruction abandoned

CONSEQUENCES:
- "Redemption" of South
- Black voting suppressed
- Civil rights eroded
- Beginning of Jim Crow

BETRAYAL:
"The slave went free; stood a brief
moment in the sun; then moved back
again toward slavery."
- W.E.B. Du Bois
```

## ‚õìÔ∏è Sub-Theme 2: Jim Crow Era

### Establishing Segregation

```
LEGAL FRAMEWORK:

BLACK CODES (1865-1866):
- Restricted Black movement
- Vagrancy laws
- Labor contracts forced
- Convict leasing

JIM CROW LAWS (1880s-1960s):
- "Separate but equal"
- Segregated everything:
  - Schools
  - Transportation
  - Restaurants
  - Water fountains
  - Hospitals
  - Cemeteries

PLESSY V. FERGUSON (1896):
- Upheld segregation
- "Separate but equal" doctrine
- Homer Plessy challenged
- Dissent by Harlan
- Not overturned until 1954
```

### Disenfranchisement

```
METHODS:

POLL TAXES:
- Payment required to vote
- Many too poor
- Cumulative in some states

LITERACY TESTS:
- Arbitrary administration
- White registrars decided
- "How many bubbles in soap?"
- Impossible standards

GRANDFATHER CLAUSES:
- If grandfather could vote, you could
- Excluded Black voters
- Struck down 1915

WHITE PRIMARIES:
- Democrats-only primaries
- Real election in South
- Blacks excluded
- Not ended until 1944

VIOLENCE:
- Lynching threatened voters
- Economic retaliation
- Burning homes and churches
- The ultimate weapon
```

### Lynching and Racial Terror

```
LYNCHING STATISTICS:

SCOPE:
- ~4,700 documented lynchings
- 1877-1950
- Mostly in the South
- Public spectacles

VICTIMS:
- 73% Black
- Accused of various "crimes"
- Often economic competition
- Asserting rights
- Random terror

IDA B. WELLS (1862-1931):
- Pioneering journalist
- Investigated lynching
- "Southern Horrors" (1892)
- Anti-lynching campaign
- "The way to right wrongs is to
  turn the light of truth upon them"

ANTI-LYNCHING MOVEMENT:
- NAACP campaigns
- Dyer Anti-Lynching Bill (1920s)
- Never passed
- Federal bill not until 2022
```

## üí° Sub-Theme 4: Competing Visions for Progress

### Booker T. Washington

```
PHILOSOPHY:

"ATLANTA COMPROMISE" (1895):

MAIN IDEAS:
- Vocational/industrial education
- Self-help and hard work
- Avoid political agitation
- Build economic foundation
- Accept segregation temporarily
- "Cast down your bucket where you are"

TUSKEGEE MODEL:
- Practical skills training
- Agriculture, trades
- Self-sufficiency
- Gradual progress

SUPPORT:
- White philanthropists
- Andrew Carnegie, Rockefeller
- "Tuskegee Machine" - patronage
- Most powerful Black leader

CRITICISM:
- Accommodating white supremacy
- Sacrificing civil rights
- Limited vision for equality
```

### W.E.B. Du Bois

```
PHILOSOPHY:

CRITIQUE OF WASHINGTON:
- "The Souls of Black Folk" (1903)
- "Of Mr. Booker T. Washington"
- Can't progress without rights
- Education for all, not just trades

"TALENTED TENTH":
- Educated Black elite
- Leadership class
- Liberal arts education
- Professional training
- Lead the race forward

ACTIVISM:
- Demand full civil rights NOW
- Political engagement
- Protest against injustice
- NAACP founding (1909)

LATER EVOLUTION:
- Pan-Africanism
- Socialism
- Moved to Ghana
- Died 1963, day before March on DC
```

### Other Voices

```
ADDITIONAL PERSPECTIVES:

IDA B. WELLS:
- Anti-lynching activism
- Journalism as weapon
- Direct confrontation
- Women's rights

ANNA JULIA COOPER (1858-1964):
- "A Voice from the South" (1892)
- Black feminist thought
- Education advocate
- Earned PhD at 67

MARCUS GARVEY (1887-1940):
- Black nationalism
- UNIA (1916)
- "Back to Africa"
- Black pride and self-reliance
- Mass movement
- Criticized by established leaders

Each offered different paths
forward - debate continued.
```

## üöÇ Sub-Theme 5: The Great Migration

### First Wave (1910-1940)

```
THE MOVEMENT:

NUMBERS:
- ~1.6 million moved North
- Peak during WWI
- From rural South
- To urban North/Midwest

PUSH FACTORS:
- Jim Crow oppression
- Lynching and violence
- Sharecropping poverty
- Boll weevil devastation
- Lack of opportunity

PULL FACTORS:
- Industrial jobs (WWI)
- Better wages
- Relative freedom
- Existing communities
- Black newspapers (The Defender)

DESTINATIONS:
- Chicago
- Detroit
- New York (Harlem)
- Philadelphia
- Cleveland
- Pittsburgh
```

### Chicago and The Defender

```
THE CHICAGO DEFENDER:

FOUNDED: 1905 by Robert Abbott

ROLE IN MIGRATION:
- National distribution
- Carried South by Pullman porters
- Advertised opportunities
- Publicized Southern atrocities
- "The Great Northern Drive"

IMPACT:
- Read aloud in communities
- Passed from hand to hand
- Challenged Southern narratives
- Built imagined community
- Called people North

CHICAGO'S BLACK BELT:
- South Side concentration
- Bronzeville neighborhood
- Vibrant community
- Also overcrowded, restricted
- Racial covenants
```

### Urban Life and Challenges

```
NORTHERN REALITIES:

OPPORTUNITIES:
- Industrial jobs
- Higher wages
- Voting rights
- Better schools
- More freedom

CHALLENGES:
- Housing discrimination
- Overcrowded neighborhoods
- Job ceiling (last hired, first fired)
- Racial violence (Red Summer 1919)
- De facto segregation

RED SUMMER (1919):
- Racial violence across US
- Chicago riot (38 dead)
- Over 25 cities
- White mobs attacked
- Black communities defended themselves
- Showed limits of "promised land"
```

## üé∑ Sub-Theme 6: The Harlem Renaissance

### Cultural Explosion

```
CONTEXT (1920s-1930s):

HARLEM:
- Great Migration destination
- Concentrated Black community
- Cultural institutions
- Publishing opportunities
- Nightlife and entertainment

"NEW NEGRO":
- Alain Locke's term
- Self-definition
- Pride in Black culture
- Artistic expression
- Political consciousness

SIGNIFICANCE:
- First major Black arts movement
- National and international impact
- White patronage (complicated)
- Laid groundwork for later movements
- "Blossoming of Negro literature"
```

### Literary Renaissance

```
KEY WRITERS:

LANGSTON HUGHES (1901-1967):
- "The Negro Speaks of Rivers"
- Jazz poetry
- Accessible language
- Working class focus
- "The Weary Blues" (1926)

ZORA NEALE HURSTON (1891-1960):
- "Their Eyes Were Watching God"
- Anthropologist and author
- Black Southern culture
- Female perspective
- Dialect and folklore

CLAUDE MCKAY (1889-1948):
- "If We Must Die" (1919)
- Jamaican-American
- Militant poetry
- "Harlem Shadows"

COUNTEE CULLEN (1903-1946):
- Traditional forms
- "Heritage"
- "Yet Do I Marvel"
- Explored duality

NELLA LARSEN (1891-1964):
- "Passing" (1929)
- "Quicksand" (1928)
- Mixed race identity
- Women's experiences
```

### Music and Performance

```
JAZZ AGE:

JAZZ ORIGINS:
- New Orleans roots
- Blues tradition
- Ragtime influence
- African rhythms

KEY FIGURES:

LOUIS ARMSTRONG (1901-1971):
- Trumpet virtuoso
- Scat singing

- Cultural ambassador

DUKE ELLINGTON (1899-1974):
- Bandleader, composer
- Cotton Club (1927-1931)

BESSIE SMITH (1894-1937):
- "Empress of the Blues"

PERFORMANCE SPACES:
```

## ‚úä Sub-Theme 7: Political Organizing

### NAACP

```
FOUNDING AND MISSION:

FOUNDED: 1909 in NYC

FOUNDERS:
- W.E.B. Du Bois
- Ida B. Wells
- Mary White Ovington
- Oswald Garrison Villard
- Others (interracial)

STRATEGIES:
- Legal challenges
- Lobbying Congress
- The Crisis magazine
- Public education
- Anti-lynching campaigns

LEGAL VICTORIES:
- Guinn v. US (1915): grandfather clause
- Buchanan v. Warley (1917): housing
- Moore v. Dempsey (1923): fair trial
- Building toward Brown v. Board

THE CRISIS:
- NAACP magazine
- Edited by Du Bois
- Circulation peaked 100,000
- News, culture, politics
```

### Labor and Organizing

```
BLACK WORKERS:

A. PHILIP RANDOLPH (1889-1979):
- Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters
- Founded 1925
- First Black union recognized by
  major corporation (1937)
- Later March on Washington leader

CHALLENGES:
- Excluded from white unions
- Last hired, first fired
- Dangerous/difficult jobs
- Lower pay for same work

STRATEGIES:
- Form separate unions
- Press for inclusion
- Political pressure
- Community support

SIGNIFICANCE:
- Economic power
- Organizing experience
- Leadership development
- Foundation for later movements
```

## üéì Key Takeaways

```
1. Reconstruction showed what was possible
   - Black political power
   - Institution building
   - Destroyed by violence and betrayal

2. Jim Crow was comprehensive oppression
   - Legal segregation
   - Disenfranchisement
   - Racial terror
   - But never total control

3. Black communities built institutions
   - Churches, schools, businesses
   - HBCUs trained leaders
   - Mutual aid sustained community
   - Foundation for resistance

4. Multiple strategies for progress
   - Accommodation (Washington)
   - Protest and education (Du Bois)
   - Nationalism (Garvey)
   - Each had supporters

5. Great Migration transformed America
   - Millions moved North
   - Urban Black communities grew
   - New opportunities and challenges
   - Changed national culture

6. Harlem Renaissance celebrated Black culture
   - Literature, music, art
   - New Negro identity
   - International impact
   - Cultural foundation for civil rights
```
