### Research Methods

#### Types of Research
| Method | Description | Pros | Cons |
|--------|-------------|------|------|
| **Experiment** | Manipulate IV, measure DV | Establishes causation | Artificial setting |
| **Correlational** | Measure relationship | Natural setting | No causation |
| **Survey** | Self-report questionnaire | Large sample, efficient | Response bias |
| **Case Study** | In-depth individual study | Rich detail | Cannot generalize |
| **Naturalistic Observation** | Observe in natural setting | Ecological validity | No control |
| **Longitudinal** | Same people over time | Shows development | Time-consuming, attrition |
| **Cross-sectional** | Different age groups at once | Quick, efficient | Cohort effects |

#### Key Experimental Terms
- **Independent Variable (IV)**: What researcher manipulates
- **Dependent Variable (DV)**: What is measured
- **Confounding Variable**: Uncontrolled variable affecting results
- **Operational Definition**: Specific, measurable definition
- **Random Assignment**: Eliminates pre-existing differences
- **Random Selection**: Allows generalization to population
- **Control Group**: No treatment, baseline comparison
- **Experimental Group**: Receives treatment
- **Placebo Effect**: Improvement from expectations
- **Double-Blind**: Neither participants nor researchers know conditions

#### Correlation Coefficients
- **Range**: -1.00 to +1.00
- **Positive correlation**: Variables increase together (r = +0.80)
- **Negative correlation**: One increases, other decreases (r = -0.80)
- **Strength**: Closer to Â±1.00 = stronger relationship
- **REMEMBER**: Correlation â‰  Causation!

### Descriptive Statistics
| Measure | Definition | When to Use |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| **Mean** | Average | Normal distribution |
| **Median** | Middle value | Skewed distribution |
| **Mode** | Most frequent | Nominal data |
| **Range** | Highest - Lowest | Quick variability measure |
| **Standard Deviation** | Average distance from mean | Precise variability |
| **Normal Distribution** | Bell curve, mean = median = mode | Many psychological traits |

### Ethics in Research
- **Informed consent**: Participants know procedures and risks
- **Confidentiality**: Data kept private
- **Debriefing**: Explain study purpose afterward
- **Right to withdraw**: Can leave at any time
- **Deception**: Only when necessary, must debrief
- **IRB Approval**: Institutional Review Board reviews all research
- **Animal Research**: Must minimize suffering, justify use

### The Neuron

#### Structure
| Part | Function |
|------|----------|
| **Dendrites** | Receive messages from other neurons |
| **Cell Body (Soma)** | Contains nucleus, processes information |
| **Axon** | Transmits electrical signal away from cell body |
| **Myelin Sheath** | Insulates axon, speeds transmission |
| **Terminal Buttons** | Release neurotransmitters |
| **Synapse** | Gap between neurons |

#### Neural Communication Process
1. **Resting Potential**: -70mV, ready to fire
2. **Action Potential**: Threshold reached, Na+ rushes in (+40mV)
3. **All-or-None Law**: Neuron fires completely or not at all
4. **Refractory Period**: Brief rest before firing again
5. **Neurotransmitters Released**: From terminal buttons into synapse
6. **Receptor Binding**: Neurotransmitters bind to receptors
7. **Reuptake**: Excess neurotransmitters reabsorbed

### Sensation vs. Perception
- **Sensation**: Detection of physical energy by sensory organs
- **Perception**: Interpretation and organization of sensory information

### Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Absolute Threshold** | Minimum stimulus detected 50% of the time |
| **Difference Threshold (JND)** | Minimum difference between two stimuli |
| **Weber's Law** | JND is proportional to original stimulus |
| **Signal Detection Theory** | Detection depends on signal, noise, and psychological factors |
| **Sensory Adaptation** | Decreased sensitivity with constant stimulation |

### Vision

**Eye Structures**
| Structure | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **Cornea** | Bends light, focuses |
| **Pupil** | Opening that admits light |
| **Iris** | Colored muscle, controls pupil size |
| **Lens** | Fine-tunes focus (accommodation) |
| **Retina** | Contains photoreceptors |
| **Fovea** | Central focus, highest acuity |
| **Optic Nerve** | Transmits visual info to brain |
| **Blind Spot** | Where optic nerve exits (no receptors) |

**Photoreceptors**
| Type | Function | Location | Light Level |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Rods** | Peripheral, night vision | Edges of retina | Low light |
| **Cones** | Color, detail | Fovea | Bright light |

**Color Vision Theories**
- **Trichromatic Theory** (Young-Helmholtz): 3 cone types (red, green, blue)
- **Opponent-Process Theory** (Hering): Red-green, blue-yellow, black-white pairs

### Hearing (Audition)

**Ear Structures**
| Part | Structure | Function |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Outer Ear** | Pinna, ear canal | Collects sound waves |
| **Middle Ear** | Hammer, anvil, stirrup | Amplifies vibrations |
| **Inner Ear** | Cochlea | Converts to neural signal |
| **Inner Ear** | Hair cells | Transduce sound |

**Sound Properties**
| Property | Physical | Perceived |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Amplitude | Wave height | Loudness |
| Frequency | Wave speed (Hz) | Pitch |
| Complexity | Wave pattern | Timbre |

**Hearing Theories**
- **Place Theory**: Different pitches stimulate different cochlea locations
- **Frequency Theory**: Neural firing matches sound wave frequency
- **Volley Principle**: Neurons fire in teams for higher frequencies

### Other Senses

**Touch (Somatosensation)**
- Pressure, pain, temperature
- **Gate-Control Theory**: Spinal cord "gates" can open/close to pain signals

**Smell (Olfaction)**
- Only sense not routed through thalamus
- Direct connection to limbic system (emotion, memory)

**Taste (Gustation)**
- Five basic tastes: Sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami

**Kinesthesis**: Body position sense
**Vestibular Sense**: Balance (semicircular canals)

### Perception Principles

**Gestalt Principles**
| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Figure-Ground** | Distinguish object from background |
| **Proximity** | Group nearby objects |
| **Similarity** | Group similar objects |
| **Continuity** | Perceive smooth, continuous patterns |
| **Closure** | Fill in gaps to complete shapes |
| **Connectedness** | Group connected items |

**Depth Perception**
| Cue | Type | Description |
|-----|------|-------------|
| **Retinal Disparity** | Binocular | Different images from each eye |
| **Convergence** | Binocular | Eyes turn inward for close objects |
| **Relative Size** | Monocular | Smaller = farther |
| **Interposition** | Monocular | Overlapping objects |
| **Linear Perspective** | Monocular | Parallel lines converge |
| **Texture Gradient** | Monocular | Detail decreases with distance |
| **Motion Parallax** | Monocular | Close objects move faster |

**Perceptual Constancies**
- **Size Constancy**: Objects maintain perceived size
- **Shape Constancy**: Objects maintain perceived shape
- **Color Constancy**: Objects maintain perceived color

## ðŸ“š Unit 4: Learning

### Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

**Key Terms**
| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **UCS** | Unconditioned Stimulus | Food |
| **UCR** | Unconditioned Response | Salivation to food |
| **NS** | Neutral Stimulus | Bell (before conditioning) |
| **CS** | Conditioned Stimulus | Bell (after conditioning) |

**Processes**
| Process | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **Acquisition** | Learning the association |
| **Extinction** | CR weakens without UCS |
| **Spontaneous Recovery** | CR returns after rest |
| **Generalization** | Responding to similar stimuli |
| **Discrimination** | Distinguishing between stimuli |

**Famous Studies**
- **Pavlov's Dogs**: Bell â†’ Salivation
- **Little Albert** (Watson): Conditioned fear of white rat
- **Garcia's Taste Aversion**: One-trial learning, biological preparedness

### Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

**Law of Effect** (Thorndike): Behaviors followed by satisfying consequences are repeated

**Reinforcement and Punishment**
| Type | Effect | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Positive Reinforcement** | Add pleasant, increase behavior | Give treat for good grades |
| **Negative Reinforcement** | Remove unpleasant, increase behavior | Take aspirin to remove headache |
| **Positive Punishment** | Add unpleasant, decrease behavior | Spanking |
| **Negative Punishment** | Remove pleasant, decrease behavior | Take away phone |

**Schedules of Reinforcement**
| Schedule | Pattern | Response Rate | Resistance to Extinction |
|----------|---------|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Continuous** | Every response | Fast learning | Low |
| **Fixed-Ratio** | After set # responses | High, pause after | Moderate |
| **Variable-Ratio** | After average # responses | Very high, steady | Very high |
| **Fixed-Interval** | After set time | Increases near time | Low |
| **Variable-Interval** | After average time | Steady, moderate | High |

**Shaping**: Reinforce successive approximations

### Cognitive Learning

**Latent Learning** (Tolman): Learning without reinforcement, demonstrated when needed
**Insight Learning** (KÃ¶hler): Sudden understanding (aha! moment)

### Observational Learning (Bandura)

**Bobo Doll Study**: Children imitated aggressive models

**Four Processes**
1. **Attention**: Notice the model
2. **Retention**: Remember the behavior
3. **Reproduction**: Ability to perform
4. **Motivation**: Reason to imitate

**Mirror Neurons**: Fire when performing AND observing actions

### Forgetting

| Theory | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|

| **Proactive** | Old interferes with new |
| **Retroactive** | New interferes with old |
| **Motivated Forgetting** | Repression of painful memories |

### Memory Errors

- **Misinformation Effect** (Loftus): Post-event info alters memory
- **Source Monitoring Error**: Forget where you learned something
- **False Memories**: Remember events that didn't happen
- **Imagination Inflation**: Imagining makes it seem real

## ðŸ‘¥ Unit 9: Social Psychology

### Social Thinking

**Attribution Theory**: How we explain behavior
- **Dispositional**: Internal (personality, character)
- **Situational**: External (circumstances, environment)

**Fundamental Attribution Error**: Overestimate dispositional, underestimate situational (for others)

**Self-Serving Bias**: Attribute success to self, failure to situation

**Just-World Phenomenon**: Believe people get what they deserve

### Attitudes

**Cognitive Dissonance** (Festinger)
- Tension from inconsistent thoughts/behaviors
- Reduce by changing attitude or behavior
- $1 vs $20 study: $1 group changed attitude more

**Persuasion Routes**
- **Central Route**: Thoughtful consideration, lasting change
- **Peripheral Route**: Superficial cues, temporary change

**Foot-in-the-Door**: Small request â†’ Large request
**Door-in-the-Face**: Large request â†’ Small request

### Social Influence

**Conformity** (Asch)
- Line experiment: 75% conformed at least once
- Factors: Group size, unanimity, culture

**Normative Social Influence**: Conform to be liked
**Informational Social Influence**: Conform to be correct

**Obedience** (Milgram)
- 65% shocked to maximum (450V)
- Factors: Authority, proximity, legitimacy

**Stanford Prison Experiment** (Zimbardo)
- Role-playing led to abusive behavior
- Situational power over behavior

### Group Behavior

| Phenomenon | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Social Facilitation** | Perform better on easy tasks with audience |
| **Social Loafing** | Less effort in groups |
| **Deindividuation** | Loss of self-awareness in groups |
| **Group Polarization** | Group discussion strengthens initial views |
| **Groupthink** | Harmony overrides realistic thinking |

### Prejudice and Discrimination

- **Stereotype**: Generalized belief about a group
- **Prejudice**: Negative attitude toward a group
- **Discrimination**: Negative behavior toward a group

**Sources of Prejudice**
- In-group/Out-group bias
- Social categorization
- Scapegoat theory
- Just-world phenomenon

**Reducing Prejudice**
- Contact hypothesis: Equal status interaction
- Superordinate goals: Cooperation for common goal

### Aggression

**Biological Factors**
- Genetics, testosterone, amygdala, serotonin

**Psychological Factors**
- Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis
- Social learning (Bandura)

**Environmental Factors**
- Heat, crowding, alcohol

### Attraction

**Factors in Attraction**
| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Proximity** | Geographic closeness |
| **Mere Exposure Effect** | Familiarity increases liking |
| **Physical Attractiveness** | Beauty bias, halo effect |
| **Similarity** | Shared attitudes, interests |
| **Reciprocity** | We like those who like us |

### Love

**Sternberg's Triangular Theory**
- **Intimacy**: Emotional closeness
- **Passion**: Physical attraction
- **Commitment**: Decision to maintain relationship

**Consummate Love** = Intimacy + Passion + Commitment

### Prosocial Behavior

**Altruism**: Helping without expectation of reward

**Bystander Effect**: Less likely to help with more people present
- Diffusion of responsibility

**Factors Increasing Helping**
- Feeling guilty
- Good mood
- Similarity to victim
- Observing helpful models

**Key Phrases**
- "According to [psychologist]..."
- "This demonstrates..."
- "The concept of _____ explains..."
- "This is an example of..."

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Don't confuse similar concepts (reinforcement vs punishment)
- Don't forget negative examples (what something is NOT)
- Don't use circular definitions
