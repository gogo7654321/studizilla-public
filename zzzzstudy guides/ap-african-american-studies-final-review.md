## üìù Exam Preparation

### Source Analysis Skills

```
ANALYZING PRIMARY SOURCES:

1. SOURCING:
   - Who created this?
   - When and where?
   - Why was it created?
   - What's the context?

2. CLOSE READING:
   - What does it say?
   - What language is used?
   - What's the tone?
   - What assumptions?

3. CONTEXTUALIZATION:
   - What was happening then?
   - How does context shape meaning?
   - What would audience understand?

4. CORROBORATION:
   - Do other sources confirm?
   - What perspectives are missing?
   - How do sources differ?

5. EVALUATION:
   - What can we learn?
   - What are limitations?
   - How reliable is this?
```

### Writing Strategies

```
ARGUMENT DEVELOPMENT:

THESIS:
- Clear, arguable claim
- Addresses the question
- Sets up analysis

EVIDENCE:
- Specific examples
- Primary sources
- Historical details
- Quotes when relevant

ANALYSIS:
- Explain significance
- Make connections
- Show cause/effect
- Address complexity

ORGANIZATION:
- Logical structure
- Clear paragraphs
- Transitions
- Introduction and conclusion

PERSPECTIVE:
- Multiple viewpoints
- Complexity and nuance
- Avoid oversimplification
```

### Common Question Types

```
QUESTION APPROACHES:

CONTINUITY AND CHANGE:
- What stayed the same?
- What changed?
- Why did change occur?
- Example: Compare resistance in
  antebellum and Jim Crow eras

CAUSATION:
- Why did X happen?
- What were consequences?
- Multiple causes
- Example: Causes of Great Migration

COMPARISON:
- Similarities and differences
- Why do they matter?
- Example: Washington vs. Du Bois

HISTORICAL ARGUMENT:
- Evaluate a claim
- Use evidence
- Consider counterarguments
- Example: "The Civil Rights Movement
  achieved its goals" - agree/disagree?
```

## üìö Study Tips

```
BEFORE THE EXAM:

1. REVIEW TIMELINES
   - Know chronology
   - Understand what led to what
   - Identify turning points

2. CONNECT THEMES
   - See patterns across eras
   - Resistance, institution building
   - Strategic debates recurring

3. KNOW KEY FIGURES
   - Who they were
   - What they contributed
   - How they connect

4. PRACTICE SOURCE ANALYSIS
   - Work with primary sources
   - Ask sourcing questions
   - Make arguments from evidence

5. WRITE PRACTICE ESSAYS
   - Time yourself
   - Use specific evidence
   - Make clear arguments

DURING THE EXAM:

1. READ CAREFULLY
   - Understand the question
   - Address all parts
   - Stay focused

2. PLAN BEFORE WRITING
   - Outline your argument
   - Organize evidence
   - Know your thesis

3. BE SPECIFIC
   - Use names, dates, details
   - Don't generalize
   - Show you know the content

4. ANALYZE, DON'T JUST DESCRIBE
   - Explain significance
   - Make connections
   - Answer "so what?"

5. MANAGE TIME
   - Don't spend too long on one question
   - Leave time to review
   - Write something for everything
```
