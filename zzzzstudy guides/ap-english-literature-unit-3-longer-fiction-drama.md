## ğŸ“š The Novel

### Defining the Novel

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Length** | Extended narrative (usually 40,000+ words) |
| **Prose** | Written in continuous sentences |
| **Fictional** | Imagined characters and events |
| **Complex** | Multiple characters, subplots, themes |
| **Realistic** | Usually represents believable world |

### Types of Novels

| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **Bildungsroman** | Coming-of-age, education | *Jane Eyre*, *Great Expectations* |
| **Picaresque** | Episodic adventures of rogue | *Don Quixote*, *Huckleberry Finn* |
| **Epistolary** | Letters, documents | *Frankenstein*, *The Color Purple* |
| **Gothic** | Dark, mysterious, supernatural | *Wuthering Heights*, *Rebecca* |
| **Social novel** | Critiques society | *1984*, *The Jungle* |
| **Psychological** | Interior consciousness | *Mrs. Dalloway*, *Crime and Punishment* |
| **Historical** | Set in past, real events | *A Tale of Two Cities*, *Beloved* |
| **Modernist** | Experimental, fragmented | *Ulysses*, *The Sound and the Fury* |

### Novel Narrative Techniques

| Technique | Description | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **First person** | "I" narrator | Intimacy, limited perspective |
| **Third limited** | One consciousness | Balance of access and distance |
| **Third omniscient** | All-knowing | God-like view, irony possible |
| **Stream of consciousness** | Interior thoughts flow | Psychological depth |
| **Free indirect discourse** | Blends narrator/character | Irony, intimacy |
| **Multiple narrators** | Various perspectives | Complexity, unreliability |
| **Frame narrative** | Story within story | Distance, multiple levels |

### Free Indirect Discourse

| Direct | Indirect | Free Indirect |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| She thought, "I am happy." | She thought that she was happy. | She was happy. |
| He said, "This is wrong." | He said that it was wrong. | This was wrong! |

Effect: Blends narrator's voice with character's consciousness; allows irony and intimacy simultaneously.

## ğŸ­ Drama

### Defining Drama

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Performance** | Written to be performed |
| **Dialogue** | Characters speak directly |
| **Stage directions** | Instructions for performance |
| **Visual** | Setting, costumes, movement |
| **Live** | Immediate, present-tense |

### Elements of Drama

| Element | Description | Function |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Dialogue** | Characters' speech | Reveals character, advances plot |
| **Monologue** | Extended speech to others | Reveals thought, persuades |
| **Soliloquy** | Speech to self/audience | Reveals inner thoughts |
| **Aside** | Brief comment to audience | Irony, secret information |
| **Stage directions** | Performance instructions | Setting, movement, tone |

### Dramatic Structure

| Classical (Aristotle) | Five-Act (Shakespeare) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| Beginning, Middle, End | Act I: Exposition |
| Unity of time, place, action | Act II: Rising action |
| Single plot | Act III: Climax/Turning point |
| | Act IV: Falling action |
| | Act V: Resolution/Catastrophe |

### Types of Drama

| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **Tragedy** | Noble hero, fatal flaw, downfall | *Hamlet*, *Oedipus Rex* |
| **Comedy** | Humor, happy ending, marriage | *A Midsummer Night's Dream* |
| **Tragicomedy** | Mix of tragic and comic | *The Winter's Tale* |
| **History** | Based on historical events | *Henry V*, *Richard III* |
| **Absurdist** | Meaninglessness, illogic | *Waiting for Godot* |
| **Realist** | Everyday life, social issues | *A Doll's House*, *Death of a Salesman* |

## ğŸ­ Tragedy

### Aristotelian Tragedy

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Hamartia** | Fatal flaw or error | Oedipus's pride, Hamlet's indecision |
| **Hubris** | Excessive pride | Oedipus challenges fate |
| **Peripeteia** | Reversal of fortune | Discovery of true identity |
| **Anagnorisis** | Recognition/discovery | Oedipus learns the truth |
| **Catharsis** | Emotional purgation | Audience's pity and fear released |

### Tragic Hero Characteristics

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Noble stature** | High-born, admirable |
| **Tragic flaw** | Weakness leading to downfall |
| **Reversal** | Fall from grace |
| **Self-awareness** | Recognizes own responsibility |
| **Evokes emotions** | Audience feels pity and fear |
| **Fate** | Larger forces at work |

### Modern Tragedy

| Playwright | Work | Innovation |
|------------|------|------------|
| **Arthur Miller** | *Death of a Salesman* | Common man as tragic hero |
| **Eugene O'Neill** | *Long Day's Journey* | Family tragedy, realism |
| **Tennessee Williams** | *A Streetcar Named Desire* | Psychological tragedy |
| **August Wilson** | *Fences* | African American tragedy |

### Miller's "Tragedy and the Common Man"

| Principle | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Common hero** | Tragedy not limited to nobility |
| **Dignity** | Every person's dignity worthy |
| **Willingness to fight** | Hero struggles against circumstances |
| **Personal stakes** | Individual's sense of self at stake |

| Five-Act Structure | Function |
|--------------------|----------|
| **Act I: Exposition** | Setting, characters, conflict introduced |
| **Act II: Rising Action** | Complications develop |
| **Act III: Climax** | Turning point, highest tension |
| **Act IV: Falling Action** | Consequences unfold |
| **Act V: Resolution** | Conclusion, restoration of order |

| Act | Content | Proportion |

## ğŸ¯ Practice Prompts

### Literary Analysis Prompts

**Prompt 1:** Many works of literature feature characters who undergo significant transformation. Choose a novel or play in which a character changes significantly. Then, in a well-organized essay, analyze how that transformation contributes to an interpretation of the work as a whole.

**Prompt 2:** In many works of literature, a character's private moral struggle parallels a larger public conflict. Choose a work in which this occurs. Then, in a well-organized essay, analyze how the character's private struggle relates to the larger conflict and what this relationship contributes to the work's meaning.

**Prompt 3:** Many works of literature use setting to convey meaning. Choose a novel or play in which setting is significant. Then, in a well-organized essay, analyze how the author uses setting to contribute to the work's meaning.

**Prompt 4:** The climax of a literary work often marks a character's most significant moment of insight or change. Choose a work in which the climax reveals something essential about a character. Analyze how this moment contributes to the work's meaning.

## ğŸ¨ Advanced Narrative Techniques

### Narrative Distance

| Type | Description | Effect | Example |
|------|-------------|--------|---------|
| **Close** | Intimate access to consciousness | Reader identifies with character | Stream of consciousness |
| **Distant** | Detached observation | Reader judges character | Ironic narration |
| **Variable** | Shifts throughout | Creates complexity | Free indirect discourse |

### Narrative Time Manipulation

| Technique | Definition | Effect | Example |
|-----------|------------|--------|---------|
| **Analepsis (flashback)** | Events before narrative present | Reveals backstory | *Beloved*'s memory fragments |
| **Prolepsis (flash-forward)** | Events after narrative present | Creates suspense/irony | "Later he would regret..." |
| **Ellipsis** | Omitting time | Focuses on key moments | Years pass between chapters |
| **Scene** | Real-time narration | Dramatic intensity | Dialogue-heavy passages |
| **Summary** | Compressed time | Covers long periods | "Five years passed" |

### Point of View Complexity

| POV Technique | Description | Example |
|---------------|-------------|---------|
| **Shifting focalization** | Different consciousnesses | *Beloved*, *As I Lay Dying* |
| **Embedded narration** | Story within story | *Wuthering Heights* frame |
| **Retrospective narration** | Older narrator on younger self | *Great Expectations* |
| **Collective voice** | "We" as narrator | "A Rose for Emily" |

## ğŸ“– Extended Passage Analysis Examples

### Example 1: Novel Opening Analysis

**Passage (from *Pride and Prejudice* by Jane Austen):**
> "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Irony** | "Truth universally acknowledged" | Satirizes social assumptions |
| **Free indirect discourse** | Whose voice? | Blends narrator with neighborhood gossip |
| **Social commentary** | Marriage as economic transaction | Sets up central theme |
| **Tone** | Witty, detached | Establishes Austen's ironic mode |

**Sample thesis:** Austen's famous opening sentence establishes her ironic method by ventriloquizing neighborhood gossip as "universal truth," immediately exposing the mercenary calculations beneath Regency courtship.

### Example 2: Drama Soliloquy Analysis

**Passage (from *Hamlet* III.i):**
> "To be, or not to be, that is the question:
> Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
> The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
> Or to take arms against a sea of troubles,
> And by opposing end them."

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Central question** | Existence vs. non-existence | Philosophical depth |
| **Mixed metaphor** | "Arms against a sea" | Futility of action |
| **Rhythm** | Iambic pentameter with variations | Emotional intensity |
| **Dramatic irony** | Audience knows what Hamlet doesn't | Deepens tragedy |

**Sample thesis:** Hamlet's soliloquy reveals his paralysis through its very structure: the balanced antitheses ("to be, or not to be," "suffer" or "take arms") formally enact the indecision that prevents action.

### Example 3: Modern Drama Analysis

**Passage (from *Death of a Salesman*):**
> "I'm not a dime a dozen! I am Willy Loman, and you are Biff Loman!"

| Element | Observation | Analysis |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Desperation** | Assertion of identity | Names as claims to significance |
| **Tragedy** | Common man's dignity | Miller's democratic tragedy |
| **Irony** | Names are ordinary | Everyman quality |
| **Theme** | American Dream failure | Salesman's crisis of worth |

**Sample thesis:** Willy's desperate invocation of his own name exposes the tragic gap between his inflated self-image and his anonymous fungibility in capitalist America, where identity itself becomes a commodity.

5. Connection to thesis

2. What is Linda demanding?
