## üéØ Sub-Theme 1: Slavery in the American Colonies

### Development of Racial Slavery

```
TIMELINE OF DEVELOPMENT:

1619:
First Africans arrive in Virginia
Status ambiguous (indentured?)

1640s-1660s:
Virginia codifies racial slavery
"Hereditary heathen" laws
Children follow mother's status

1676:
Bacon's Rebellion
Increased racial division after

1705:
Virginia Slave Codes
Comprehensive legal framework

1787:
Constitution and slavery
Three-Fifths Compromise
Fugitive Slave Clause

The shift from varied forms of unfree
labor to race-based hereditary slavery
was a PROCESS, not inevitable.
```

### Legal Structure of Slavery

```
SLAVE CODES (varied by state):

PROPERTY STATUS:
- Enslaved people = chattel property
- Could be bought, sold, inherited
- No legal personhood
- No right to contract or sue

RESTRICTIONS:
- No reading or writing (in most states)
- No assembly without whites
- No weapons
- No travel without pass
- No testimony against whites

PUNISHMENTS:
- Whipping
- Branding
- Mutilation
- Sale away from family
- Execution for rebellion

"PRIVILEGES" (could be revoked):
- Some could hire out
- Some could grow food
- Some practiced trades
- Urban enslaved had more mobility
```

### Regional Variations

| Region | Economy | Characteristics |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Upper South | Tobacco, mixed | Smaller farms, more hiring out |
| Deep South | Cotton, sugar, rice | Large plantations, harsh conditions |
| Urban | Domestic, trades | More autonomy, some literacy |
| Border States | Mixed | Both enslaved and free Black populations |

## üî• Sub-Theme 2: Daily Life Under Slavery

### Work and Labor

```
AGRICULTURAL LABOR:

GANG SYSTEM:
- Groups working together
- Overseer supervision
- Common in cotton, sugar
- Brutal pace

TASK SYSTEM:
- Assigned daily tasks
- Some autonomy when done
- Common in rice cultivation
- Allowed extra production

DOMESTIC WORK:
- Cooking, cleaning, childcare
- Close to enslavers (could be worse)
- Sexual exploitation risk
- Sometimes learned to read

SKILLED LABOR:
- Blacksmithing, carpentry
- More autonomy
- Could be hired out
- Some saved to purchase freedom
```

### Family and Community

```
FAMILY UNDER SLAVERY:

THREATS:
- No legal marriage
- Sale could separate families
- Children belonged to enslaver
- Sexual exploitation
- Forced "breeding"

RESILIENCE:
- Fictive kinship networks
- Community childrearing
- Extended family emphasis
- Naming practices (African traditions)
- Maintaining connections after sale

COMMUNITY:
- Shared religious practices
- Music and storytelling
- Mutual aid
- Secret communication
- Collective memory
```

### Religion and Culture

```
AFRICAN AMERICAN CHRISTIANITY:

DEVELOPMENT:
- Syncretism with African traditions
- Emphasis on liberation (Exodus)
- Secret worship ("hush harbors")
- Call and response
- Spirituals

KEY THEMES:
- God of the oppressed
- Moses as liberator
- Heaven as freedom
- Judgment for slaveholders
- Hope and endurance

SPIRITUALS:
"Steal Away to Jesus"
"Go Down Moses"
"Wade in the Water"
"Follow the Drinking Gourd"

These often contained coded
messages about escape routes.
```

## ‚úä Sub-Theme 3: Resistance to Slavery

### Everyday Resistance

```
COVERT RESISTANCE:

WORK SLOWDOWNS:
- Working slowly
- Breaking tools
- Feigning illness
- "Misunderstanding" orders

SABOTAGE:
- Damaging crops
- Poisoning food
- Setting fires
- Harming livestock

CULTURAL RESISTANCE:
- Maintaining African practices
- Teaching children history
- Coded songs and stories
- Preserving dignity

RUNNING AWAY:
- Short-term "truancy"
- Long-term escape attempts
- Underground Railroad
- Maroon communities
```

### Armed Resistance and Rebellions

```
MAJOR REBELLIONS:

STONO REBELLION (1739):
- South Carolina
- ~20 enslaved people initially
- Headed toward Spanish Florida
- Suppressed with violence
- Led to stricter slave codes

GABRIEL'S CONSPIRACY (1800):
- Virginia
- Planned large-scale uprising
- Betrayed before execution
- Gabriel executed
- Increased surveillance

NAT TURNER'S REBELLION (1831):
- Southampton County, Virginia
- Killed ~60 white people
- Turner captured, executed
- Massive white retaliation
- Stricter laws nationwide

SIGNIFICANCE:
- Demonstrated desire for freedom
- Enslaved not passive victims
- White fear of Black uprising
- Led to harsher restrictions
```

### The Underground Railroad

```
STRUCTURE:

"CONDUCTORS":
- Guides who led escapees
- Harriet Tubman most famous
- Local guides crucial
- Free Blacks and white allies

"STATIONS":
- Safe houses
- Churches
- Businesses
- Private homes

"PASSENGERS":
- Those escaping slavery
- ~100,000 escaped via network
- Most went to North or Canada

ROUTES:
- Often traveled at night
- Used the North Star
- Rivers and swamps for cover
- Varied by region

HARRIET TUBMAN (1822-1913):
- Escaped slavery herself
- Made ~13 rescue missions
- Freed ~70 people directly
- Never lost a passenger
- Later served as Civil War spy
```

## üì¢ Sub-Theme 4: Free Black Communities

### Free Black Population

```
SOURCES OF FREEDOM:

SELF-PURCHASE:
- Saving money through extra work
- Hiring out arrangements
- Could take years or decades

MANUMISSION:
- Owner granted freedom
- Sometimes in wills
- Revolutionary era increase
- Became harder after 1800

BIRTH TO FREE MOTHER:
- Children followed mother's status
- Free Black families grew

ESCAPE:
- Underground Railroad
- Northern states
- Canada
- Mexico


BY 1860:
- ~500,000 free Blacks in US
- 250,000 in the South
- 250,000 in the North
- Largest communities in cities
```

### Free Black Institutions

```
CHURCHES:
- African Methodist Episcopal (AME)
- Richard Allen, founder (1816)
- African Baptist churches
- Community centers
- Schools often attached

SCHOOLS:
- African Free Schools
- Private academies
- Some college attendance
- Literacy as resistance

MUTUAL AID SOCIETIES:
- Insurance and death benefits
- Support for sick and elderly
- Economic cooperation
- Free African Society (1787)

NEWSPAPERS:
- Freedom's Journal (1827)
- First Black newspaper
- The North Star (Frederick Douglass)
- Abolitionist platforms
```

### Challenges for Free Blacks

```
RESTRICTIONS:

LEGAL:
- Voting rights limited
- Testimony restrictions
- Curfews
- Bond requirements
- Could be kidnapped into slavery

SOCIAL:
- Segregation
- Employment discrimination
- Violence and harassment
- Housing restrictions
- Educational barriers

EXAMPLES:
- Black Codes in Northern states
- Fugitive Slave Act dangers
- Draft Riots (1863)
- Constant threat of kidnapping
```

## üìú Sub-Theme 5: The Abolitionist Movement

### Early Abolitionism

```
ORIGINS:

QUAKERS:
- Early opponents of slavery
- Gradual emancipation advocacy
- Anthony Benezet

REVOLUTIONARY ERA:
- "All men are created equal"
- Contradiction with slavery
- Northern states abolish gradually
- Vermont (1777), Massachusetts (1783)

COLONIZATION MOVEMENT:
- American Colonization Society (1816)
- Send free Blacks to Africa
- Liberia founded
- Most Black Americans opposed
- Gradual approach criticized
```

### Radical Abolitionism

```
KEY FIGURES:

WILLIAM LLOYD GARRISON (1805-1879):
- The Liberator newspaper (1831)
- Immediate abolition demand
- Moral suasion approach
- American Anti-Slavery Society
- "I will not retreat a single inch"

FREDERICK DOUGLASS (1818-1895):
- Escaped slavery
- Powerful orator and writer
- Narrative (1845)
- The North Star newspaper
- Later supported political action

HARRIET BEECHER STOWE (1811-1896):
- Uncle Tom's Cabin (1852)
- Massive cultural impact
- "The little woman who wrote the
  book that started this great war"

SOJOURNER TRUTH (c.1797-1883):
- Escaped slavery
- Preacher and lecturer
- "Ain't I a Woman?" speech
- Women's rights advocate
```

### Black Abolitionists

```
CENTRAL ROLE:

BLACK LEADERSHIP:
- Not just white-led movement
- Black voices essential
- Fugitive narratives powerful
- Speaking from experience

KEY FIGURES:

DAVID WALKER (1796-1830):
- Walker's Appeal (1829)
- Called for armed resistance
- Radical challenge to slavery
- Distributed in the South

MARIA STEWART (1803-1879):
- First American woman to lecture
  publicly to mixed audiences
- Religious and political messages
- Education advocate

HENRY HIGHLAND GARNET (1815-1882):
- "Address to the Slaves" (1843)
- Called for resistance
- Pan-African connections
- Minister and diplomat

MARTIN DELANY (1812-1885):
- Black nationalism
- Emigration advocate
- First Black field officer in US Army
```

## ‚öîÔ∏è Sub-Theme 6: Road to Civil War

### Sectional Crisis

```
KEY EVENTS:

MISSOURI COMPROMISE (1820):
- Missouri = slave state
- Maine = free state
- 36¬∞30' line

COMPROMISE OF 1850:
- California = free state
- Fugitive Slave Act strengthened
- Popular sovereignty in territories

KANSAS-NEBRASKA ACT (1854):
- Popular sovereignty
- "Bleeding Kansas" violence
- Republican Party formed

DRED SCOTT (1857):
- Blacks not citizens
- Congress can't restrict slavery
- Outraged the North

JOHN BROWN'S RAID (1859):
- Harper's Ferry, Virginia
- Attempted to start uprising
- Captured and executed
- Martyrdom in North
```

### Black Perspectives on the Coming War

```
DEBATES IN BLACK COMMUNITIES:

STAY AND FIGHT:
- Frederick Douglass initially
- The nation must change
- Too many connections here
- Fight for inclusion

EMIGRATION:
- Martin Delany
- Leave for Africa or Caribbean
- No future in racist America
- Pan-African connections

ARMED RESISTANCE:
- Support for John Brown
- Self-defense necessary
- Harriet Tubman connections
- Revolutionary tradition

POLITICAL ACTION:
- Some supported Republicans
- Vote where possible
- Legal challenges
- Petition and protest
```

## üîî Sub-Theme 7: The Civil War and Emancipation

### African Americans and the War

```
EARLY WAR (1861-1862):

UNION POLICY:
- Initially not about slavery
- Refused Black enlistment
- Returned escaped enslaved people
- "Contraband" policy begins

BLACK PRESSURE:
- Frederick Douglass advocated
- "Men of Color, To Arms!"
- Volunteered anyway
- Pushed Lincoln

CONFEDERATE USE:
- Enslaved built fortifications
- Worked in factories
- Some impressed as laborers
- Key to Confederate economy
```

### Emancipation

```
PATH TO FREEDOM:

CONFISCATION ACTS (1861-1862):
- Enslaved used by Confederacy
  could be seized
- Beginning of federal action

EMANCIPATION PROCLAMATION (1863):
- January 1, 1863
- Freed enslaved in rebel states
- Did NOT free in border states
- War measure, not moral act
- Made war about freedom

THIRTEENTH AMENDMENT (1865):
- Abolished slavery entirely
- Ratified December 1865
- Constitutional change
- Applied to all states

SIGNIFICANCE:
"Forever free" - but what did
freedom mean? The struggle continued.
```

### Black Military Service

```
UNITED STATES COLORED TROOPS:

NUMBERS:
- ~180,000 Black soldiers
- ~10% of Union Army
- ~20,000 Black sailors

TREATMENT:
- Lower pay initially
- White officers often
- Segregated units
- Dangerous assignments
- Confederate threats

HEROISM:
- Fort Wagner (54th Massachusetts)
- Battle of the Crater
- Milliken's Bend
- Medal of Honor recipients

SIGNIFICANCE:
- Proved capability
- Earned claim to citizenship
- Changed attitudes (some)
- Pride and dignity
- "Once let the black man get upon
  his person the brass letters U.S."
  - Frederick Douglass
```

## üìñ Sub-Theme 8: Key Documents and Sources

### Slave Narratives

```
IMPORTANT NARRATIVES:

FREDERICK DOUGLASS:
Narrative of the Life of Frederick
Douglass, an American Slave (1845)
- Most famous narrative
- Literary achievement
- Political argument
- Multiple editions

HARRIET JACOBS:
Incidents in the Life of a Slave
Girl (1861)
- Female perspective
- Sexual exploitation focus
- Linda Brent pseudonym
- Complex publication history

SOLOMON NORTHUP:
Twelve Years a Slave (1853)
- Free man kidnapped
- Detailed description
- Basis for 2013 film

WPA NARRATIVES (1930s):
- Interviews with formerly enslaved
- 2,300+ narratives
- Elderly interviewees
- Complex source issues
```

### Primary Source Analysis

```
QUESTIONS TO ASK:

1. AUTHOR'S PERSPECTIVE:
   - Who wrote this?
   - What was their position?
   - What are their interests?

2. AUDIENCE:
   - Who was this for?
   - What reaction sought?
   - How might that shape content?

3. CONTEXT:
   - When was this created?
   - What was happening then?
   - How does context matter?

4. CONTENT:
   - What does it say?
   - What does it leave out?
   - What can we learn?

5. CORROBORATION:
   - Do other sources confirm?
   - What's unique here?
   - What's typical?
```

## üéì Key Takeaways

```
1. Slavery was systematic oppression
   - Legal structure
   - Economic system
   - Social hierarchy
   - Violence and control

2. Enslaved people resisted constantly
   - Everyday resistance
   - Armed rebellion
   - Escape and Underground Railroad
   - Cultural preservation

3. Free Black communities built institutions
   - Churches
   - Schools
   - Newspapers
   - Mutual aid societies

4. Black abolitionists were essential
   - Not just white-led movement
   - Frederick Douglass, Sojourner Truth
   - Fugitive narratives
   - Pressure for radical action

5. Emancipation came through struggle
   - Black pressure crucial
   - Military service demonstrated
   - Constitutional change
   - But freedom's meaning still contested
```
