### Unit 1: The Global Tapestry (c. 1200-1450)

#### Key Concepts
- **Diverse states** existed across regions with varying political structures
- **Trade networks** connected distant regions (Silk Road, Indian Ocean, Trans-Saharan)
- **Religions spread** through trade and conquest

#### Major Empires & States

| Region | Empire/State | Key Features |
|--------|--------------|--------------|
| East Asia | Song Dynasty China | Neo-Confucianism, economic revolution, technology |
| East Asia | Yuan Dynasty (Mongols) | Unified trade, Pax Mongolica |
| South Asia | Delhi Sultanate | Muslim rule in India, blend of cultures |
| Southeast Asia | Khmer Empire | Hindu-Buddhist syncretism, Angkor Wat |
| Middle East | Abbasid Caliphate | Islamic Golden Age, Baghdad center |
| Africa | Mali Empire | Trans-Saharan trade, Mansa Musa |
| Africa | Great Zimbabwe | Gold trade, stone architecture |
| Europe | Byzantine Empire | Orthodox Christianity, preserved Roman culture |
| Americas | Aztec Empire | Tribute system, Tenochtitlan |
| Americas | Inca Empire | Road system, mit'a labor, no written language |

#### Trade Networks in 1200-1450

**Silk Road (Overland)**
- Connected China to Mediterranean
- Spread Buddhism, Islam, technologies
- Mongol Empire facilitated "Pax Mongolica"
- Caravanserais provided rest stops

**Indian Ocean Trade**
- Monsoon winds dictated sailing seasons
- Connected East Africa, Arabia, India, Southeast Asia, China
- Spread Islam, created Swahili culture
- Dhows were primary vessels

**Trans-Saharan Trade**
- Gold, salt, slaves exchanged
- Camels essential for desert crossing
- Spread Islam to West Africa
- Cities: Timbuktu, Gao, Djenne

**Mediterranean Trade**
- Italian city-states (Venice, Genoa) dominated
- Connected Europe to Asian goods
- Crusades increased trade interest

### Unit 2: Networks of Exchange (c. 1200-1450)

#### Key Concepts
- **Mongol Empire** created largest land empire, facilitated trade
- **Cross-cultural exchanges** of ideas, technologies, diseases
- **Environmental effects** including Black Death spread

#### The Mongol Empire

**Genghis Khan's Conquests (1206-1227)**
- United Mongol tribes
- Conquered northern China, Central Asia, Persia
- Brutal military tactics but religious tolerance

**Pax Mongolica (1250s-1350s)**
- Unified trade across Eurasia
- Protected merchants on Silk Road
- Spread technologies: gunpowder, compass, printing

**Mongol Khanates After Division:**
| Khanate | Region | Key Features |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| Yuan Dynasty | China | Kublai Khan, Marco Polo visited |
| Chagatai Khanate | Central Asia | Controlled Silk Road |
| Ilkhanate | Persia/Middle East | Eventually converted to Islam |
| Golden Horde | Russia/Eastern Europe | Dominated Russian principalities |

#### Effects of Mongol Rule
- **Positive**: Trade flourished, cultural exchange, meritocracy
- **Negative**: Mass destruction, depopulation, disease spread

#### The Black Death (1347-1351)
- Originated in Central Asia, spread via Mongol trade routes
- Killed 30-60% of European population
- **Effects**:
  - Labor shortage â†’ higher wages
  - Decline of feudalism

  - Economic disruption

### Unit 3: Land-Based Empires (c. 1450-1750)

#### Key Concepts
- **Gunpowder empires** emerged using new military technology
- **Centralized bureaucracies** administered vast territories
- **Religious and ethnic diversity** managed through various policies

#### Major Land-Based Empires

**Ottoman Empire (1299-1922)**
- Capital: Constantinople (Istanbul) after 1453
- **Military**: Janissaries (enslaved soldiers), gunpowder weapons
- **Government**: Sultan with devshirme system, millet system
- **Religion**: Sunni Islam, tolerated other religions
- Key rulers: Mehmed II, Suleiman the Magnificent
- **Economy**: Controlled trade routes, collected tribute

**Safavid Empire (1501-1736)**
- Capital: Isfahan
- **Religion**: Shia Islam (state religion)
- **Rivalry**: Constant conflict with Sunni Ottomans
- Key rulers: Shah Abbas I
- **Economy**: Silk trade, artistic patronage
- **Decline**: Afghan invasion, internal weakness

**Mughal Empire (1526-1857)**
- Founded by Babur (descendant of Mongols)
- **Religion**: Muslim rulers over Hindu majority
- **Akbar's policies**: Religious tolerance, abolished jizya, Din-i Ilahi
- **Architecture**: Taj Mahal, blended Islamic and Hindu styles
- Key rulers: Babur, Akbar, Shah Jahan, Aurangzeb
- **Decline**: Aurangzeb's intolerance, regional rebellions

**Qing Dynasty China (1644-1912)**
- Manchu conquest of Ming Dynasty
- **Government**: Maintained Chinese bureaucracy, examination system
- **Expansion**: Tibet, Taiwan, Central Asia conquered
- **Trade**: Limited European contact (Canton system)
- **Culture**: Manchu queue required, but Chinese culture preserved
- Key rulers: Kangxi, Qianlong

**Russian Empire (expanding)**
- **Peter the Great (1682-1725)**: Westernization, built St. Petersburg
- **Catherine the Great (1762-1796)**: Expanded territory, Enlightened absolutism
- Serfdom remained intact
- Expansion into Siberia, Central Asia

**Tokugawa Japan (1603-1868)**
- Unified Japan after civil war
- **Isolation policy** (sakoku): Limited foreign contact
- **Daimyo system**: Feudal lords controlled regions
- **Economy**: Agricultural productivity, merchant class grew
- **Culture**: Kabuki, ukiyo-e flourished

#### Comparison: Gunpowder Empires

| Feature | Ottoman | Safavid | Mughal |
|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| Religion | Sunni Islam | Shia Islam | Sunni Islam |
| Treatment of minorities | Millet system | Forced Shia conversion | Varied (Akbar tolerant, Aurangzeb not) |
| Military | Janissaries | Qizilbash warriors | Mansabdar system |
| Art/Architecture | SÃ¼leymaniye Mosque | Isfahan architecture | Taj Mahal |
| Decline factors | Military defeats, corruption | Afghan invasion | Religious conflict, regionalism |

### Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections (c. 1450-1750)

#### Key Concepts
- **European maritime exploration** connected all world regions
- **Columbian Exchange** transformed global ecology and economies
- **Colonial empires** established systems of exploitation

#### European Maritime Expansion

**Motivations (God, Gold, Glory)**
- **Economic**: Direct access to Asian spices, gold
- **Religious**: Spread Christianity, crusading spirit
- **Political**: National prestige, competition
- **Technological**: Better ships (caravels), navigation tools

**Key Voyages**
| Explorer | Sponsor | Achievement | Year |
|----------|---------|-------------|------|
| Dias | Portugal | Rounded Cape of Good Hope | 1488 |
| Columbus | Spain | Reached Americas | 1492 |
| da Gama | Portugal | Reached India by sea | 1498 |
| Magellan | Spain | First circumnavigation | 1519-1522 |

#### The Columbian Exchange

**From Americas to Old World:**
- Crops: Potatoes, corn (maize), tomatoes, cacao, tobacco
- Animals: Turkeys
- Diseases: Syphilis (debated)

**From Old World to Americas:**
- Crops: Wheat, sugar, coffee, bananas
- Animals: Horses, cattle, pigs, sheep
- Diseases: Smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza

**Effects:**
- **Population collapse**: 90% of indigenous Americans died from disease
- **Agricultural revolution**: New crops increased global population
- **Labor systems**: African slavery replaced indigenous labor
- **Environmental changes**: Deforestation, new ecosystems

#### Colonial Systems in the Americas

**Spanish Empire**
- **Encomienda**: Land grants with indigenous labor
- **Hacienda**: Large agricultural estates
- **Mita**: Forced labor in mines (especially PotosÃ­ silver)
- **Casta system**: Racial hierarchy
- **Viceroyalties**: Administrative divisions

**Portuguese Brazil**
- Sugar plantations dominant
- Heavy reliance on African slave labor
- Less rigid racial hierarchy than Spanish colonies

**British North America**
- Settlement colonies (families migrated)
- Indentured servitude â†’ African slavery
- Representative assemblies (limited self-governance)
- Less racial mixing than Iberian colonies

**French America**
- Fur trade in Canada
- Sugar plantations in Caribbean
- Alliance with indigenous peoples for trade

#### The Atlantic Slave Trade

**Triangle Trade:**
1. Europe â†’ Africa: Manufactured goods, weapons
2. Africa â†’ Americas: Enslaved Africans (Middle Passage)
3. Americas â†’ Europe: Sugar, tobacco, cotton, silver

**Scale**: 12-15 million Africans transported (1500s-1800s)

**Effects on Africa:**
- Depopulation of regions
- Increased warfare for captives
- Economic disruption
- Some kingdoms benefited (Dahomey, Asante)

**Effects on Americas:**
- Plantation economy developed
- African cultural influences (music, religion, food)
- Racial slavery institutionalized

**Origins in Britain:**
- Agricultural revolution freed labor
- Coal and iron deposits
- Capital from trade/colonies
- Stable government, property rights
- Rivers for transportation
- Entrepreneurial culture

### Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (c. 1750-1900)

#### Key Concepts
- **New imperialism** driven by industrial needs
- **Nationalism** reshaped political boundaries
- **Migration** increased globally
- **Reform movements** addressed social issues

#### New Imperialism (1870s-1914)

**Motivations:**
- **Economic**: Raw materials, markets for manufactured goods
- **Strategic**: Naval bases, control of routes
- **Ideological**: "Civilizing mission," Social Darwinism, racism
- **Technological**: Weapons, medicine (quinine), transportation

**The Scramble for Africa (1884-1914)**
- Berlin Conference (1884-85) set rules for partition
- By 1914, only Ethiopia and Liberia independent
- Arbitrary borders ignored ethnic/linguistic groups

**Major Colonial Powers:**
| Power | Key Colonies |
|-------|--------------|
| Britain | India, Egypt, South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya |
| France | Algeria, West Africa, Indochina |
| Germany | Namibia, Tanzania, Cameroon |
| Belgium | Congo (brutal exploitation) |
| Italy | Libya, Eritrea, Somalia |
| Netherlands | Indonesia (Dutch East Indies) |
| United States | Philippines, Puerto Rico, Hawaii |
| Japan | Korea, Taiwan, Manchuria |

**British India**
- East India Company rule â†’ Crown rule (1858)
- **Indian Rebellion (Sepoy Mutiny) 1857**: Led to direct British rule
- Economic exploitation: De-industrialization, cash crops
- Infrastructure: Railroads, education, English language
- Rising nationalism: Indian National Congress (1885)

**China's Semi-Colonialism**
- Opium Wars (1839-42, 1856-60): Forced to open ports
- Unequal treaties, extraterritoriality
- Spheres of influence divided China
- Taiping Rebellion (1850-64): 20 million died
- Boxer Rebellion (1899-1901): Anti-foreign uprising crushed

**Japan's Response (Meiji Restoration 1868)**
- Ended isolation after Perry's arrival
- Rapid industrialization, modernization
- Western-style military, government
- Became imperialist power (defeated China 1895, Russia 1905)

#### Nationalism

**Unification Movements:**
- **Italy**: Cavour, Garibaldi, Mazzini unified by 1871
- **Germany**: Bismarck's "blood and iron," unified by 1871

**Independence Movements:**
- Greece (1821-32)
- Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania (1800s)
- Latin American nations (1810s-1820s)

**Effects of Nationalism:**
- Created new nation-states
- Increased competition between nations
- Contributed to imperialism
- Ethnic conflicts, minorities persecuted

#### Global Migration (1750-1900)

**Types of Migration:**
- **Free migration**: Europeans to Americas, Australia
- **Indentured servitude**: Indians, Chinese to plantations
- **Forced migration**: Slavery (until abolition)
- **Internal migration**: Rural to urban

**Major Migration Patterns:**
| From | To | Reasons |
|------|-----|---------|
| Europe | Americas, Australia | Economic opportunity, escape persecution |
| China | Southeast Asia, Americas | Gold rush, railroad building |
| India | Caribbean, East Africa | Indentured labor on plantations |
| Japan | Americas | Economic opportunity |

### Unit 7: Global Conflict (c. 1900-present)

#### Key Concepts
- **Total war** mobilized entire societies
- **World wars** reshaped political boundaries
- **New ideologies** (fascism, communism) emerged

#### World War I (1914-1918)

**Long-term Causes (MANIA):**
- **Militarism**: Arms race, especially naval
- **Alliances**: Triple Entente vs. Triple Alliance
- **Nationalism**: Competition, ethnic tensions
- **Imperialism**: Colonial rivalries
- **Assassination**: Franz Ferdinand (immediate cause)

**Key Features:**
- Trench warfare, stalemate on Western Front
- New weapons: Machine guns, poison gas, tanks, airplanes
- Ottoman front, Eastern Front
- Total war: Civilian involvement, propaganda

**Major Outcomes:**
- 17 million dead
- Collapse of empires: Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, German, Russian
- Treaty of Versailles: War guilt, reparations on Germany
- League of Nations created
- Mandate system in Middle East
- Russian Revolution (1917)

#### Russian Revolution (1917)

**February Revolution**: Tsar Nicholas II abdicated

**October Revolution**: Bolsheviks (Lenin) seized power

**Civil War (1918-1921)**: Reds vs. Whites

**Soviet State:**
- War Communism â†’ New Economic Policy
- Stalin's rule (1924-1953): Five-Year Plans, collectivization, purges
- Totalitarian state with command economy

#### Interwar Period (1919-1939)

**Global Depression (1929):**
- US stock market crash
- Global trade collapsed
- Mass unemployment
- Facilitated rise of extremism

**Rise of Fascism:**
- **Italy**: Mussolini (1922)
- **Germany**: Hitler and Nazi Party (1933)
- **Spain**: Franco (after civil war)
- **Japan**: Military government

**Fascist Characteristics:**
- Ultranationalism
- Totalitarian state
- Glorification of war
- Racial superiority ideology
- Opposition to communism and democracy

#### World War II (1939-1945)

**Causes:**
- Treaty of Versailles resentment
- Appeasement of Hitler
- Japanese expansion in Asia
- Nazi aggression in Europe

**Key Events:**
- German invasion of Poland (1939)
- Fall of France (1940)
- Operation Barbarossa - invasion of USSR (1941)
- Pearl Harbor - US enters war (1941)
- Holocaust - 6 million Jews murdered
- D-Day (1944)
- Atomic bombs on Japan (1945)

**Outcomes:**
- 70-85 million dead
- United Nations created
- Division of Europe (Iron Curtain)
- Decolonization accelerated
- US and USSR emerged as superpowers
- Nuremberg Trials

### Unit 8: Cold War and Decolonization (c. 1900-present)

#### Key Concepts
- **Superpower rivalry** shaped global politics
- **Decolonization** created new nations
- **Non-Aligned Movement** offered alternative

#### The Cold War (1947-1991)

**Origins:**
- Ideological conflict: Capitalism vs. Communism
- Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe
- Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan
- Berlin Blockade (1948-49)

**Key Features:**
- Nuclear arms race
- Proxy wars
- Espionage, propaganda
- Space race
- Military alliances: NATO vs. Warsaw Pact

**Major Events:**
| Event | Year | Significance |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Korean War | 1950-53 | Division at 38th parallel |
| Hungarian Uprising | 1956 | Soviet suppression |
| Cuban Missile Crisis | 1962 | Closest to nuclear war |
| Vietnam War | 1955-75 | US defeat, communist victory |
| Prague Spring | 1968 | Soviet suppression |
| Soviet-Afghan War | 1979-89 | Soviet defeat |
| Fall of Berlin Wall | 1989 | End of Cold War symbolized |
| Soviet collapse | 1991 | End of Cold War |

**Cold War in the Third World:**
- Proxy conflicts in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan
- Coups supported by superpowers (Iran, Guatemala, Chile)
- Economic aid as tool of influence

#### Decolonization

**Causes:**
- WWII weakened colonial powers
- Nationalist movements grew
- Cold War competition for allies
- UN support for self-determination

**South Asia:**
- **India** (1947): Gandhi's nonviolent movement
- Partition: India and Pakistan created
- 1-2 million died in religious violence
- Later: Bangladesh (1971)

**Southeast Asia:**
- **Vietnam**: Defeated French (1954), then US (1975)
- **Indonesia** (1949): Independence from Netherlands
- **Philippines** (1946): Independence from US

**Africa:**
- Ghana (1957): First sub-Saharan independence
- Wave of independence in 1960 ("Year of Africa")
- **Algeria** (1962): Violent struggle against France
- **Congo**: Chaos after Belgian withdrawal
- **Kenya**: Mau Mau uprising
- **Angola, Mozambique** (1975): Portuguese withdrawal

**Middle East:**
- **Israel** (1948): Arab-Israeli conflicts
- **Egypt** (1952): Nasser's revolution
- **Iran** (1979): Islamic Revolution
- **Algerian War** of Independence

#### Non-Aligned Movement

- Founded at Bandung Conference (1955)
- Leaders: Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), Tito (Yugoslavia), Sukarno (Indonesia)
- Rejected alignment with US or USSR
- Advocated for Third World interests

#### Post-Colonial Challenges

- Arbitrary borders, ethnic conflicts
- Economic dependency (neocolonialism)
- Cold War intervention
- Authoritarian governments
- Brain drain

## âœï¸ DBQ Strategy Guide

### Document-Based Question Structure

**Time**: 60 minutes (15 reading, 45 writing)
**Points**: 7 total

### Scoring Rubric Breakdown

| Category | Points | Requirements |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Thesis | 1 | Historically defensible claim responding to prompt |
| Contextualization | 1 | Broader historical context (events, developments, processes) |
| Evidence from Documents | 2 | Use 3 docs (1 pt) or 6 docs (2 pts) to support argument |
| Evidence Beyond Documents | 1 | Additional specific historical evidence |
| Sourcing | 1 | Explain significance of POV, purpose, audience, or context for 2+ docs |
| Complexity | 1 | Demonstrate sophisticated understanding |

### DBQ Template

**Paragraph 1: Introduction**
```
[Contextualization - 2-3 sentences on broader historical background]
[Thesis - Clear argument with categories of analysis]
```

**Body Paragraphs (2-3):**
```
[Topic sentence connecting to thesis]
[Document evidence with analysis]
"According to Document X, [quote/paraphrase]. This shows [analysis]..."
[Sourcing for at least 2 documents]
"The author's perspective as a [role] explains why [analysis]..."
[Outside evidence]
[Mini-conclusion connecting to thesis]
```

**Conclusion:**
```
[Restate thesis differently]
[Briefly address complexity - contradiction, change over time, causation]
```

### Sourcing HAPP Analysis

| Element | Questions to Ask |
|---------|-----------------|
| **H**istorical situation | What was happening when this was created? |
| **A**udience | Who was this intended for? |
| **P**urpose | Why was this created? |
| **P**oint of view | How does the author's identity affect this? |

## âœï¸ LEQ Strategy Guide

### Long Essay Question Structure

**Time**: 40 minutes
**Points**: 6 total

### Scoring Rubric Breakdown

| Category | Points | Requirements |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Thesis | 1 | Historically defensible claim responding to prompt |
| Contextualization | 1 | Broader historical context |
| Evidence | 2 | Specific examples supporting argument |
| Analysis and Reasoning | 2 | Historical reasoning (comparison, causation, continuity/change) |

### LEQ Template

**Paragraph 1: Introduction**
```
[Contextualization - 3-4 sentences]
[Thesis with clear argument and preview of evidence]
```

**Body Paragraphs (2-3):**
```
[Topic sentence with clear claim]
[Specific historical evidence - names, dates, events]
[Analysis explaining HOW evidence supports claim]
[Connect back to thesis]
```

**Conclusion:**
```
[Restate thesis with new insight]
[Connect to broader patterns or later developments]
```

### Historical Reasoning Skills

**Comparison:**
- Similarities AND differences
- Explain significance of comparison
- Use comparative language (similarly, in contrast, whereas)

**Causation:**
- Short-term and long-term causes
- Effects and consequences
- Chains of causation

**Continuity and Change Over Time:**
- What changed?
- What stayed the same?
- Why did change occur?
- Significance of continuity

## ğŸ’¡ Test-Taking Tips

### Multiple Choice
1. Read the question stem carefully
2. Eliminate obviously wrong answers
3. Look for qualifier words (always, never, most, least)
4. Consider the time period
5. Watch for "which of the following" questions

### Short Answer
1. Answer all parts of the question
2. Be specific with examples
3. Don't write in full paragraphs
4. Use bullet points if helpful
5. Directly address the prompt

### DBQ
1. Spend 15 minutes reading and planning
2. Create a quick outline
3. Use at least 6 documents
4. Include outside evidence
5. Source at least 2 documents
6. Write a clear thesis

### LEQ
1. Choose the prompt you know best
2. Plan before writing
3. Include specific evidence
4. Show historical reasoning
5. Connect evidence to thesis
