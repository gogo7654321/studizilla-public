### Sound Devices

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Alliteration** | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | "Peter Piper picked a peck" |
| **Assonance** | Repetition of vowel sounds | "The rain in Spain stays mainly" |
| **Consonance** | Repetition of consonant sounds anywhere in words | "pitter-patter" |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words that imitate sounds | "buzz," "crash," "whisper" |
| **Euphony** | Pleasant, harmonious sounds | "cellar door" |
| **Cacophony** | Harsh, discordant sounds | "cracking, crunching, shrieking" |
| **Sibilance** | Repetition of 's' sounds | "slithering snake silently slid" |

### Figurative Language

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Simile** | Comparison using "like" or "as" | "Her eyes were like diamonds" |
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison without like/as | "Life is a journey" |
| **Extended Metaphor** | Metaphor sustained throughout a passage | Dickinson's "Hope is a thing with feathers" |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things | "The wind whispered secrets" |
| **Apostrophe** | Addressing absent person/abstract idea | "O Death, where is thy sting?" |
| **Hyperbole** | Extreme exaggeration | "I've told you a million times" |
| **Understatement** | Deliberately minimizing something | "It's just a scratch" (of a deep wound) |
| **Litotes** | Understatement using double negative | "She's not unattractive" |
| **Metonymy** | Substituting related term | "The Crown" for monarchy |
| **Synecdoche** | Part represents whole (or vice versa) | "All hands on deck" |
| **Oxymoron** | Contradictory terms together | "deafening silence," "bitter sweet" |
| **Paradox** | Seemingly contradictory statement with truth | "Less is more" |

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Anaphora** | Repetition at beginning of successive clauses | "We shall fight... We shall fight..." |

| **Chiasmus** | Reversed parallel structure (ABBA) | "Ask not what your country can do for you..." |
| **Anadiplosis** | End of one clause begins next | "Fear leads to anger, anger leads to hate" |
| **Juxtaposition** | Placing contrasting elements side by side | Light imagery next to dark imagery |
| **Enjambment** | Line break without punctuation pause | Thought continues to next line |
| **Caesura** | Pause within a line of poetry | "To be, or not to beâ€”that is the question" |

- **Characteristics**: Religious themes, allegory, oral tradition, courtly love

- **Key Works**: Shakespearean plays/sonnets, *Paradise Lost*, metaphysical poetry

## ðŸŽ¯ TPCASTT: Poetry Analysis Strategy

### T - Title
**Before reading:**
- What does the title suggest?
- What expectations does it create?
- Note: Return to title after reading for deeper understanding

### P - Paraphrase
**Line by line:**
- Put each stanza/section in your own words
- Identify what literally happens
- Note shifts in speaker or subject

### C - Connotation
**Beyond literal meaning:**
- Identify figurative language
- Analyze imagery and sensory details
- Examine diction choices and their effects
- Note sound devices and their impact

### A - Attitude/Tone
**Speaker's attitude toward:**
- Subject matter
- Audience
- Self
- Use tone words: contemplative, sardonic, reverent, etc.

### S - Shifts
**Look for changes in:**
- Tone (signaled by "but," "yet," "however")
- Speaker or addressee
- Setting or time
- Structure (stanza breaks, punctuation)

### T - Title (Revisited)
**After analysis:**
- How has meaning of title deepened?
- What irony or symbolism appears?
- How does title connect to theme?

### T - Theme
**Universal message:**
- State theme as complete sentence
- Avoid clichÃ©s ("love conquers all")
- Connect to human experience
- Consider multiple possible themes

## ðŸ“– Prose Analysis Strategies

### SOAPSTONE Method

| Letter | Element | Questions to Ask |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| **S** | Speaker | Who is narrating? What's their perspective? Reliable? |
| **O** | Occasion | What is the context? Time period? Circumstances? |
| **A** | Audience | Who is the intended reader? How does this affect style? |
| **P** | Purpose | Why was this written? To persuade? Inform? Entertain? |
| **S** | Subject | What is the main topic? Central conflict? |
| **Tone** | Tone | What is the author's attitude? How is it conveyed? |
| **E** | Evidence | What literary devices support the analysis? |

### Key Elements to Analyze in Prose

**Narrative Perspective:**
- First person (limited, reliable/unreliable)
- Third person limited vs. omniscient
- Second person (rare but significant)
- Effect of chosen perspective

**Characterization:**
- Direct vs. indirect characterization
- Static vs. dynamic characters
- Flat vs. round characters
- Character foils

**Setting:**
- Time and place
- Social/historical context
- Atmosphere created
- Setting as character

**Plot Structure:**
- Exposition, rising action, climax
- Falling action, resolution
- Narrative pacing
- Chronological vs. non-linear

**Style Elements:**
- Sentence length and variety
- Paragraph structure
- Dialogue vs. narration
- Descriptive passages

## âœï¸ Essay Templates for All 3 FRQs

### FRQ 1: Poetry Analysis Essay Template

**Introduction (3-4 sentences):**
```
In [Author]'s poem "[Title]," the speaker [brief description of 
situation/subject]. Through [literary device 1], [literary device 2], 
and [literary device 3], [Author] reveals [theme/meaning]. The poem 
ultimately [makes argument about what poem does/means].
```

**Body Paragraph Template (3 paragraphs):**
```
[Topic sentence identifying device and its effect]. Early in the poem, 
the speaker [quote with context]. This [device] suggests [interpretation]. 
The word choice of "[specific word]" connotes [meaning], while 
"[another word]" implies [meaning]. This technique [connects to thesis]. 
Furthermore, [additional analysis]. Thus, [Author] uses [device] to 
[reinforce theme/create effect].
```

**Conclusion (2-3 sentences):**
```
Through [restate key devices], [Author] crafts a poem that [restate theme 
with new insight]. The [final image/line] leaves readers with [lasting 
impression/question], suggesting [broader significance].
```

### FRQ 2: Prose Fiction Analysis Essay Template

**Introduction (3-4 sentences):**
```
In this excerpt from [Author]'s [novel/short story], the [narrator/
protagonist] [brief situation]. Through [narrative element 1], 
[literary device 2], and [technique 3], [Author] conveys [theme/
characterization/meaning]. This passage reveals [thesis about meaning 
or effect].
```

**Body Paragraph Template (3 paragraphs):**
```
[Topic sentence about technique and effect]. In the [beginning/middle/end] 
of the passage, [Author] writes, "[quote]." This [technique] reveals 
[interpretation]. The [specific word/phrase] "[quote]" particularly 
emphasizes [meaning] because [explanation]. Additionally, the [syntax/
structure/imagery] creates [effect]. Through this technique, [Author] 
suggests [connection to thesis].
```

**Conclusion (2-3 sentences):**
```
By employing [summarize key techniques], [Author] creates a passage 
that [restates central interpretation]. Ultimately, this excerpt 
[makes broader claim about character/theme/meaning].
```

### FRQ 3: Literary Argument (Open Question) Essay Template

**Introduction (4-5 sentences):**
```
[Hook connecting to prompt's concept]. In [Author]'s [full title], 
[brief plot/character context relevant to prompt]. Through [element 1] 
and [element 2], [Author] explores how [restate prompt concept]. 
Ultimately, [Thesis: argument about how the work addresses the prompt, 
stating the meaning of the work as a whole].
```

**Body Paragraph Template (3 paragraphs):**
```
[Topic sentence connecting to thesis]. [Context for evidence]. For 
instance, when [character] [action/quote], [Author] reveals [interpretation]. 
This moment is significant because [analysis]. The [specific technique] 
of [quote/example] further demonstrates [point]. [Author] uses this 
[element] to suggest [connection to thesis and meaning of work].
```

**Conclusion (3-4 sentences):**
```
Through [character/element]'s [journey/development/conflict], [Author] 
illustrates [restate thesis with new language]. [Title] ultimately 
argues that [broader thematic statement]. This exploration of [prompt 
concept] reveals [insight about human nature/society/existence].
```

|-------|----------|

| 2 | Evidence with some commentary, inconsistent |
| 3 | Evidence with mostly effective commentary |
| 4 | Evidence with consistent, sophisticated commentary |

âœ… **Tips for Evidence:**
- Embed quotes smoothly (never stand-alone)
- Choose specific, short quotes
- Explain effect, not just identify device
- Connect all evidence to thesis

**Row C: Sophistication (0-1 points)**
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 0 | Does not meet sophistication criteria |

âœ… **Ways to Earn Sophistication Point:**

- Illuminate meaning through style choices

### Literary Argument Rubric (6 points total)

**Row A: Thesis (0-1 points)**

```

### Complex Thesis Formula

```

```

### Sophistication-Level Thesis Formula
```
While [work] initially appears to [surface interpretation], [author]'s 
complex use of [technique] complicates this reading, suggesting that 
[nuanced interpretation with tension/ambiguity].
```

### Open Question Thesis Examples

**For character-focused prompts:**
```
In [work], [character]'s struggle with [conflict] illuminates [author]'s

```

**For technique-focused prompts:**
```
[Author]'s strategic use of [technique] in [work] functions to [effect], 
ultimately suggesting that [thematic claim].
```

**For theme-focused prompts:**
```
Through [element], [author] explores the tension between [concept A] 
and [concept B], ultimately arguing that [resolution/insight].

| Essay | Suggested Time | Breakdown |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| Poetry (Q1) | 40 minutes | 5 min read/plan, 30 min write, 5 min review |

**Planning Phase (5-7 minutes):**
1. Read prompt carefully (circle key words)

3. Brainstorm literary devices/techniques

5. Draft thesis statement

**Writing Phase (28-30 minutes):**
1. Introduction with thesis (3-5 min)
2. Body paragraph 1 (7-8 min)
3. Body paragraph 2 (7-8 min)
4. Body paragraph 3 (7-8 min)
5. Conclusion (3-5 min)

**Review Phase (5 minutes):**
1. Check thesis addresses prompt
2. Verify evidence is embedded correctly
3. Add transitions if needed
4. Fix obvious errors

## âš ï¸ Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Thesis Mistakes
âŒ **Plot summary instead of analysis**
- Wrong: "In the novel, the character travels to many places."
- Right: "Through the protagonist's physical journey, Author explores the psychological journey toward self-acceptance."

âŒ **Vague or obvious claims**
- Wrong: "The author uses imagery to create effects."
- Right: "The author's dark water imagery reflects the protagonist's descent into moral ambiguity."

âŒ **Listing without argument**
- Wrong: "Author uses simile, metaphor, and personification."
- Right: "Through personifying nature as a nurturing force, Author argues that humanity finds solace in the natural world."

### Evidence Mistakes
âŒ **Dropped quotes**
- Wrong: "The light was green." This shows hope.
- Right: As Nick observes, "the single green light" at the end of Daisy's dock represents Gatsby's unattainable dream.

âŒ **Too much summary**
- Wrong: Retelling the plot of the passage
- Right: Analyzing specific moments with quotes

âŒ **Identifying without analyzing**
- Wrong: "This is an example of metaphor."
- Right: "This metaphor comparing love to a 'red, red rose' emphasizes both passion and fragility."

### Analysis Mistakes
âŒ **"The author uses this to show..."**
- Better: "The author suggests/implies/reveals/argues..."

âŒ **Paraphrasing instead of interpreting**
- Wrong: "The speaker says they are sad."
- Right: "The melancholic tone reveals the speaker's profound sense of loss."

âŒ **Ignoring the "so what?"**
- Always connect analysis to theme and meaning
- Ask: What does this technique accomplish?

### Open Question Mistakes
âŒ **Choosing an unfamiliar work**
- Stick to works you know well
- Have 3-4 versatile works prepared

âŒ **Summarizing plot**
- Focus on analysis, not retelling
- Use plot only as needed for context

âŒ **Ignoring "meaning of the work as a whole"**
- Must connect to larger theme
- End with significance beyond the text

### General Mistakes
âŒ **Running out of time**
- Pace yourself strictly
- A complete essay beats a perfect incomplete one

âŒ **Ignoring the prompt**
- Answer what is asked
- Circle key terms in prompt

âŒ **Generic conclusions**
- Don't introduce new ideas
- Leave reader with insight
