## ğŸ¯ Sub-Theme 1: Understanding Poetic Form

### Fixed Forms vs. Free Verse

```
FIXED FORMS:
Poetry with established patterns of:
- Line length
- Rhyme scheme
- Stanza structure
- Meter

Examples:
- Sonnet (14 lines, specific rhyme)
- Villanelle (19 lines, two refrains)
- Haiku (3 lines, 5-7-5 syllables)
- Limerick (5 lines, AABBA)

FREE VERSE:
Poetry without regular:
- Meter
- Rhyme scheme
- Line length

Still uses:
- Line breaks for emphasis
- Imagery and figurative language
- Sound devices (internal rhyme, alliteration)
```

### Major Sonnet Types

| Type | Rhyme Scheme | Structure | Origin |
|------|--------------|-----------|--------|
| Italian (Petrarchan) | ABBAABBA CDECDE | Octave + Sestet | 14th c. Italy |
| English (Shakespearean) | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG | 3 Quatrains + Couplet | 16th c. England |
| Spenserian | ABAB BCBC CDCD EE | Linked quatrains | Edmund Spenser |
| Miltonic | ABBAABBA + varies | No clear volta | John Milton |

### Other Fixed Forms

```
VILLANELLE (19 lines):
- 5 tercets + 1 quatrain
- Two refrains (lines 1 and 3)
- ABA ABA ABA ABA ABA ABAA
- Famous example: "Do Not Go Gentle 
  into That Good Night" (Dylan Thomas)

SESTINA (39 lines):
- 6 stanzas of 6 lines + 3-line envoi
- No rhyme, but six repeated end-words
- Complex word rotation pattern
- Famous example: Elizabeth Bishop's "Sestina"

PANTOUM:
- Quatrains with lines repeating
- Lines 2 and 4 become lines 1 and 3
- Creates circular, dream-like effect

ODE:
- Formal address to a subject
- Elevated language
- Often irregular stanzas
- Horatian, Pindaric, or Irregular
```

## ğŸµ Sub-Theme 2: Meter and Rhythm

### Understanding Meter

```
METER = Pattern of stressed and 
        unstressed syllables

FOOT = Basic unit of meter
       (combination of stressed/unstressed)

SCANSION = Process of marking meter
           / = stressed (ictus)
           u = unstressed (slack)
```

### Types of Metrical Feet

| Foot | Pattern | Example | Sound Effect |
|------|---------|---------|--------------|
| Iamb | u / | de-LIGHT | Rising, natural speech |
| Trochee | / u | GAR-den | Falling, emphatic |
| Anapest | u u / | in-ter-VENE | Rolling, quick |
| Dactyl | / u u | BEAU-ti-ful | Falling, waltz-like |
| Spondee | / / | HEART-BREAK | Heavy, emphatic |
| Pyrrhic | u u | of the | Light, transitional |

### Line Lengths

| Term | Feet per Line | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| Monometer | 1 foot | "Thus I" |
| Dimeter | 2 feet | "The way a crow" |
| Trimeter | 3 feet | "The only news I know" |
| Tetrameter | 4 feet | "Because I could not stop for Death" |
| Pentameter | 5 feet | Most common in English |
| Hexameter | 6 feet | Alexandrine (French) |
| Heptameter | 7 feet | Common in ballads |

### Scanning Poetry

```
IAMBIC PENTAMETER:
u   /    u   /    u  /   u  /    u    /
Shall I | com-PARE | thee TO | a SUM | mer's DAY?

TROCHAIC TETRAMETER:
 /    u    /   u    /   u    /   u
DOUB-le, | DOUB-le, | TOIL and | TROU-ble

ANAPESTIC TRIMETER:
u    u   /    u  u    /    u   u   /
And the SOUND | of a VOICE | that is STILL

Tips for Scanning:
1. Read aloud naturally
2. Mark obvious stresses
3. Look for patterns
4. Allow for substitutions
5. Check against known meter
```

### Variations and Substitutions

```
Poets vary meter for effect:

SUBSTITUTION:
Replacing expected foot with different one
- Trochee for iamb at line start
- Spondee for emphasis

CATALEXIS:
Dropping final unstressed syllable
"Tiger, tiger, burning bright" (missing final u)

FEMININE ENDING:
Extra unstressed syllable at line end
"To BE or NOT to BE, that IS the QUES-tion"
                              (extra -tion)

CAESURA:
Strong pause within a line
"To be, or not to be: || that is the question"

ENJAMBMENT:
Running sentence across line break
"I think that I shall never see
A poem lovely as a tree"
```

### Meter and Meaning

```
FAST METERS (anapests, dactyls):
- Movement, excitement
- Lightness, speed
- Often in comic or narrative verse

SLOW METERS (spondees, heavy iambs):
- Weight, seriousness
- Grief, contemplation
- Emphasis on content

REGULAR METER:
- Order, control
- Tradition, formality
- Can feel sing-song if too regular

IRREGULAR METER:
- Tension, disruption
- Naturalness
- Emotional turbulence

Example Analysis:
"Break, break, break,
On thy cold gray stones, O Sea!"
               - Tennyson

Three spondees (BREAK, BREAK, BREAK) 
create the pounding rhythm of waves 
and echo the speaker's grief.
```

## ğŸ”Š Sub-Theme 3: Sound Devices

### Types of Sound Devices

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Alliteration | Repeated initial consonants | "Peter Piper picked" |
| Assonance | Repeated vowel sounds | "The rain in Spain" |
| Consonance | Repeated consonant sounds | "pitter patter" |
| Onomatopoeia | Words mimicking sounds | "buzz," "splash" |
| Euphony | Pleasant sounds | "mellow yellow" |
| Cacophony | Harsh sounds | "crashing, crunching" |

### Rhyme Types

```
END RHYME:
Words rhyme at line ends
"The cat sat on the mat,
And wore a funny hat."

INTERNAL RHYME:
Words rhyme within a line
"Once upon a midnight dreary, 
while I pondered, weak and weary"

SLANT RHYME (Near/Half):
Imperfect sound match
"hope/up," "eyes/light," "heaven/given"

EYE RHYME:
Looks like rhyme but isn't
"love/move," "cough/through"

MASCULINE RHYME:
Stress on final syllable
"delight/tonight"

FEMININE RHYME:
Stress before final syllable
"treasure/measure"
```

### Sound and Meaning

```
HARSH SOUNDS (Cacophony):
Hard consonants: k, t, g, d
Short vowels: i, e, a (as in "cat")
Effects: Discord, violence, unease
Example: "And clashed and crashed in 
         the grinding grim of war"

SOFT SOUNDS (Euphony):
Soft consonants: l, m, n, s, f
Long vowels: oh, oo, ah
Effects: Beauty, peace, flowing
Example: "The murmuring of innumerable 
         bees on summer days"

SIBILANCE (s sounds):
Can suggest: Whispers, secrets, snakes,
             smoothness, menace
Example: "The snake slithered silently 
         through the shadowed grass"
```

### Types of Imagery

| Sense | Name | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| Sight | Visual | "crimson sunset" |
| Sound | Auditory | "crackling fire" |
| Touch | Tactile | "rough bark" |
| Taste | Gustatory | "bitter coffee" |
| Smell | Olfactory | "perfumed roses" |
| Movement | Kinesthetic | "spinning dancer" |

### Figurative Language in Poetry

```
SIMILE:
Explicit comparison using "like" or "as"
"My love is like a red, red rose"

METAPHOR:
Implicit comparison
"The road was a ribbon of moonlight"

EXTENDED METAPHOR:
Sustained comparison across multiple lines
The entire "Funeral" conceit in Dickinson

PERSONIFICATION:
Human qualities to non-human
"The wind whispered secrets"

APOSTROPHE:
Direct address to absent/abstract
"O Death, where is thy sting?"

SYNECDOCHE:
Part represents whole
"All hands on deck" (hands = sailors)

METONYMY:
Related term substitutes
"The crown" for "the monarchy"
```

### Symbol and Allegory

```
SYMBOL:
Concrete thing representing abstract idea

CONVENTIONAL SYMBOLS:
- Rose = love, beauty
- Night = death, ignorance
- Light = knowledge, goodness
- Water = purification, life

CONTEXTUAL SYMBOLS:
Meaning derived from the poem itself
(Frost's "woods" in "Stopping by Woods")

ALLEGORY:
Extended narrative where elements
systematically represent abstract ideas
- Characters = virtues/vices
- Journey = life
- All elements have correspondences
```

### Analyzing Imagery

```
CLUSTER ANALYSIS:
1. Identify recurring images
2. Group by type or theme
3. Trace their development
4. Connect to theme/meaning

Example - Keats' "Ode to a Nightingale":
- Darkness/night imagery
- Wine/intoxication imagery
- Death imagery
- Nature imagery

  escaping mortal suffering

CONTRAST ANALYSIS:
- Light vs. dark
- Nature vs. urban
- Past vs. present
```

## ğŸ“ Sub-Theme 5: Structure and Organization

### Stanza Types

| Stanza | Lines | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Couplet | 2 | Often rhymed, epigrammatic |
| Tercet | 3 | Used in villanelles, terza rima |
| Quatrain | 4 | Most common, versatile |
| Quintet | 5 | Less common, limericks |
| Sestet | 6 | Sonnet second part |
| Septet | 7 | Rhyme royal |
| Octave | 8 | Sonnet first part |

### Structural Patterns

```
LINEAR STRUCTURE:
Beginning â†’ Middle â†’ End
- Narrative poems
- Clear progression
- Temporal sequence

CIRCULAR STRUCTURE:
Returns to beginning
- Creates frame
- Emphasizes change/sameness
- "Dover Beach" effect

ASSOCIATIVE STRUCTURE:
Connected by images, ideas
- Stream of consciousness
- Emotional logic
- Free verse often

PARALLEL STRUCTURE:
Repeated syntactical patterns
- Creates rhythm
- Emphasizes comparison
- Whitman's catalogues
```

### Enjambment vs. End-Stop

```
END-STOPPED LINE:
Complete thought at line end
"The curfew tolls the knell of parting day."
Effect: Closure, formality, weight

ENJAMBED LINE:
Thought continues to next line
"I caught this morning morning's minion, king-
dom of daylight's dauphin, dapple-dawn-drawn Falcon..."
Effect: Speed, continuation, emphasis on run-on word

Strategic enjambment creates:
- Suspense (what's coming next?)
- Emphasis (run-on word highlighted)
- Momentum (keeps reader moving)
- Surprise (unexpected completion)
```

### Volta and Shifts

```
VOLTA:
Turn or shift in thought/emotion
- Common in sonnets
- Creates contrast, resolution
- Signal words: but, yet, however, still

Types of Shifts:
- Problem â†’ Solution
- Question â†’ Answer
- Past â†’ Present
- General â†’ Specific
- External â†’ Internal
- Despair â†’ Hope

Finding the Turn:
1. Look for conjunctions
2. Note tense changes
3. Identify perspective shifts
4. Observe imagery changes
5. Notice emotional transitions
```

## ğŸ­ Sub-Theme 6: Voice and Perspective

### Speaker vs. Poet

```
Important distinction:

POET = Real person who wrote the poem

SPEAKER = Voice/persona in the poem
- May or may not be the poet
- Could be a character
- Could be persona adopted

NEVER assume speaker = poet!

Example:
Browning's "My Last Duchess"
- Speaker: Duke (murderous, controlling)
- Poet: Browning (critiquing Duke)
```

### Types of Speakers

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Personal | Uses "I," autobiographical feel | Confessional poetry |
| Persona | Adopted character | Dramatic monologue |
| Collective | "We" voice | Community perspective |
| Impersonal | No clear speaker | Some modernist work |
| Addressee-focused | "You" prominent | Direct address |

### Tone in Poetry

```
Analyzing Tone:

DICTION â†’ TONE
- Formal words = serious, elevated
- Informal words = casual, intimate
- Archaic words = timeless, ceremonial
- Technical words = precise, intellectual

SYNTAX â†’ TONE
- Long sentences = contemplative
- Short sentences = urgent, emphatic
- Questions = uncertainty, engagement
- Imperatives = commanding

IMAGERY â†’ TONE
- Dark imagery = somber, threatening
- Bright imagery = hopeful, cheerful
- Violent imagery = angry, chaotic
- Soft imagery = gentle, peaceful
```

### Tone Vocabulary

```
POSITIVE TONES:
reverent, affectionate, exuberant, 
hopeful, whimsical, playful, 
celebratory, serene, tender

NEGATIVE TONES:
melancholic, bitter, despairing,
caustic, indignant, mournful,
ominous, sardonic, contemptuous

NEUTRAL/COMPLEX TONES:
detached, ironic, reflective,
ambivalent, nostalgic, resigned,
wistful, pensive, philosophical

TONE CAN SHIFT:
Track tonal changes through poem
- Beginning to end
- Before/after volta
- In response to events
```

### Poetry Analysis Framework

```
S.O.A.P.S.Tone + Structure:

SPEAKER: Who is speaking?
OCCASION: What prompted the poem?
AUDIENCE: Who is being addressed?
PURPOSE: Why was this written?
SUBJECT: What is it about?
TONE: What is the attitude?
STRUCTURE: How is it organized?
```

### Close Reading Steps

```
1. FIRST READ:
   - Overall impression
   - Basic meaning
   - Initial reactions

2. SECOND READ:
   - Mark sound devices
   - Note imagery
   - Track speaker/tone

3. THIRD READ:
   - Scan meter
   - Analyze structure
   - Find patterns

4. ANALYSIS:
   - Connect devices to meaning
   - Identify themes
   - Develop interpretation
```

### Strong Poetry Thesis

```
WEAK:
"This poem uses many poetic devices."
"The poet describes nature beautifully."

STRONG:
"Through jarring enjambment and cacophonous 
diction, Hopkins conveys the speaker's 
spiritual anguish at nature's destruction."

"Dickinson's strategic dashes and slant 
rhymes create a sense of instability that 
mirrors the speaker's uncertain relationship 
with death."

Formula:
Through [specific techniques], the speaker/poet
conveys/creates/reveals [specific effect/meaning]
to suggest/illuminate [theme/insight].
```
